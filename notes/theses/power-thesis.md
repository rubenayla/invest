### Money-First Shortlist (what to actually buy, and why)

Models are a filter, not the thesis. If the question is "how do we earn money from this bottleneck without paying peak hype multiples?", the current best risk-adjusted expressions are:

- **EXC (regulated wires)**: most durable path to monetize capex (rate base growth), typically less theme premium and less "one headline ruins you" risk than merchant power.
- **NRG (merchant scarcity)**: higher upside torque if tightness persists (capacity/scarcity/ancillary value), but higher political/regulatory intervention risk; size smaller.

**Sizing default** (if you want exposure now): start a **small sleeve** and scale only if (a) demand keeps showing up in utility capex plans and PPAs, and (b) pricing/regulatory outcomes remain constructive.

## 0. Clarifying the thesis: "energy production shortage" vs. "power bottleneck"

When people say **"energy production shortage"**, the investable reality is usually a **shortage of deliverable, firm power** (especially at peak hours and in constrained regions), not a literal shortage of annual energy volume.

**What’s scarce (investable):**
- **Interconnection rights / queue position** (time-to-power is valuable).
- **Transmission + distribution capacity** (rate-base growth, multi-year build cycles).
- **Grid equipment** (transformers, switchgear, breakers; long lead times).
- **Firm 24/7 generation near load** (nuclear, gas, peakers; capacity value + PPAs).

**What’s less scarce (often commoditized):**
- **Incremental energy** from fast-build renewables (volume grows quickly, margins compete down unless paired with constraints/firming).

**Bottom line**: The thesis is not “buy energy.” It’s **buy the bottlenecks** created by demand growth + permitting + supply-chain + grid build timelines.

## 1. Macro Demand: AI load growth is real, but 10x power is multi-decade

- US net generation in 2024 was 4,308,634,297 MWh (~4.31 PWh). A 10x world implies ~43 PWh, which is a multi-decade shift, not a 2030s outcome.
- IEA projects data center consumption at ~945 TWh by 2030 (about 3% of global electricity demand) and notes data centers drive nearly half of US electricity demand growth.

### The market mechanics that actually pay us

"Demand growth" is not enough. Returns happen when the system prices **deliverability**:

- **Capacity value**: when reserve margins compress, capacity and reliability get priced explicitly (capacity markets and bilateral structures).
- **Congestion / basis**: the same MWh is worth very different dollars depending on whether it can be delivered where load is.
- **Volatility and ancillary services**: tight systems pay for ramping, reserves, and fast response.
- **Time-to-power**: interconnection/transmission delays shift value to anything that can deliver power sooner (existing generation, brownfield upgrades, behind-the-meter).

### Bottleneck valuation math (back-of-envelope, to sanity-check upside)

These are not precise forecasts. They are "is the upside even plausible vs. market cap?" checks.

1. **Merchant scarcity premium (NRG/VST style)**
   - If tightness adds **$25 to $75 per kW-year** of net capacity/ancillary value in a constrained region, then a **5 GW** effective fleet earns **$125M to $375M per year** in incremental gross profit.
   - At a rough **6x to 10x** EBITDA multiple for volatile merchant cash flows, that is **$0.8B to $3.8B** of enterprise value swing.
   - Translation: merchant names are **regime bets**. If the tight-grid regime persists, you can get paid quickly; if policy clamps down or new supply hits faster, multiples and cash flow can compress fast.

2. **Regulated wires rate base (EXC/EIX/PCG style)**
   - If a utility is allowed ~**9% to 10% ROE** on the equity portion of incremental rate base, with ~**50% equity** in the capital structure, then **$5B** of incremental rate base can plausibly translate to roughly **$225M to $250M pre-tax** equity return per year (order-of-magnitude).
   - After tax, call it **$170M to $190M** of net income. At **13x to 17x** earnings, that is **$2.2B to $3.2B** of equity value impact.
   - Translation: wires are a **slow compounding** bet. The "shortage" shows up as multi-year capex plans and allowed returns, not one explosive quarter.

3. **Grid equipment / EPC (ETN/PWR/GEV/HUBB style)**
   - When lead times are long, vendors can hold pricing and margin, but the key risk is **multiple compression** if lead times normalize.
   - The sanity check is: does incremental margin expansion + volume growth plausibly produce a durable **FCF yield** that justifies the multiple? If not, you need a pullback entry to avoid being the liquidity for the theme.

### Grid Bottlenecks

- Interconnection queues are massive: ~1,480 GW generation + ~1,030 GW storage in US queues at end-2023, with only ~13% built.
- Transformer supply is a hard constraint: lead times now average ~120 weeks, indicating record backlogs.
- These delays make speed-to-power valuable and are nudging some data center developers toward behind-the-meter or distributed power solutions.
- Capacity markets can reflect tightness when reserve margins compress (example: PJM’s Base Residual Auction results).

**Implication**: In the near-to-medium term, profits accrue to scarce, firm capacity and to the companies building or servicing the grid.

### Where the “shortage” actually shows up (and how it gets priced)

Electricity is a **deliverability** business. The system can have “enough energy” in annual MWh terms and still have a shortage of **power where/when it’s needed**.

The investable shortage shows up as:
- **Peak + evening scarcity**: solar adds cheap midday energy, but the system still pays up for **firm capacity** and **ramping** during peak/shoulder hours.
- **Congestion / basis**: constrained transmission creates **regional price separation** (high local prices even if the country is fine in aggregate).
- **Time-to-power**: interconnection delays become the gating item; hyperscalers can sign PPAs but can’t energize sites.
- **Reliability premiums**: capacity markets, bilateral PPAs, and “behind-the-meter” contracts start pricing reliability explicitly.

**Key idea**: Most “new” generation is fast-to-build but not always fast-to-deliver (queues + wires) and not always firm (intermittency). That’s why the profit pool concentrates in **wires, equipment, and firm capacity**.

## 2. Technology Mix and Supply Response

- IEA expects renewables and natural gas to lead in meeting data center demand, with nuclear also contributing; SMRs are expected to arrive around 2030.
- For US data centers, IEA estimates electricity supply is currently dominated by natural gas (>40%), followed by renewables (~24%), nuclear (~20%), and coal (~15%).
- Lazard's 2025 LCOE+ report notes utility-scale wind and solar remain the lowest-cost new build, while gas new-build costs are higher due to turbine shortages and longer lead times.

### What this means for "desert solar"

Solar is likely to dominate energy volume because it is the fastest and cheapest to deploy. However, profit tends to be captured by:
- Interconnection rights and transmission capacity
- Firming assets (gas and nuclear)
- Grid equipment and services

### Likely Architecture: Hybrid Microgrids / Behind-the-Meter

Due to multi-year interconnection and transmission timelines, some data center developers are adopting behind-the-meter or distributed power solutions to get online faster. The emerging pattern is a hybrid stack rather than a single technology bet:

- **Primary**: Existing nuclear (if available) or natural gas for firm 24/7 power
- **Augmented**: Onsite solar to reduce average cost and support clean-energy targets
- **Storage**: 4-hour lithium-ion batteries to smooth solar and provide ride-through

This is not universal, but the trend is becoming visible in project announcements and analyst notes focused on speed-to-power.

## 3. Where Profits Likely Concentrate (10x Scenario)

1. **Transmission and distribution (regulated rate base)**: utilities earn allowed returns on expanding the grid.
2. **Firm 24/7 generation near load**: nuclear and gas can sell reliability in constrained regions.
3. **Grid equipment and services**: switchgear, transformers, and EPC services benefit from the buildout, with pricing power during supply bottlenecks.

## 5. Bottleneck Valuation Framework (Beyond the Models)

The models are a **filter**. The valuation work for this theme is understanding which bottleneck you’re buying, what it earns in each scenario, and whether today’s price already discounts the “tight grid” regime.

### 5.1 Bottleneck math (simple, useful back-of-envelope)

**A) Firm clean power (nuclear) “premium”**

If a hyperscaler pays a **premium** for 24/7 clean power:
- 1 GW running at 90% capacity produces ~**7.9 TWh/year**.
- Every **$10/MWh** premium is ~**$79M/year** of incremental revenue on that 1 GW.

That makes it easy to reason about why long-dated PPAs can re-rate firm generators: you’re effectively selling **reliability + time-to-power**.

**B) Regulated wires (rate base)**

For a regulated utility, incremental capex tends to become **rate base**. A crude mental model:
- Incremental earnings roughly track **(Equity portion of capex) × (Allowed ROE)**, with lags and regulatory details.

That’s why “grid build” can be a compounding story when capex plans are credible and the regulator allows recovery.

**C) Equipment and EPC**

Equipment/EPC doesn’t get paid by power prices directly; it gets paid by **capex budgets** and by how constrained supply is:
- When lead times are long, vendors can push **price** and win **mix**.
- When lead times normalize, the main risk is **multiple compression** even if the business stays healthy.

## 9. Final Conclusion

The AI power trade is real, but the biggest profits are likely in bottlenecks rather than raw energy volume. Solar will dominate new energy because it is cheap and fast, but nuclear and gas provide the firming that hyperscalers need. The emerging architecture is hybrid microgrids (gas or nuclear + solar + storage), especially where grid delays are severe. If the system ever approaches 10x power, the most durable profits should accrue to wires, firm capacity, and grid equipment. The basket above captures all three profit pools with staged, disciplined entries.
