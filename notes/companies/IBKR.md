# Interactive Brokers Group, Inc. (IBKR)

**Sector:** Financial Services | **Industry:** Capital Markets
**Price:** $83.06 (2026-05-19) | **Market Cap:** ~$141.2B
**Analysis Date:** 2026-05-19

## Business Primer

**What they do today.** Interactive Brokers Group is a low-cost, technology-driven electronic broker that sells access to global markets. Customers — sophisticated retail traders, professional traders, hedge funds, proprietary trading firms, Registered Investment Advisors (RIAs), and introducing brokers who white-label IBKR's pipes — pay IBKR to execute trades and to borrow against or earn yield on their cash and securities. Revenue comes from three big buckets: net interest income (the biggest, ~55–60% of net revenues — IBKR earns the spread between what it pays customers on idle cash and what it earns lending out that cash, plus margin loans it extends to customers); commissions (~30–35% — per-share/contract fees on stocks, options, futures, foreign exchange, fixed income, crypto, mutual funds across 150+ exchanges in 30+ countries); and other fee income (market data, exposure fees, payment for order flow on US options). The business is unusual for a broker: ~77% pretax margin, run by founder Thomas Peterffy who still controls ~75% of votes via supervoting stock, with proprietary clearing and risk-management software built in-house since the 1970s. They serve 4.75 million accounts holding $789B in client equity (Q1 2026).

**Where they want to grow.** Management's strategic bets are explicit and consistent: (1) keep compounding account growth at 25–35% per year by expanding outside the US — Asia (Korea direct access just launched), Europe, Latin America — where local brokers are expensive and IBKR's commission and FX advantages are largest; (2) deepen the RIA / introducing-broker / institutional channel, which has stickier multi-year revenue than retail traders; (3) build out adjacent products that monetize the same customer base — the ForecastEx CFTC-regulated prediction-markets exchange (event contracts on economic and election outcomes), 24/5 overnight trading on US equities (volumes nearly tripled YoY), expanded crypto and fixed-income coverage, and securities lending; (4) take share from incumbents (Schwab, Fidelity, Robinhood Gold) as those names raise fees or cut customer cash yields. Success in 3-5 years looks like 8-10M accounts, $1.5T+ client equity, and net revenues meaningfully above $10B with margins still north of 70%.

**What could go wrong (business risks).** The biggest structural risk is interest rates — every 25 basis-point Fed cut reduces annual net interest income by roughly $82M, and a full 1% cut is ~$417M off the top, so a fast easing cycle compresses margins faster than account growth can refill them. Second, the customer mix skews toward sophisticated and pro traders whose activity is volatility-dependent — a long calm tape with no tariffs or macro shocks slows DARTs (daily average revenue trades), and options/crypto retail churn during quieter periods. Third, competitor copycats: Schwab and Fidelity have closed much of the commission gap, Robinhood Gold pays 4%+ on cash, and over time the "lowest cost" moat is incrementally eroded — though IBKR's institutional/RIA/global-access offering is harder to replicate than retail commissions. Fourth, regulatory and margin-rule risk: leverage limits, payment-for-order-flow rule changes, prediction-market regulatory pushback (ForecastEx is novel), or capital requirements on the broker-dealer subsidiary could compress economics. Fifth, founder-control optics: Peterffy controls ~75% of votes via supervoting stock, so minority shareholders have limited governance leverage if capital allocation or succession decisions go sideways. Sixth, a tail risk that cuts both ways — a tariff-induced volatility spike helps trading revenue but a true market shock can cause customer defaults on margin loans.

**How to think about it.** IBKR is a low-cost, technology-driven global broker whose earnings power is leveraged to three independent variables — account growth, interest rates, and market volatility — with proprietary clearing and risk tech as the underlying moat. The bet here is on global account expansion plus interest-on-cash compounding, NOT on US-only retail. Mental model: think "global broker infrastructure with a fintech-quality account-growth curve and a quasi-bank's net interest income engine," not "retail trading app." A trader-driven Robinhood comparison undersells the sticky institutional/RIA channel and the 30%-CAGR international account flywheel.

## Situation Summary

IBKR just reported a strong Q1 2026: GAAP EPS $0.59 (adjusted $0.60), net revenues $1.67B, customer accounts +31% YoY to 4.75M, customer equity +37% YoY to $789B, commissions +19%, net interest income +17% to $904M, pretax margin 77%. The board raised the dividend and the share count reflects the 4-for-1 stock split executed June 2025 (so today's $83 price equals ~$332 pre-split). The dominant Wall Street debate is whether the Fed easing cycle compresses net interest margins faster than the account-growth flywheel can compensate — bulls point to 30%+ account CAGR plus ForecastEx, overnight trading, and the SEC's elimination of the Pattern Day Trader rule as durable tailwinds; bears point to $82M per 25bp cut, more aggressive competitor cash yields (Robinhood Gold at 4%+), and a possible volatility lull post-tariff resolution. Consensus is "Buy" with $90 mean target (≈+8.5% from spot), 84% buy share among 17 analysts, very high institutional ownership (89.7%). Stock is near 52-week high ($88.44) — much of the strong Q1 is already in the price.

## Variant Perception

- **Consensus view:** A high-quality compounder with operating leverage, but priced for perfection at 35x trailing P/E; rate cuts cap upside, $90 PT implies single-digit return from here. Most analysts model 12-14% normalized EPS growth and a stable-to-slightly-compressed multiple.
- **Our view:** The market is correctly seeing the rate-cut headwind but is *systematically underweighting* the durability and rate-independence of the account-growth and commission flywheel, particularly the international and RIA channels. With 4.75M accounts growing 31% YoY and a 77% pretax margin, IBKR is one of the few financials that can keep compounding earnings *through* a rate-cut cycle because (a) commission revenue grew 19% on volumes that are not rate-sensitive, (b) customer margin loans and credit balances still grew 17%+ on principal even with lower rates, and (c) the ForecastEx + overnight-trading + PDT-rule optionality is all free in the current valuation. The RIM model in the DB prints -89% (anchored on book value), which is structurally wrong for an asset-light business compounding ROE at 23.6% — typical RIM-on-broker bias.
- **Trigger:** Q2 2026 results (~mid-July 2026) showing continued 25%+ account growth and rate-cut absorption — i.e., NII flat-to-up YoY despite Fed easing — would force the bear camp to re-rate. Continued ForecastEx volume growth and a regulatory green light for prediction markets would add a second leg. Conversely, a sharp NII miss in Q2/Q3 invalidates the "rate-independent" thesis.

## Inflection Point

No classic inflection — this is a "more of the same" compounder rather than a turnaround. The closest thing to an inflection is the *combination* of three currently-free optionalities: (a) ForecastEx prediction-markets adoption (currently de minimis revenue, could be material if institutional and political-event flows scale), (b) overnight 24/5 trading on US equities (volumes nearly tripled YoY but still small share of total), and (c) the SEC's elimination of the Pattern Day Trader rule, which removes a friction on smaller US accounts and could accelerate retail account growth. None of these are individually transformative, but together they raise the plausible terminal multiple and earnings power if they hit.

## Bull Case

- Account growth holds 25%+ for another 3-5 years as IBKR takes share globally (Korea direct access just live, Latin America expanding) — by 2029 accounts could approach 10M, customer equity $1.5T+.
- Rate cuts are slower and shallower than priced (e.g., 50bp total in 2026 vs. 100bp+ expected), so NII compression is modest while commission and margin loan growth more than offsets it.
- ForecastEx becomes a meaningful business as prediction-market regulation crystallizes — institutional adoption for macro/political hedging would be high-margin and rate-independent.
- Continued operating leverage drives pretax margin from 77% toward 80%+, supporting EPS growth in the high teens even with flat-to-down NII per account.
- Re-rating from 35x to 38-40x as the market acknowledges the rate-independent commission and account flywheel.

## Bear Case

- Aggressive Fed easing cycle (150-200bp over 2026-27) takes ~$500-700M out of annual NII faster than commission growth can replace it, breaking the EPS compounding story for 4-6 quarters.
- Competitor cash-yield warfare: Robinhood Gold's 4%+ on cash forces IBKR to pay up to retain client cash balances, compressing the NII spread independently of Fed rates.
- Volatility lull: a calm post-tariff-resolution tape compresses DARTs and options/crypto activity, hitting commission revenue.
- **Underappreciated risk from primary source: management explicitly flagged that ~55-60% of net revenue is net interest income, which means rate-cut sensitivity is structurally higher than commission-focused peers. The headline narrative ("strong Q1, 31% account growth") masks that the *largest* revenue line is the most rate-exposed.** A consensus narrative of "rate cuts are manageable" leans on account growth offsetting NII, but the math requires accounts and commissions to grow ~15-20% just to stand still on NII at -100bp rates — which is achievable but not guaranteed.
- Founder succession / governance risk: Peterffy is 81 — an unexpected succession event introduces multi-quarter overhang regardless of operations.
