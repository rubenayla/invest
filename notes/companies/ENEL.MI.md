# Enel SpA (ENEL.MI)

**Sector:** Utilities | **Industry:** Utilities - Diversified
**Price:** €9.56 (2026-05-19) | **Market Cap:** ~€95B
**Analysis Date:** 2026-05-19

## Business Primer

**What they do today.** Enel is an integrated electricity company, meaning it owns and operates every link of the chain that turns fuel or sunlight into the power coming out of a wall socket. It owns the *generators* — nuclear reactors and hydro dams (mostly in Spain through its Endesa subsidiary), wind farms and solar parks across Europe and Latin America, plus gas and a small remaining coal fleet for backup. It owns the *grids* — the high-voltage transmission lines and, more importantly, the low-voltage distribution wires that physically connect to roughly 70 million end-customers in Italy (E-Distribuzione), Spain, and large parts of Brazil, Chile, Colombia, and Peru. And it owns the *retail business* — the contract that bills the household or factory for the kilowatt-hours they actually consume, plus gas supply and adjacent services like home insurance, boiler maintenance, and Enel X public EV charging. Roughly speaking the EBITDA split is: ~45% regulated networks, ~35% generation + integrated commercial (sell electricity to end customers, hedged against own generation), ~20% Latin America (a separately listed sub in Chile, plus Brazilian distribution). Customers pay them every month on a utility bill; about half of group EBITDA is regulator-set and largely indifferent to the wholesale price of power.

**Why customers choose them (the value proposition).** For the *distribution-grid* business there is no choice — Enel's grids are legal monopolies. If you live in Rome, Milan, Madrid, Barcelona, Rio, or São Paulo, the wires physically attached to your building are Enel's, and an Italian or Spanish regulator (ARERA / CNMC) sets the price the company is allowed to charge to use them. Whether you buy your electricity contract from Enel, Eni Plenitude, or Iberdrola, the *delivery* fee on your bill goes to Enel's grid arm. For the *retail* business — where Italian and Spanish households can now legally switch supplier since liberalization — Enel's pitch versus alternatives is concrete: a single bill for power and gas with one customer service line, dual-fuel discount typically €5–10 per month off versus separate suppliers, automatic enrollment continuity for households exiting the regulated "tutela" tariff (Enel inherits the customer by default unless they actively switch away), an Enel X mobile app that lets the customer see consumption hour-by-hour, a network of ~25,000 Enel X public EV chargers across Italy and Spain that Enel-electricity customers get at preferential per-kWh rates, and bundled add-ons like extended-warranty home appliance repair and rooftop-solar-plus-battery installation financed inside the electricity bill. Switching away saves the typical Italian household maybe €30–60 per year on commodity but loses the bundle and forces dealing with a new app and call center — a small enough number that ~60% of legacy Enel customers in Italy haven't bothered to switch even after the "tutela" market formally closed, which is the inertia Enel monetizes. For the *generation* side, the customer is the wholesale electricity market (or large industrial buyers via long-term PPAs — power purchase agreements), and the value proposition is simple: Enel's hydro and nuclear plants in Spain and renewables fleet have marginal costs near zero, so when wholesale prices spike they earn the spread; what they sell is essentially predictable, low-carbon megawatt-hours at scale.

**Where they want to grow.** Management's 2026–2028 strategic plan calls for ~€53 billion of gross capex over three years, roughly +€10bn versus the prior plan — i.e. the "renewables capex slowdown" narrative is *yesterday's* story; the company has quietly nudged spending back up. About €26bn of that goes to renewables and integrated generation (15 GW of new capacity by 2028, three-quarters in wind and dispatchable storage rather than plain solar, reflecting the lesson that solar-only buildouts cannibalize their own pricing in sunny midday hours). The other ~€26bn goes to power grids, mostly Italian and Spanish low-voltage modernization to handle two-way flows from rooftop solar and EV charging — this is the part that grows the regulated rate base, and therefore the part that mechanically compounds earnings. Geographically the message is "Italy first, Spain close second, Latin America selectively." The aggressive Latam exit under the prior CEO (Starace) — sales in Romania, Peru, Argentina, Russia — is now finished and the remaining LatAm footprint (Brazil distribution, Chilean integrated, Colombian + Peruvian generation) is being run for cash, not expansion. Guidance for 2026: EBITDA €23.1–23.6bn, net income €7.1–7.3bn, EPS €0.72–0.74 (+6% YoY), dividend floor of €0.46/share. Success in three to five years looks like: regulated networks at €10bn+ EBITDA, group net debt/EBITDA back below 2.5×, and an EPS run-rate of ~€0.85.

**What could go wrong (business risks).** Three structural threats. (1) Regulated-return reset risk: the Italian energy regulator ARERA recalculates the allowed return on the distribution rate base every few years, and a lower allowed weighted average cost of capital — say a cut from current 5.9% real pre-tax toward 5.0% — would mechanically shave a few hundred million off network EBITDA, and the market would re-rate the regulated stream lower. The next review window is 2027. (2) Italian political risk on retail margins: every Italian government for the past decade has, at some point, capped or "windfall-taxed" utility margins when household bills get politically loud — Meloni's government did so in 2022–2023 and could again, particularly if 2026–2027 European gas prices spike. (3) Brazilian and Latin currency drag: ~20% of EBITDA is generated in BRL, CLP, COP — currencies that have lost 30–50% versus the euro over the last decade, and continue to depreciate; reported group results are euro, so Latam contribution shrinks every year in translation even when local operations grow. Plus the always-present execution risk on €53bn of three-year capex (cost overruns on grid digitalization, returns on wind/storage projects below assumed levels), and the secular threat that distributed rooftop solar plus home batteries eventually let some households defect partially from the grid, shrinking volume-based revenue — though the regulator typically responds by shifting tariff design toward a fixed connection fee.

**How to think about it.** Enel is a regulated-grid compounder with a renewables overlay and a Latin-America-in-translation-FX-drag attached — closer to a National Grid plus Iberdrola hybrid than to a NextEra-style growth utility. Think bond proxy with embedded EPS growth and ~5–6% dividend yield, not turnaround.

## Situation Summary

Q1 2026 came in roughly in line: revenue down 6.7% YoY to €20.6bn (lower commodity pass-through, not weaker volumes) but ordinary EBITDA up 3.6% YoY to €6.0bn and EPS €0.20, hitting 27% of full-year target — i.e. the regulated-and-integrated mix is doing exactly what management said it would. Growth came from Spain (Endesa retail margins) and Latin America; Italy generation margins compressed as expected. 2026 guidance reaffirmed. The newly issued 2026–2028 strategic plan added ~€10bn of capex versus the prior plan (so the "Enel pulling back from renewables" narrative that dominated 2023–2024 is now stale — they are quietly nudging spending back up, weighted toward wind and storage rather than solar). The stock sits at ~€9.56, near the upper half of its 52-week range, with 22 sell-side analysts mean-rated buy and a target around €10.20. Dividend yield ~5.4%, payout ratio above 100% of trailing EPS but more like ~65% of "ordinary" guided EPS.

## Variant Perception

- **Consensus view:** Steady regulated utility — buy for the dividend, expect 5–7% total return per year, no excitement. Twenty-two analysts at "buy" with a target €10.20 implies the market thinks fair value is roughly €0.60 above spot.
- **Our view:** Consensus is approximately right on direction and magnitude. The interesting variant is that the +€10bn capex bump in the new plan is a regulated-rate-base expansion story that compounds for a decade, not a one-off; combined with finished Latam divestments removing currency-translation drag and Spanish retail outperforming, mid-cycle EPS power is probably closer to €0.85–0.90 by 2028 (consensus implies ~€0.80). That is *some* upside on numbers but most of the gap is multiple — at a 13× forward P/E ratio the stock screens cheap versus US regulated utility comps near 18–20×, but the discount is rational given Italian political risk and EUR/BRL FX. No clear catalyst for the discount to close.
- **Trigger:** The ARERA 2027 regulatory review outcome is the single biggest dateable event; if the new allowed return is unchanged or modestly higher, the stock re-rates to ~€11. If cut materially, the stock de-rates to ~€8.50. Other dates: 2026 full-year results in March 2027, capital markets day usually in November.

## Inflection Point

Modest inflection rather than a sharp one. The Latam-divestment-and-deleverage program that Cattaneo executed since 2023 has now largely completed — debt is down ~€20bn from 2022 peak, the strategic plan is back on offense with capex up, the dividend is rising again. So the company has just transitioned from "fix the balance sheet" mode to "compound the rate base" mode, which is the more attractive of the two phases. Not a high-conviction turnaround but the inflection is real and observable.

## Bull Case
- Regulated rate base in Italy and Spain expands ~€26bn over three years, mechanically compounding EBITDA at 5–7% per year on the network side regardless of macro
- Latam currency headwinds finally stabilizing; Brazilian distribution business throws off cash for buybacks/dividend
- Dividend ratchets from €0.46 floor toward €0.55–0.60 by 2028, attracting yield buyers and supporting a multiple re-rating toward 14–15× P/E
- 2027 ARERA regulatory review preserves current allowed return (~5.9% real pre-tax) or modestly raises it — the most likely outcome given European grid investment is politically encouraged
- Spain (Endesa) retail outperforms thanks to dual-fuel bundling

## Bear Case
- **(Under-covered in news):** ARERA 2027 regulatory review cuts allowed return on Italian distribution rate base; this is a slow-moving, technical event but it is the single largest *quantified* downside management acknowledges in capital-markets-day disclosures and gets almost zero mainstream news coverage relative to its earnings impact
- Italian government imposes another windfall tax on utility margins if 2026–2027 winter gas prices spike politically
- BRL/CLP/COP continue depreciating versus EUR, translating Latam EBITDA growth into euro stagnation
- Capex execution on €53bn plan slips (typical utility cost overruns 10–20%), pushing net debt back above 3.5× EBITDA and forcing dividend reset
- Distributed rooftop solar + home battery adoption accelerates in Italy, eroding retail volume base over a decade

## Verdict

**WATCH** — Conviction: MEDIUM

Enel offers a clean, regulated-rate-base compounder with ~5.4% dividend yield, finished deleveraging, and modest scope for EBITDA growth from a €53bn three-year capex plan — but at €9.56 versus a fair value cluster of €11–12, the upside is roughly 15–25% on price plus dividends, not the asymmetric reward needed for a high-conviction utility position. The bigger constraint is the lack of a near-term dateable catalyst: the ARERA 2027 review is the meaningful re-rating event and it is 12+ months out. Better as a yield holding than a capital-appreciation idea, and arguably a less attractive entry than during the 2023 debt-fear lows around €5–6.

**If BUY:** Entry at €8.80 or below would shift the risk/reward materially; full-position thesis-break at €7.50.
**If WATCH:** Would upgrade to BUY on a) pullback to €8.50–8.80 on macro noise, or b) early signal that the 2027 ARERA review will hold the allowed return steady (typically signaled in late-2026 consultation drafts).
