## Situation Summary
Bunge is a thin-margin, high-volume agricultural intermediary: it originates grains/oilseeds from farmers, **crushes** soybeans and softseeds into meal (animal feed) and oil (food + biofuel feedstock), refines specialty oils, and merchandises/ships commodities globally. Revenue is enormous (~$70B) but net margin is ~1% — earnings are driven by **crush spreads** and **merchandising margins**, both of which swing with harvests, trade flows, and biofuel policy. This is a cyclical, not a staple, despite the "Consumer Defensive" label.

The defining 2025 event: Bunge closed its **merger with Viterra** (the Glencore-backed grain handler) on 2 July 2025 — roughly $8B stock + ~$2B cash + ~$9.8B assumed debt. It roughly doubles Bunge's origination/handling footprint (especially Argentina, North America, Brazil, plus wheat/grain) and is the source of the leap in EV and net debt. Management targets **~$250M+ run-rate synergies**, with capture accelerating through 2026.

On the screen this reads as deep-cyclical value:
- **~12.6x forward EPS** on the *raised* FY2026 adjusted guidance midpoint (~$9.25); yfinance's 10.7x forward / $10.89 EPS appears to be an out-year (2027, fuller synergies) analyst figure above the company's own number.
- **~1.40x price/book**, **~0.28x price/sales** (P/S is meaningless here — it's a pass-through commodity book).
- **2.41% dividend** ($2.80/yr). The 74% payout shown on screen is on *depressed trailing GAAP* earnings; against ~$9.25 adjusted EPS the payout is ~30% — comfortably covered.
- Analyst mean target **~$142 (+22%)**, "buy" consensus (9 analysts).

The catch — why it's cyclically risky right now:
- **GAAP net income has been falling all year**: $354M (Q2'25) → $166M → $95M → **$68M (Q1'26)**. That GAAP line is heavily distorted by mark-to-market on commodity hedges and Viterra acquisition/integration costs (Bunge guides on *adjusted* EPS for this reason), but the gap between $1.83 adjusted and ~$0.35 GAAP in Q1'26 is large and recurring — a real **earnings-quality** caveat, not pure noise.
- **Record Brazilian soybean crop (~180M tonnes)** plus Argentina's export shifts are creating a global oilseed **oversupply that compresses export and crush margins** — the very spread Bunge earns on. The guidance raise rests on forward curves and synergy capture *offsetting* this, not on a margin tailwind.
- **~$14.6B net debt** post-Viterra on a 1%-margin business: small swings in crush spreads move EPS a lot, and the leverage amplifies both directions.

## Variant Perception
- **Consensus view:** "Screen-cheap (~10–12x) ABCD trader that just beat and raised — Viterra makes it a scaled grain major with $250M+ synergies and a clear path back to ~$9–11 normalized EPS. Buy the cycle trough at 1.4x book."
- **Our view (partial pushback):** The beat-and-raise is real and the Viterra scale is strategically sound, but you're being asked to pay a mid-range price (~12.6x guided) for a **commodity processor heading into a soybean oversupply**, not out of one. Crush margins are a spread, and record South-American supply pushes that spread the wrong way; the raised guidance leans on synergy capture and favorable forward curves to *hold the line*, not expand it. With ~$14.6B of net debt and ~1% margins, the downside in a margin-compression scenario is steep. This is a "buy cheap, sell dear" cyclical — and $116, +62% off the low, isn't cheap enough.
- **Why it is NOT an obvious PASS either:** Management actually *raised* into the oversupply headwind (FY26 adjusted EPS $9.00–9.50, up from $7.50–8.00) on +63% YoY segment EBIT, Viterra volumes are flowing, the dividend is well-covered on adjusted earnings, and at 1.4x book with a low 0.62 beta you're not paying a quality premium. If crush spreads merely normalize and synergies land, $9–11 EPS at 12–13x supports $120–140.
- **Trigger / what re-rates it:** Evidence the soybean glut is clearing (tighter crush spreads, stronger biofuel/renewable-diesel feedstock demand on firmer US policy); two to three quarters of synergy capture showing in *adjusted* segment EBIT; deleveraging the Viterra debt. Conversely, a deepening oilseed surplus or a biofuel-policy setback breaks it.
