# Vistra Corp (VST)

**Sector:** Utilities | **Industry:** Independent Power Producers
**Price:** $159.99 (2026-05-28) | **Market Cap:** $53.9B | **EV:** $75.8B
**Analysis Date:** 2026-05-28

## Business Primer

**What they do today.** Vistra is the largest competitive (non-regulated) electricity generator in the United States. It owns roughly 41 gigawatts of power plants — a fleet of nuclear reactors (~6.4 GW), natural-gas plants, coal plants, solar farms and grid-scale batteries — and it sells the electricity those plants produce into wholesale power markets, chiefly Texas (ERCOT) and the Mid-Atlantic/Midwest (PJM). It also runs a large retail electricity business (TXU Energy and others) that sells power directly to ~5 million homes and businesses. So Vistra makes money two ways: (1) the *Generation* business produces electrons and sells them at the prevailing market price, and (2) the *Retail* business buys/produces power and resells it to end customers at a markup. In Q1 2026, of $1.49B segment adjusted EBITDA, the East region (PJM) contributed $801M, Texas $586M, West $56M, and Retail $68M — generation dominates the earnings.

**Why customers choose them (the value proposition).** Electricity is a commodity — a megawatt-hour from Vistra is identical to one from a competitor — so Vistra does not win on product differentiation the way a software company does. It wins on two things. First, *cost and dispatchability*: its nuclear plants run 24/7 at near-zero marginal fuel cost, so when wholesale prices spike (hot Texas summer, cold snap, grid stress), Vistra's already-running nuclear and its fast-starting gas plants capture those high prices while their costs barely move. Second, and newer, *being the counterparty a data center can actually sign with*. An AI hyperscaler (Meta, Amazon) that wants to power a multi-hundred-megawatt data center needs firm, carbon-free, 24/7 power delivered for 20 years from a creditworthy supplier — and there are only a handful of companies in the country that own enough existing nuclear capacity to promise that. A new data center can't build its own reactor (takes a decade and NRC approval); buying contracted output from Vistra's existing Comanche Peak or PJM reactors is the fastest path to firm clean power. That scarcity — existing nuclear is effectively un-buildable today — is the real reason hyperscalers come to Vistra rather than the next-best alternative.

**Where they want to grow.** Note the pattern: Vistra grows mostly by *acquiring* and *uprating* existing plants and building *gas* — it is deliberately NOT building new nuclear (new reactors take ~10 years + NRC approval, which is the whole scarcity thesis). Four explicit bets. (1) *Contract the existing nuclear fleet to hyperscalers* — ~3,800 MW of 20-year power purchase agreements: up to 1,200 MW to Amazon Web Services at Comanche Peak (Texas/ERCOT), and 2,609 MW to Meta from PJM nuclear (1,268 MW at Perry, 908 MW at Davis-Besse, plus uprate energy/capacity from multiple sites). This converts merchant (price-risk) nuclear revenue into contracted, predictable cash flow. (2) *Acquire dispatchable gas* via the ~$4.7B Cogentrix deal (~5,500 MW of modern gas at ~$730/kW, ~$2.3B cash + ~$1.5B assumed debt + 5M shares; 2H-2026 close) plus the late-2025 Lotus acquisition. (3) *Build new gas in Texas* — two new units totaling **860 MW under construction at the Permian Basin plant** (tripling that site 325→1,185 MW), part of a multi-year plan to add >2,000 MW of ERCOT capacity 2024-2028, backed by ~$1B of grid-reliability capex; plus the ~200 MW Oak Hill solar project. (4) *Squeeze more from existing assets* — ~400-500 MW of uprates across the Texas gas fleet and nuclear uprates at Comanche Peak/PJM, capacity added with no new plant construction. Success in 3-5 years looks like $8B+ adjusted EBITDA with a meaningfully larger share of it under long-term contract.

**What could go wrong (business risks).** (1) *AI/data-center demand disappoints* — if hyperscaler capex slows or model-efficiency gains (a "DeepSeek" shock) cut power-per-query, the structural demand story that underpins the re-rating weakens. (2) *Regulation reshapes the economics* — FERC rulings on data-center co-location, PJM's Reliability-Backstop and capacity-market reforms, or ERCOT market-design changes could cap how much of the upside Vistra keeps. (3) *Merchant price reversion* — most generation is still sold at market prices; a mild-weather year or a glut of new gas/renewable/battery supply compresses spark spreads. (4) *Integration/leverage* — digesting Cogentrix and Lotus while carrying ~$19B net debt leaves less room for error. (5) *Nuclear operational risk* — an unplanned reactor outage removes the highest-margin, contracted asset.

**How to think about it.** Vistra is an *infrastructure/commodity play riding an AI-demand tailwind*, not a software compounder. The category label is "picks-and-shovels AI power" — but the substance is that it owns a scarce, hard-to-replicate fleet of always-on power plants at the exact moment electricity demand is inflecting upward for the first time in two decades, and it is locking a slice of that fleet into 20-year contracts with the deepest-pocketed buyers on earth.

## Situation Summary

Vistra reported a strong Q1 2026 on May 1: Ongoing Operations Adjusted EBITDA of $1.494B (up ~20% YoY, ~85% vs Q1 2024) and a swing to positive GAAP net income of $1.029B (vs a $268M loss in Q1 2025), driven by higher realized energy/capacity prices in PJM, the Lotus acquisition's first full quarter, and a $723M unrealized hedging gain. Management *reaffirmed* 2026 guidance (Adjusted EBITDA $6.8–7.6B; FCFbG $3.925–4.725B, both excluding Cogentrix and the Meta PPA) and held the 2027 midpoint opportunity at $7.4–7.8B. A second major rating agency upgraded Vistra to investment grade. Yet the stock sits at ~$160, still ~27% below its $220 late-2025 high and below its 200-day moving average (~$174) — the post-Q4-miss de-rating has not reversed despite the operational beat. Analyst consensus remains Strong Buy with a ~$225 mean target.

## Variant Perception

- **Consensus view:** Analysts are already bullish (Strong Buy, ~$225 mean target, +40% implied), so this is a *consensus long* on the desk-research level. But the *price* tells a different story: the stock has de-rated ~27% from its high and trades at ~14.6x forward earnings / ~10.5x EV/EBITDA on FY26 guidance — the market is pricing in skepticism that the AI-demand EBITDA ramp is real and durable, and is discounting Cogentrix leverage and PJM/ERCOT regulatory overhang.
- **Our view:** The gap between the operational reality and the price has *widened* in Vistra's favor since the prior analysis. Three things de-risked the thesis in Q1: (1) the EBITDA ramp showed up in actual numbers (+20% YoY), not just guidance; (2) net leverage is *improving* toward ~2.7x net-debt/EBITDA on FY26 guidance (the prior "rising leverage" worry inverted — IG upgrade confirms it); (3) management is conspicuously *conservative*, repeatedly stating its own ERCOT load-growth forecast (5-6%/yr, 10-15 GW of data-center load by 2030) sits *below* third-party forecasts — a credibility signal, not hype. The trailing P/E that the market fixated on has already corrected from ~75x to ~27x as net income normalized, removing the headline "expensive" objection. Meanwhile Cogentrix and the Meta PPA are *excluded* from current guidance — they are unpriced optionality on top of a $7B+ base.
- **Trigger:** (1) Cogentrix close in 2H 2026 followed by upward guidance revision incorporating its EBITDA + the Meta PPA; (2) continued quarterly beats demonstrating the ramp is structural; (3) FERC/PJM regulatory clarity on co-location removing an overhang; (4) any new hyperscaler PPA on the remaining uncontracted nuclear/gas capacity.

## Inflection Point

**Yes — and the inflection is now confirmed in actuals, not just guidance.** The prior note called the demand-driven inflection (hyperscaler nuclear PPAs converting merchant nuclear to contracted revenue amid structurally rising US power demand). Q1 2026 provided the observable evidence the protocol asks for: EBITDA up 20% YoY, a swing to positive GAAP income, guidance reaffirmed, and a second IG credit upgrade confirming the deleveraging. The inflection has clearly begun; the next leg (Cogentrix close + Meta PPA contribution) is excluded from current guidance and represents priced-out upside.

## Bull Case

- 2026 EBITDA lands at the high end ($7.6B), then Cogentrix + Meta PPA layer on in 2027, pushing EBITDA toward $8B+ — neither is in current guidance.
- Cogentrix closes accretively in 2H 2026; management revises guidance upward and the market re-rates the now-more-contracted earnings base.
- New hyperscaler PPAs on remaining uncontracted nuclear/gas capacity (Google/Microsoft/OpenAI-class buyers); each adds contracted, de-risked cash flow.
- ERCOT load growth comes in at or above management's deliberately-conservative 5-6%/yr; spark spreads stay elevated.
- Continued buyback at a depressed multiple compounds per-share value (count already -30% since 2021).

## Bear Case

- AI/data-center capex slows or efficiency gains cut power-per-query, undermining the secular demand thesis (the "DeepSeek" scenario).
- **PJM/ERCOT regulatory drag (under-covered in headlines):** management and analysts flagged on the call that the PJM Reliability-Backstop Procurement and Connect-and-Manage rulemakings, plus FERC co-location rules, are *delayed and unresolved*, creating "negotiation complexity" that could cap how much PPA upside Vistra keeps. The news narrative leads with the bullish PPA wins, not this regulatory friction. Management also called battery returns in ERCOT "debatable" absent long-term contracts.
- Merchant price reversion in a mild-weather year compresses the still-large unhedged/merchant portion of generation.
- Cogentrix or Lotus integration stumbles, or leverage rises faster than EBITDA, stalling the deleveraging.
- Unplanned nuclear outage removes the highest-margin, now-contracted asset.
