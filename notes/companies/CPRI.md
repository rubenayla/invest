# CPRI — Capri Holdings Limited

**Price:** $19.13 (2026-06-24)
**Rating:** WATCH (lean PASS) — beaten-down deep-value optionality on a clean post-Versace balance
sheet, but the core (Michael Kors) is still in a -15%/yr secular slide and "cheap" only holds if you
trust an optimistic margin-recovery consensus. Falling knife. Want a sub-$15 entry, or wait for a
revenue-stabilization quarter before paying up.
**First look:** 2026-06-24

Capri Holdings is what's left of the accessible-luxury roll-up that John Idol built: **Michael Kors**
(~80% of remaining revenue — handbags, accessories, watches, ready-to-wear) and **Jimmy Choo**
(luxury footwear). The third leg, **Versace**, was **sold to Prada for $1.375B in cash, closing
December 2, 2025**, with proceeds used to repay the majority of group debt. The stock is the wreckage
of a failed deal: Tapestry agreed to buy Capri for **$57/share** in Aug 2023, the FTC sued to block it
on accessible-handbag concentration, and a federal court enjoined the merger in Oct 2024. Capri
collapsed from ~$50 to ~$19, and now trades at **$19.13** — near the bottom of its 52-week range
($16.72–$28.27), a market cap of ~$2.2B on 114.8M shares.

## Why it's on the radar
- Down ~66% from the $57 deal price and ~32% from the 52-week high; ~14% off the 52-week low. A genuine
  multi-year washout, not a dip.
- **Versace sale de-levered the balance sheet**: total debt (incl. leases) fell from $3.58B (FY24) to
  $2.55B (FY25) to **$1.42B (FY26)** as proceeds were applied. Net debt now ~$1.28B — RemainCo is far
  cleaner and a much easier acquisition target than the pre-spin conglomerate.
- Forward P/E ~7.4 on consensus FY27 EPS of **$2.60**; analyst mean target **$25.72** (+34%), 16
  analysts, "buy". Short interest ~9.4% of float — a squeeze/takeout setup if sentiment turns.
- The "Coach playbook" precedent: Tapestry spent a decade turning a fatigued accessible-luxury handbag
  brand (Coach) back into a 25%+ operating-margin machine. The bull thesis is that Michael Kors is
  early in the same arc.

## The bear case (lead with it — this is the crux)
1. **Michael Kors is in an active secular decline, not a trough.** Group revenue has fallen every year:
   FY23 $5.62B → FY24 $4.14B → FY25 $3.62B → FY26 $3.47B (the FY26 figure still includes ~9 months of
   Versace). Michael Kors revenue fell **-15.6%** in the most recent quarter to $694M. This is the
   opposite of stabilization — the brand is shrinking *now*, and handbag fatigue / accessible-luxury
   over-distribution is a structural problem, not a one-quarter air pocket.
2. **Operating margin has collapsed and "cheap on forward EPS" depends on a big recovery.** FY26
   operating income was just **$78M on $3.47B revenue (2.2% margin)**, vs the ~20%+ Michael Kors earned
   in its heyday. Build RemainCo bottom-up: MK+JC revenue ~$2.6–2.8B; at a *recovered* 10% op margin
   that's ~$270M EBIT, less ~$60–70M interest and ~20% tax ≈ **$1.50–1.60 normalized EPS** — not the
   $2.60 consensus. To hit $2.60 you need ~13–15% operating margins, i.e. most of the way back to
   former-glory profitability on a still-declining top line. At my more conservative $1.50–1.80
   normalized EPS, the stock is **11–13x**, not 7x — fairly valued to slightly expensive for a no-growth
   business.
3. **Book value is meaningless and equity is nearly wiped.** Stockholders' equity is **$80M** (down from
   $1.85B in FY23) after $1.4B+ of goodwill/intangible write-downs; P/B is ~27x. There is no asset-value
   floor here — this is a pure earnings-power/brand-equity call.
4. **The takeout optionality is real but not free.** Tapestry — the natural buyer — is legally barred.
   A PE buyer or a different strategic could bid, but the FTC has signaled it views accessible-luxury
   handbags as a concentrated market, which chills strategic interest. Don't underwrite a bid; treat it
   as a free option.

## The bull case (what would have to go right)
- **Post-Versace, Capri is a focused, nearly net-debt-free turnaround.** Interest expense drops sharply
  (~$1.1B of debt repaid ≈ $45–55M/yr after-tax interest saved ≈ ~$0.40 EPS tailwind), and management
  attention narrows to one brand to fix.
- **Michael Kors is structurally a high-margin model when healthy.** The current ~2% group margin is
  cyclically/transitionally depressed by markdowns, soft wholesale, weak China, and restructuring. A
  recovery to even 12–15% — well below the historical 18–24% — gets EPS to $2.00–2.60 and a re-rate to
  10–12x lands the stock in the high-$20s to mid-$30s.
- **Capital return + buybacks on a tiny share count.** With the balance sheet repaired, FCF can fund
  repurchases that shrink 115M shares meaningfully at a $2.2B cap.
- **Takeout / squeeze tail.** Beaten-down, clean balance sheet, 9.4% short interest, recognizable
  brands — the kind of setup where a single bid or a stabilization print moves the stock 30–50% fast.

## Valuation — my own build (classic/GBM models run DB-wide, not per-ticker; done by hand)
The whole call hinges on normalized Michael Kors + Jimmy Choo earnings power. I deliberately use a
more conservative normalized EPS (~$1.80) than the $2.60 sell-side consensus, because revenue is still
falling 15%/yr and the margin recovery has not begun to show up. Horizon ~2–3 years.

| Scenario | Assumptions | Normalized EPS | Multiple | Target | Return |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Bear (35%)** | MK decline persists, margins stuck 4–6%, no bid | ~$1.10 | 11x | **$12.50** | **−35%** |
| **Base (45%)** | Decline moderates to mid-single-digits, margins recover to ~10%, partial re-rate | ~$2.00 | 11x | **$22** | **+15%** |
| **Bull (20%)** | Real turnaround to 13–15% margins OR a takeout bid | ~$3.00 | 11–12x / deal | **$34** | **+78%** |

**Probability-weighted EV ≈ +10%** over 2–3 years (0.35·−35 + 0.45·+15 + 0.20·+78). Positive skew, but
a thin reward for the volatility, and the downside is a genuine value trap rather than a temporary dip.
On the calibrated base rate (73% of stocks up 1y) this is *below*-average attractiveness on a
risk-adjusted basis — the dispersion is the problem, not the midpoint.

## Rating rationale
**WATCH (lean PASS).** The setup is tempting — washed-out price, clean post-Versace balance sheet,
recognizable brands, takeout/squeeze optionality — but the central fact is that Michael Kors is still
*shrinking 15% a year*, and the 7x "forward P/E" rests on a margin recovery that hasn't started. On
conservative normalized earnings the stock is fairly valued near $19, not a bargain. This is a
high-variance falling knife, not a fresh actionable mispricing.

- **Entry interest:** sub-$15 (margin of safety on a no-growth, declining core), **or** any price after a
  quarter that shows Michael Kors revenue *stabilizing* — that single data point flips the thesis from
  "catching a knife" to "buying a confirmed trough" and is worth paying up for.
- **Thesis-break (avoid / would turn PASS):** Michael Kors comps stay down double-digits for another
  2–3 quarters with no margin improvement, or net debt creeps back up (buying back stock with borrowed
  money before the brand stabilizes).
- **What would change my mind to BUY:** a stabilization/positive-comp quarter at Michael Kors, a credible
  margin-recovery trajectory (group op margin back above ~8%), or a concrete acquisition approach.
