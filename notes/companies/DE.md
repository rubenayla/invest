# Deere & Company (DE)

## Business Primer

**What they do today.** Deere builds and sells the big machines that grow food, build roads, and harvest forests — green-and-yellow tractors and combines being the famous ones. Revenue splits into three equipment segments plus a captive bank. (1) *Production & Precision Agriculture* (the largest, ~40%+ of equipment sales) — high-horsepower row-crop tractors, combines, planters and sprayers sold to large commercial farmers in the US Corn Belt, Brazil, and Europe. (2) *Small Agriculture & Turf* — smaller utility tractors, hay/forage equipment, riding mowers and compact machines sold to dairy/livestock farms, landscapers, and homeowners. (3) *Construction & Forestry* — excavators, loaders, dozers, and logging machines, plus the Wirtgen road-building line. The fourth piece, *John Deere Financial*, is an in-house lender that finances dealers' inventory and farmers' equipment purchases, earning interest spread (FY26 guided ~$840M net income). A combine can cost $600K-$1M; a fleet of large-ag iron easily runs into seven figures, so financing is integral to selling the metal.

**Why customers choose them (the value proposition).** A farmer has a roughly two-week window each spring to plant and each fall to harvest; a machine breakdown during that window can cost an entire season's income. Deere wins on two concrete things competitors (CNH/Case-New Holland, AGCO, Kubota) struggle to match. First, the *dealer network and uptime*: Deere's dealers are denser and better-stocked with parts, so a broken machine gets a part or a loaner faster — measured in hours, not days, during harvest. Second, *precision agriculture technology baked into the machine*: GPS auto-steer that drives the tractor to centimeter accuracy so rows don't overlap (saving seed and fuel), See & Spray cameras that identify weeds and spray herbicide only on the weed (cutting chemical use up to ~60%), and harvest automation where the combine adjusts its own settings — 99% of combines in the latest Early Order Program took some automation, ~80% took the top package. This data flows to the John Deere Operations Center software, which now manages ~500 million "engaged acres." A farmer who has years of field data and equipment locked into Deere's software faces real switching cost to move to a rival — the next-best tractor is cheaper up front but doesn't talk to the rest of his fleet or his agronomy data.

**Where they want to grow.** Management's headline bet is shifting from selling a machine once to earning *recurring software/data revenue per acre* — the "Solutions as a Service" model — targeting roughly 10% of total revenue from recurring streams by 2030. They are pushing precision-ag adoption deeper (autonomy, See & Spray retrofit kits for older machines, the autonomous tractor), expanding in Brazil and India, building out the Construction & Forestry digital toolset (the Tenna fleet-tracking acquisition, proprietary excavator models replacing a sourcing partnership), and re-shoring some production to blunt tariffs. Success in 3-5 years looks like: through-cycle margins structurally higher than the last cycle, a meaningful recurring-revenue line that smooths the equipment cycle, and engaged acres compounding past 500M.

**What could go wrong (business risks).** The core risk is the *commodity/farm-income cycle* — when corn and soybean prices fall, farmers stop buying $700K machines and Deere's volumes can drop 30-40% peak-to-trough (FY23 net income ~$10.2B → FY26 guided $4.5-5.0B shows the swing). Tariffs are a live cost shock: management quantified ~$1.2 billion of pretax tariff burden for FY2026. Competitive threats include AGCO/CNH undercutting on price and newer entrants in autonomy/ag-tech (including startups and the equipment-agnostic software players). Execution risk on the recurring-revenue pivot is real — farmers may resist paying subscriptions. And dependence on US farm policy (the $12B Farmer Bridge Assistance Program and government payments are currently propping up farm balance sheets) means a policy reversal removes a demand crutch.

**How to think about it.** Deere is the dominant franchise in agricultural equipment — a cyclical industrial with a genuine razor-and-blades layer (machines = razor, parts + precision-ag software/data = blades) — currently sitting near the trough of a farm-income downturn. The investment question is whether the *structural* improvements (precision-ag attach rates, recurring revenue, a lower cost base) make the next cycle's peak and trough both higher than the last, versus the bear view that it is simply an expensive cyclical at a low point in the cycle.

## Situation Summary

Deere is at the self-declared bottom of an agricultural down-cycle. In Q1 FY2026 (quarter ended Feb 1, 2026, reported Feb 19) net income fell to $656M ($2.42/sh) from $869M ($3.19/sh) a year earlier, yet beat consensus by ~20% and worldwide net sales/revenues rose 13% to $9.61B as Construction & Forestry (+34% sales) and Small Ag & Turf (+24%) rebounded while Production & Precision Ag operating margin collapsed to 4.4% (from 11.0%) on tariffs, mix, and warranty. Management reaffirmed FY2026 net income guidance of $4.5-5.0B and stated explicitly that "2026 marks the bottom of the current cycle," pointing to dealer new inventory down ~40% YoY, used large-tractor inventory falling, China resuming soybean purchases, and government farm-support payments. The market narrative is "Moderate Buy, expensive cyclical" — analysts average ~$665 target on ~$555 price — with the next datapoint imminent: Q2 FY2026 prints May 21, 2026 (tomorrow), where Street expects ~$5.74 EPS.

## Variant Perception

- **Consensus view:** Quality cyclical at the trough; "Moderate Buy" with ~$665 mean target (~20% upside) but a high trailing PE (~31x) and ~24x forward earnings make many call it fairly-to-richly valued for a machinery name. The market broadly accepts the "2026 = bottom" framing but is cautious that recovery is slow and tariff/farm-income risk lingers.
- **Our view:** The variant is *modest, not dramatic*. Where we differ from the cautious cohort: the trough is being under-credited for two structural reasons the headline PE obscures. (1) Trailing PE on trough earnings overstates expensiveness — on normalized mid-cycle earnings (~$24-28/sh power) the stock is ~20-23x, not 31x. (2) The precision-ag/recurring-revenue layer (500M engaged acres, +25% highly-engaged growth, ~80% top-package automation attach) is structurally raising the margin floor and softening the cyclical trough — Q1 already showed production-cost favorability ex-tariffs. The inventory destocking (new field inventory -40% YoY, used 8R down >40% from peak) means the next up-leg in shipments can outpace the slow retail recovery. We do NOT have a strong contrarian edge on direction — this is a "buy the trough of a great franchise" thesis, not a hidden mispricing.
- **Trigger:** Q2 FY2026 print (May 21, 2026) and the FY2027 order book / Early Order Program signals through mid-2026; confirmation that large-ag retail demand has stopped falling (industry guided -15-20% in FY26) and order velocity is building into FY27 would force a re-rate from trough-multiple to recovery-multiple.

## Inflection Point

Yes — observable evidence of a cyclical bottom. (1) Dealer new field inventory down ~40% YoY and used large-tractor inventory falling (8R MY22-23 down >40% from peak, -20% sequentially in Q1) — destocking creates pent-up reorder demand. (2) Construction & Forestry (+34% sales) and Small Ag & Turf (+24%) already inflecting up. (3) Management explicitly calls 2026 the bottom, supported by China soybean buying resuming and government farm payments. The catch: large-ag, the profit engine (Production & Precision Ag), is still guided *down* 5-10% in FY26 — the inflection in the most important segment has not yet shown in retail, only in inventory normalization. This is an early-inflection setup, not a confirmed turn.

## Bull Case

- Trough is confirmed and FY2027 large-ag shipments re-accelerate as -40% dealer inventory forces restocking ahead of slow retail recovery; PPA margin recovers toward 11-13% guide.
- Precision-ag/recurring revenue compounds (engaged acres past 500M, recurring toward 10% of revenue), structurally lifting the margin floor and earning a higher through-cycle multiple.
- Mid-cycle earnings power of ~$24-28/sh re-rates the stock from trough-PE to a normalized ~22-24x, independent of multiple expansion.
- Tariff mitigation (sourcing optimization, re-shoring) recovers part of the $1.2B FY26 burden in FY27.

## Bear Case

- **Warranty expense (under-covered risk).** Management flagged *higher warranty expenses* as a specific drag on Production & Precision Ag's margin collapse to 4.4% — a quality/cost issue, not just cyclical volume, that the "bottom of the cycle" headlines largely ignore. If warranty costs persist it signals product-quality or reliability problems that erode the uptime moat and margin recovery.
- Farm income recovery is propped by *government payments, not crop receipts* (USDA sees 2026 net cash farm income +3%, driven by the $12B Farmer Bridge Assistance Program) — a policy reversal or expiry removes the demand crutch and the "bottom" slips to 2027.
- Tariffs ($1.2B pretax FY26, <half IEEPA, rest 232 steel) could escalate; price/cost is only guided "neutral" and could turn negative.
- Large-ag retail still falling (industry -15-20% FY26); if destocking does not convert to reorders, FY27 disappoints and the trough-multiple is justified.
- Stock is not cheap on trough earnings (PE ~31x); a slow/no recovery makes the entry expensive.

## Verdict

**WATCH** — Conviction: MEDIUM

Deere is a top-tier industrial franchise (moat 5/5, quality 20/25) at an observable early cyclical inflection with reliable models pointing to ~+15-30% and analysts ~+20%. But the variant perception is modest rather than contrarian (consensus already accepts "2026 = bottom"), the most important segment (large ag) is still guided down in FY26, the entry is not cheap on trough earnings (~31x trailing), and the under-covered warranty/quality drag plus government-payment-dependent farm income are genuine open risks. With Q2 earnings landing tomorrow (May 21), the asymmetry favors waiting for the print rather than buying into binary event risk a day early.

**If WATCH:** Would upgrade to BUY on (1) Q2/Q3 confirmation that large-ag *order velocity* is building into FY2027 (not just inventory normalizing), with PPA operating margin trending back toward the 11-13% guide and warranty costs proving transient; or (2) a price pullback toward ~$480-500 (mid-cycle ~20x) that builds in a margin of safety against a delayed recovery. Generic guidance for existing holders: a high-quality core hold through the cycle; trimming only warranted if the position is oversized given the binary near-term event.
