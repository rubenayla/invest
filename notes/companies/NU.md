# Nu Holdings Ltd. (NU)

## Business Primer

**What they do today.** Nu Holdings is the parent of Nubank, a branchless digital bank that runs entirely through a smartphone app. It serves over 135 million customers across Brazil (115M+), Mexico (15M+), and Colombia (~5M). It makes money three ways. First, lending: it issues credit cards and personal loans and earns interest plus card-network/interchange fees — this is the biggest piece. Second, deposits: customers park money in a Nubank account ("Nu account" / Cuenta in Mexico) that pays interest, and Nubank re-lends that cheap funding at higher rates, pocketing the spread (net interest income was $3.25B in Q1 2026 at a 21.1% net interest margin). Third, fees and float: payments, insurance, marketplace, and investment products. In Q1 2026 it cleared $5B of quarterly revenue and $871M of net income for the first time, at a 29% return on equity (ROE = profit divided by shareholder equity; 29% is exceptional for a bank).

**Why customers choose them (the value proposition).** Latin American incumbent banks (Itaú, Bradesco, BBVA, Banamex) historically charged some of the world's highest fees, demanded paperwork and branch visits, and turned away tens of millions of people with no formal credit history. Nubank's pitch is concrete: open a full bank account from your phone in minutes with no monthly maintenance fee, get a no-annual-fee purple credit card, send instant free transfers (via Brazil's Pix system), and earn interest on idle cash automatically. Where a traditional bank might charge a poor worker monthly account fees and reject them for a card, Nubank onboards them for free and extends a small starting credit line, then grows it as they prove repayment. The result is mass adoption among the previously unbanked and fee-weary: roughly 6 in 10 Brazilian adults now use Nubank, and 83% of all customers are monthly-active. The cost advantage is structural — no branches means Nubank's efficiency ratio (operating cost as a share of revenue) is ~18-20% versus 40-50% for legacy banks, so it can profitably serve customers the incumbents found uneconomic.

**Where they want to grow.** Three explicit bets. (1) Deepen Brazil monetization — push customers from a free account into loans, payroll-deductible lending, insurance, investments, and a planned mobile-phone service, lifting average revenue per active customer (ARPAC, ~$16/month, still a fraction of a mature bank's). (2) Scale Mexico and Colombia, which just passed inflection — Mexico delivered its FIRST quarter of accounting profitability in Q1 2026, ahead of plan, after burning ~$30M/quarter; Colombia is next. (3) Begin a measured United States expansion, capped to cost less than 100 basis points of efficiency ratio in 2026 and 2027 and explicitly contingent on proving product-market fit. Success in 3-5 years looks like 200M+ customers, Mexico/Colombia each as standalone profit engines, and ARPAC climbing toward incumbent levels.

**What could go wrong (business risks).** This is a credit lender in volatile emerging markets, so the core risk is a credit cycle: Brazil's central bank rate (Selic) has been high, household debt-service burden is elevated, and a recession or unemployment spike would drive loan losses up across its $37.2B credit book. The 15-90 day early-delinquency ratio already rose 89 basis points to 5.0% in Q1 2026 (management attributes it to seasonality and intentional risk expansion, not deterioration). Competition is intensifying — incumbents have built their own apps, and Mercado Pago (MELI), Inter, and PicPay compete for the same digital customer. Currency risk is large: results are earned in Brazilian real, Mexican peso, and Colombian peso but reported in dollars, so a weakening real compresses dollar earnings even when local growth is strong. Regulatory risk includes possible interest-rate caps on payroll loans. And the US expansion is unproven capital at risk.

**How to think about it.** Nubank is a high-growth emerging-market fintech "challenger bank" eating share from slow, expensive incumbents — closer to a profitable, hyperscaling consumer-lending platform than a sleepy regional bank. The bull frame is a 30%-ROE compounder with a long customer-and-product runway; the bear frame is a single-cycle-untested EM lender whose dollar earnings live or die on the Brazilian real and the Brazilian consumer credit cycle.

## Situation Summary
Nubank reported a record Q1 2026 on 2026-05-14: revenue crossed $5B for the first time, net income hit $871M (+41% year-over-year), ROE was 29%, and it added ~4M customers to reach 135M+. The standout was Mexico turning its first-ever quarterly profit (IFRS basis) ahead of internal plan. Yet the stock trades near 52-week lows ($12.62 vs. $18.98 high), because the market is fixated on credit quality: the 15-90 day early-delinquency NPL ratio jumped 89 bps to 5.0% and risk-adjusted net interest margin fell 100 bps sequentially to 9.5% as provisions rose 33% quarter-over-quarter to $1.79B. Management frames the NPL move as seasonality (65 of the 89 bps), intentional risk expansion (17 bps), and product mix (4 bps) — not asset-quality decay — and notes 90+ NPLs actually fell 10 bps to 6.5%, well below the 7% 2024 peak. Note: contrary to the smart-money premise, Berkshire Hathaway fully EXITED its Nu position by end-2024 and is no longer a holder.

## Variant Perception
- **Consensus view:** Analysts are bullish (21 opinions, "strong buy," mean target $19.49 = +54% upside), but the *stock* trades near 52-week lows, implying the marginal price-setter is skeptical — worried Brazil's elevated household debt-service ratio and rising early delinquencies signal the start of a credit-cycle downturn that will erode the 30% ROE, compounded by a weak Brazilian real cutting dollar earnings.
- **Our view:** The Q1 NPL increase is dominated by seasonality and *deliberate* risk-taking, not involuntary deterioration — 90+ NPLs (the lagging, harder truth) actually fell, coverage is 2.5x the 90+ balance, and provisions are running at 153.8% of new NPL formation (over-reserving). Meanwhile Mexico just crossed into profit ahead of plan, removing a structural drag the market still prices as a cash sink. The market is conflating "the most profitable quarter ever, plus a second country reaching breakeven" with "deteriorating credit." If 90+ NPLs stay flat-to-down through 2026, the de-rating reverses.
- **Trigger:** Q2 2026 earnings (~mid-August 2026) showing 90+ NPLs stable/declining and risk-adjusted NIM reverting toward H2 2025 levels, plus a second consecutive profitable Mexico quarter — forcing the market to re-rate from "early credit deterioration" to "intentional growth investment."

## Inflection Point
Yes — two genuine ones. (1) **Mexico crossed into profitability** in Q1 2026 for the first time, ahead of plan, flipping a ~$30M/quarter cash drain into a profit engine; Colombia is the next domino. This is observable, not hoped-for. (2) The market is treating Q1's NPL uptick as the *start* of a credit-cycle inflection downward; if it instead proves seasonal (as 90+ NPLs declining suggests), the stock is at a sentiment trough, not a fundamental one. The stock near 52-week lows on record fundamentals is the setup.

## Bull Case
- Mexico just turned profitable ahead of plan; Colombia next — two of three countries become standalone profit engines, multiplying earnings beyond Brazil.
- ARPAC at ~$16/month is a fraction of mature-bank levels; cross-selling loans, insurance, investments, and the new mobile service has years of monetization runway with minimal incremental cost.
- 90+ NPLs (the lagging truth) FELL to 6.5%, well below the 7% 2024 peak; provisions over-reserve at 153.8% of new formation — if credit normalizes, risk-adjusted NIM reverts up and reported earnings inflect.
- Sustained 29-30% ROE while trading at 10.9x forward earnings — multiple is depressed relative to growth and profitability.
- Stock near 52-week lows on the most profitable quarter in company history = sentiment/fundamental divergence.

## Bear Case
- Brazil credit cycle turns for real: elevated household debt-service ratio + rising unemployment push the $37.2B book's losses up; the 15-90 NPL already rose 89 bps and risk-adjusted NIM fell 100 bps.
- **Under-covered disclosed risk:** in Q1, credit cards + unsecured lending were **98% of new exposure** (vs 88% a year ago) — the incremental book tilted hard toward the highest-loss products, mechanically lifting future provisions regardless of underwriting quality. Headlines led with "record profit"; this mix shift is the quieter structural drag. Management also flagged 10-15% first-payment-default on private payroll loans (a supposedly *secured* product) and pending regulation to cap payroll-loan pricing.
- Currency: earnings are in real/peso, reported in dollars — a weakening Brazilian real compresses dollar EPS and the dollar stock price even if local growth is fine.
- Competition: Mercado Pago (MELI), Inter, PicPay, and incumbent banks' own apps erode the digital-first edge and pressure ARPAC.
- Premium valuation (5.4x book) leaves no cushion if ROE slips from 30% toward the low 20s.
