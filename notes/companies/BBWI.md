# BBWI — Bath & Body Works, Inc.

**Price:** $21.81 (2026-06-24)
**Rating:** BUY LOW (lean) / WATCH — deep-value, cash-gushing #1 US body-care brand at ~8-9x
*adjusted* earnings and ~19% trailing FCF yield, freshly washed out ~25% on a credible-but-unproven
turnaround reveal; the asymmetry is attractive but FY26 is explicitly a down/"investment" year and
the balance sheet is levered, so I want a sub-$20 entry before sizing up.
**First look:** 2026-06-24

Bath & Body Works is the remainco of the old L Brands after Victoria's Secret was spun off in 2021.
It is the dominant US specialty retailer of body care (lotions, washes, fragrance mists) and home
fragrance (the 3-wick candle, wallflowers), ~$7.2B revenue, ~1,800 mostly-mall/strip stores plus a
growing direct channel, vertically integrated with a fast, largely US-based supply chain. Gross
margin ~43%, operating margin ~17% — franchise-grade economics for a retailer. The stock has fallen
from ~$34 to $21.81 (only ~38% of its 52-week range of $14.28–$33.96), most of the damage coming when
new CEO Daniel Heaf unveiled his multi-year turnaround plan alongside an earnings miss and the stock
dropped ~25% in a single session.

## Why it's on the radar
- ~36% off the 52-week high, near the low. A genuine washout, not a dip.
- ~7x trailing GAAP EPS (~$3.1) and ~8.7x the midpoint of FY26 *adjusted* EPS guidance ($2.40–$2.65).
- Trailing FCF ~$0.86B on a $4.4B market cap = **~19% FCF yield**; EV/EBITDA ~6.1x.
- 3.7% dividend yield, payout only ~23% of GAAP EPS (~$0.80 on $3+) — well covered, you're paid to wait.
- Credible new operator: Daniel Heaf (ex-Nike, ran Nike Direct from ~$10B to $22.3B) took over May 2025.
- Analyst mean target ~$25.6 (≈ +18%), range $19–$54 — wide disagreement, the hallmark of a contested name.

## The bear case (lead with it — this is the crux)
1. **Secular erosion, four straight years of revenue decline.** Net sales: FY22 $7.56B → FY23 $7.43B →
   FY24 $7.31B → FY25 $7.29B, and FY26 is **guided down another 2.5–4.5%**. Body care / home fragrance
   demand normalized hard after the COVID candle-and-soap boom, and the category faces dupes, Amazon,
   and Sephora/Ulta fragrance encroachment. A no-growth mall retailer deserves a single-digit multiple —
   which is exactly what it has, so cheapness alone is not a thesis.
2. **FY26 is explicitly a down "investment" year, and the real earnings power is lower than the headline.**
   Management guides GAAP EPS $3.00–$3.25 but **adjusted EPS only $2.40–$2.65** (the GAAP figure is
   flattered by one-time/tax items). So normalized earnings power is ~$2.50, not $3.11, and Heaf is
   spending behind the brand this year — margins compress before they (maybe) recover. You are catching
   a falling knife mid-restructuring, not buying a stabilized cash cow.
3. **Levered, negative book equity.** ~$4.71B total debt vs ~$0.82B cash (net debt ~$3.9B); EV ~$8.3B.
   Net debt/EBITDA ~2.9x. Stockholders' equity is **negative (−$1.28B)** from years of heavy buybacks
   plus the L Brands split structure. That is survivable for a high-FCF business (HD/MCD/SBUX run negative
   equity too), but it caps buyback firepower and leaves little cushion if a consumer recession hits a
   discretionary-fragrance retailer at the same time as refinancing.
4. **The turnaround is a "show me."** Heaf is exiting recently-entered adjacencies (men's grooming, hair),
   launching on Amazon in 2026 (reach, but brand-dilution and margin risk), and "reigniting the brand."
   These are reasonable moves, but every value-trap retailer has a credible-sounding plan. There is no
   proof yet that the core can return to growth; "early evidence" is management's word, not the tape's.

## The bull case
1. **Valuation is genuinely cheap for the quality of the cash flows.** ~8.7x adjusted EPS and ~19% FCF
   yield for a 43% gross-margin, 17% operating-margin, #1-in-category franchise is a low bar. Even *flat*
   normalized EPS (~$2.50) re-rated to a still-modest 10x is ~$25 + dividends — the market is pricing
   permanent decline, not stabilization.
2. **You're paid to wait and the float keeps shrinking.** 3.7% dividend covered ~4x by FCF, plus ~$0.4B/yr
   buybacks on a $4.4B cap (~9% of shares/yr at these prices if sustained). Cheapness + buyback = EPS
   support even with flat sales.
3. **Credible operator with a direct-to-consumer playbook.** Heaf's Nike Direct record is exactly the
   skill set a brand-led retailer with an under-monetized loyalty file (~38M members) and a weak digital/
   international footprint needs. Optionality on Amazon distribution and international franchising is real.
4. **Vertically integrated, US-made supply chain** is a tariff/geopolitics hedge versus import-reliant peers,
   and supports the high gross margin and fast newness cadence that drives the category.

## Entry / exit discipline
- **Entry interest:** sub-$20 (pushes FCF yield >20% and dividend toward 4%+, improves the margin of safety
  on a leveraged, secularly-challenged name). At $21.81 it is a starter/WATCH, not a back-up-the-truck.
- **Thesis-break (sell):** adjusted EPS guided below ~$2.00 with no FCF recovery; net debt/EBITDA pushing
  toward 3.5x; or a dividend cut (would signal the cash-flow cushion that underpins the whole thesis is
  gone). Two more years of accelerating sales declines with no margin stabilization = value trap confirmed.
- **What would change my mind (to a real BUY):** evidence the core category has stopped declining (flat/positive
  comps for 2+ quarters), Amazon/international adding incremental revenue without gutting margin, and
  adjusted EPS inflecting back above ~$2.80.

## Bottom line
A classic deep-value, cash-gushing, no-growth specialty retailer at a single-digit adjusted multiple with a
fresh ~25% washout and a credible new operator — positive but not slam-dunk asymmetry (~+22% EV/3y vs ~−22%
bear). The leverage, the negative book equity, the four-year sales slide, and the explicit "investment year"
mean this is a BUY LOW (lean) with entry discipline, not a clean bargain. Best bought sub-$20; a WATCH at $21.81.
