# Carrefour SA (CA.PA)

**Sector:** Consumer Defensive | **Industry:** Grocery Stores
**Price:** EUR 17.28 (2026-05-19) | **Market Cap:** EUR 12.2B | **EV:** EUR 27.7B
**Analysis Date:** 2026-05-19

## Business Primer

**What they do today.** Carrefour runs grocery stores. Roughly 14,000 of them across France, Spain, Brazil, Belgium, Poland, Argentina, plus franchise partners in the Middle East and Africa. The format mix matters: very large out-of-town hypermarkets selling food plus clothing, electronics and bicycles; mid-size supermarkets in towns; small Carrefour City and Carrefour Express convenience shops on busy streets; and in Brazil and Argentina, the Atacadão chain — giant warehouse-style stores selling pallets and bulk packs to restaurant owners, small shopkeepers and bargain-hunting families, alongside Sam's Club membership warehouses. FY 2025 revenue was about EUR 82B. France contributes roughly half the sales (EUR 11.1B in Q1 2026), Brazil about a fifth (EUR 4.7B in Q1 2026, almost entirely cash-and-carry through Atacadão), Spain about an eighth (EUR 2.8B in Q1 2026), and the rest is the smaller European and Latin American countries. Customers pay at the till in cash, card, or with the Carrefour-branded loyalty/payment card. The company also makes money from rental income on the gallery shops around its hypermarket parking lots, from financial services (insurance, consumer credit through Carrefour Banque), and from selling shelf space and ad inserts to the brands it stocks (a retail-media business that has grown fast since 2022).

**Why customers choose them (the value proposition).** A shopper in Lyon picks Carrefour over Leclerc, Auchan, Intermarché or the local independent because of a specific bundle: one large store within driving distance that stocks fresh fish, ready meals, baby clothes, a bicycle, and a TV in the same trip; a private-label range with three explicit price tiers — the deep-discount "Simpl" line at 20-30% below national brands, the mid-tier "Carrefour" own label at roughly 15% below, and the premium "Reflets de France" / "Carrefour Bio" lines for picky shoppers — so a household can trade up or down without leaving the store; the loyalty card that turns spend into vouchers usable a fortnight later, which mechanically encourages the next visit; a price-matching pledge on roughly 1,000 named everyday items advertised against the discounters; and "drive" pickup lanes where the customer orders online and a packer loads the boot in the parking lot in under five minutes, avoiding aisles entirely. Against Mercadona in Spain, which is cheaper on private label but has narrower range, Carrefour wins on assortment breadth (electronics, fashion, larger non-food). Against E. Leclerc in France, which has historically been about 5-8% cheaper on a basket, Carrefour has spent 2024-2026 closing the price gap (Nielsen pricing index moved from 95.6 to roughly the market average) and competes harder on loyalty rewards, store experience, and convenience-store density in cities where Leclerc is mostly suburban. Against the corner shop, Carrefour City wins on price, hours, and a guaranteed range. In Brazil, the value proposition flips entirely: a pizzeria owner in São Paulo drives a van to Atacadão because the store sells a 20kg sack of flour at a price 15-25% below the supermarket equivalent, no membership required, with the bulk-pack design (24-can trays of soda, 5L oil jugs, palletised rice) explicitly built for someone who will resell or feed many mouths. The same store also lets a low-income family buy a single can of beans at the warehouse price, which is why Atacadão is described as a "hybrid" — wholesale to small business plus retail to the price-sensitive consumer in one box. That dual customer base is what gives Atacadão higher and steadier volumes per square metre than a regular hypermarket.

**Where they want to grow.** CEO Alexandre Bompard's "Carrefour 2030" plan, reiterated at the FY 2025 and Q1 2026 updates, narrows the company to three core markets — France, Spain, Brazil — and treats the others (Belgium, Poland, Argentina, Romania) as "dynamic asset management," meaning sell or shrink. Italy was divested in 2025, Romania's sale (~EUR 823M to Megaimage) is in antitrust review. In France, the goal is to digest the 2024 Cora/Match acquisition (175 stores rebranded under Carrefour banners with aligned pricing, loyalty, IT, and logistics) and push private-label penetration toward 40%. In Spain, more convenience openings (34 new shops in Q1 2026) and e-commerce growth (+9% Q1). In Brazil, keep rolling out Atacadão — management has talked about a 470-store cash-and-carry footprint by 2026 and 70 more Atacadão openings by 2030. Layered on top: a EUR 1.2B annual cost-savings programme, a digital/retail-media business (Carrefour Links) sold to brands as a Criteo-powered ad network, and a "Maxi" hard-discount banner being tested as a Leclerc counter. Targets are growth in recurring operating income, +25 basis points of margin improvement vs 2025, growth in net free cash flow above the 2025 base of EUR 1.57B, and high-single-digit growth in adjusted EPS for 2026.

**What could go wrong (business risks).** Five real concerns. (1) Brazil cash-and-carry deflation: food inflation dropped from 4.1% in Q4 2025 to 2% in Q1 2026, which is what made Brazil LFL print -0.8% — Atacadão's model amplifies any volume shortfall because gross margin is razor-thin, and arch-rival Assaí (~R$85B sales, growing 5%+) is the most aggressive same-store-sales competitor in the country. (2) France margin: operating margin is 2.5%, leaving almost no error budget; the Cora/Match price alignment cost real gross profit dollars in 2025 (net income fell from EUR 1.66B in 2023 to EUR 0.32B in 2025, partly tax/one-offs but also genuine integration drag), and Leclerc's cooperative structure lets it permanently undercut on price. (3) Non-food deflation: hypermarket non-food (clothing, electronics, household goods) has been declining for three straight years across the network — non-food was -4.3% LFL in France 2025 and -6.4% LFL in Brazil Q1 2026. Hypermarkets are 50% of the floor space; if non-food keeps shrinking, the format is uneconomic. (4) Regulatory: France's Egalim laws cap retailer pricing power vs food producers; the "Loi Galland" framework on supplier rebates limits margin levers; a wealth/transition tax cost EUR 516M in 2025. (5) Capital allocation: balance sheet is fine (net debt/EBITDA 2x, EUR 6.2B cash) but the EUR 1.5B+ in annual capex required to refurbish hypermarkets and grow Atacadão competes with the dividend (current payout ~65%) and ad-hoc buybacks for free cash flow.

**How to think about it.** Mature European grocer trading at trough valuation (EV/EBITDA 5.9x, FCF yield ~20%, dividend yield 5.6%) where the bull case rests on Atacadão's secular cash-and-carry growth in Brazil compounding while France stops bleeding margin, and the bear case rests on Brazilian disinflation and French price wars both grinding the cash flow shield down to nothing.

## Situation Summary

Q1 2026 (reported April 2026) was a "good enough, not great" print: Group sales +2.2% LFL to EUR 21.1B, France +1.4% LFL (an acceleration from FY25's +0.4% — proof that Cora/Match alignment is starting to work), Spain +3.1% LFL, Brazil -0.8% LFL (food inflation collapse, non-food weak, but Atacadão still took market share). Management reaffirmed FY 2026 guidance: ROI growth, +25bps margin improvement vs 2025, NFCF growth above EUR 1.57B, high-single-digit EPS growth. Capital return: ordinary dividend EUR 0.97/share (+5.4%) plus a EUR 150M special dividend; no fresh buyback announced. Stock has rallied from EUR 11.58 (52w low, mid-2025) to EUR 17.28 — up ~49% — and now sits at the 52w high (EUR 17.54). Analyst consensus target is EUR 15.77, below current price; rating "hold."

## Variant Perception

- **Consensus view:** Hold. Mean target EUR 15.77 (~9% downside). Market sees a structurally challenged French hypermarket operator with Brazilian exposure that has now re-rated to fair value after a 49% twelve-month run. Investors who bought it at EUR 11-12 as a deep-value/turnaround trade are taking profits; the next marginal buyer has to underwrite the 2030 plan.
- **Our view:** The 49% rally has closed most of the easy upside. Models cluster tightly around EUR 19-25 (GBM_3y is the bearish outlier at EUR 15.55, -10%). The Q1 2026 print confirms France is healing but does not yet prove margin expansion is mechanical. Atacadão's growth story is real but cyclically pressured by Brazilian disinflation — exactly when management needs cash-and-carry to compensate for slower France, the macro tailwind weakens. At 12.2x trailing P/E and 9.2x forward, the stock is no longer screaming-cheap; it is "fairly priced for a low-growth grocer with a 5.6% dividend and a real-but-not-explosive Brazilian growth engine." Variant view is weak in either direction.
- **Trigger:** H1 2026 results (late July 2026) showing actual operating margin expansion, not just sales growth; Romania disposal close and special dividend confirmation; Brazil food inflation trajectory (if it stays under 3%, Atacadão LFL stays weak).

## Inflection Point

Partial. France is genuinely inflecting positive — +1.4% LFL in Q1 2026 vs +0.4% FY 2025, Cora/Match price/loyalty alignment now visibly working (NPS up 3-11 points). Spain accelerating. **But Brazil is inflecting the wrong way** — food deflation has bitten, Atacadão LFL turned negative. The two largest divisions are moving in opposite directions, which dampens the inflection signal. Romania divestment plus Carrefour 2030 plan are corporate-event tailwinds. Net: modest inflection, not the kind of unmissable turn that demands an immediate position.

## Bull Case

- France LFL keeps accelerating through 2026 as Cora/Match synergies fully ramp and Loi Egalim price war eases; operating margin hits guidance +25bps and stays there.
- Brazil food inflation re-accelerates back to 3-4% later in 2026, restoring Atacadão LFL to mid-single digits while store openings keep the absolute growth high.
- Romania sale closes, EUR 800M+ funds a larger special dividend or buyback (the French tax on buybacks is a friction not a blocker).
- Retail-media (Carrefour Links) becomes a visible margin contributor; market gives the segment a higher multiple than groceries.
- Re-rates to 11-12x earnings on EUR 1.10-1.20 EPS = EUR 13-14? Actually the model already has it at 12x. Bull needs multiple to EUR 22-25.

## Bear Case

- **Brazil disinflation persists** (deflation in commodities continues, Atacadão LFL stays negative through 2026) — this is the risk management called out explicitly in Q1 that is NOT in the headline news narrative (the press mostly led on "France improves"). Brazil is ~22% of group revenue and arguably most of the next-three-years growth story.
- French hypermarket non-food (-4.3% LFL) keeps shrinking; large stores become harder to fill profitably, requiring write-downs or footprint reduction.
- Leclerc / Mercadona / Aldi-Lidl intensify price war; the gross-margin investment to maintain the 1,000-SKU price pledge eats the +25bps margin guidance.
- Argentina collapses further or Belgium/Poland exits prove value-destructive on cash recovered.
- French wealth/transition tax recurs; payout ratio (65%) becomes uncomfortable if FCF dips below EUR 2B.

## Verdict

**WATCH** — Conviction: MEDIUM

The thesis is intact but the stock has already paid out most of the easy upside. Expected value +8-9% from here is positive but unexciting given the residual risk that Brazilian disinflation persists. Quality score 12/25 is below the "yellow flag" threshold (15) — this is a low-quality compounder, fine to own at a deep discount but not at fair value. **Existing holders: hold, collect 5.6% dividend, trim partially on any further EUR 18-19 spike before H1 results.** New buyers should wait for a setback.

**If BUY:** Entry at EUR 14-15 (pullback to ~9x trailing earnings) gives a real margin of safety; full position only on confirmation that both France LFL and Brazil LFL are positive simultaneously.
**If WATCH:** Would upgrade to BUY on (a) pullback to EUR 14-15, OR (b) two consecutive quarters of positive Brazil LFL plus France LFL >1.5% plus reaffirmed margin guidance.
