# Rocket Lab Corporation (RKLB)

**Sector:** Industrials | **Industry:** Aerospace & Defense
**Price:** $125.28 (2026-05-21) | **Market Cap:** ~$72.6B | **EV:** ~$71.0B
**Analysis Date:** 2026-05-21

## Business Primer

**What they do today.** Rocket Lab makes and sells two things: rocket launches, and the satellites (and satellite parts) that fly on rockets. The launch business runs **Electron**, a small rocket that carries about 300 kg to low Earth orbit — think a courier van for satellites the size of a fridge, used by governments and commercial operators who need a specific orbit on a specific date and don't want to wait for a shared SpaceX rideshare slot. It also runs **HASTE**, a suborbital version of Electron used by the U.S. military to test hypersonic weapons. The bigger half of the company now is **Space Systems**: Rocket Lab builds satellite components (reaction wheels, solar panels, star trackers, separation systems, and — after buying Mynaric — laser communication terminals) and increasingly whole satellites for defense primes, the Space Development Agency, and constellation operators. In Q1 2026, of $200M revenue, Space Systems was $137M (68%) and Launch was $64M (32%). They have enabled parts on more than 1,700 missions.

**Why customers choose them (the value proposition).** For launch: if you have a 200 kg satellite that must reach a precise orbit, your alternatives are (a) buy a seat on a SpaceX Falcon 9 rideshare, which is cheap but drops you in a generic orbit on SpaceX's schedule, or (b) buy an entire Falcon 9, which is overkill and expensive. Electron gives you a dedicated launch to your exact orbit on your timeline at a price scaled to a small rocket — the only Western small launcher flying at high cadence (it is the most-launched orbital small rocket in the world). For Space Systems: a defense contractor that wins a constellation contract but doesn't build spacecraft can hand the whole job to Rocket Lab — bus, components, integration — instead of stitching together a dozen suppliers. Rocket Lab's pitch is vertical integration: they make the wheels, the solar panels, the optical terminals, the bus, and (soon) the medium rocket, so a customer gets an end-to-end Western alternative to SpaceX from one vendor.

**Where they want to grow.** The defining bet is **Neutron** — a medium-lift, partially reusable rocket (about 13,000 kg to low Earth orbit) aimed squarely at SpaceX Falcon 9's market. Electron is structurally too small to ever be highly profitable; Neutron is the path to national-security launch contracts, constellation deployment, and eventually cargo/crew. First flight is targeted for Q4 2026 (FAA permit window July–December 2026). Beyond Neutron, the strategy is to keep buying its way up the satellite value chain (Mynaric for laser comms, Motiv for space robotics and the supply-constrained motion-control parts it was buying from others) and to win prime national-security work — most notably selection to support **Golden Dome / Space Based Interceptor** with Raytheon, which management calls potentially the U.S. government's most important national-security program. Success in 3–5 years looks like "the credible number-two launch-and-satellite provider behind SpaceX, with Neutron flying at a Falcon-9-like cadence."

**What could go wrong (business risks, not stock risks).** Neutron is the dominant execution risk: a reusable medium-lift rocket built from scratch is brutally hard, the program has slipped repeatedly (now Q4 2026), and a Stage 1 tank ruptured during a January 2026 hydrostatic test — fixed by switching to an automated fiber-placement process, but a reminder that hardware-rich milestones remain (engine qualification, stage separation, the landing barge sea trials). SpaceX dominates global launch so completely that Rocket Lab must permanently occupy a price/schedule niche SpaceX won't bother to crush. Cash burn is structural — 2025 free cash flow was −$322M and management warns capex stays elevated through first flight — so a slip into 2027/2028 risks another equity raise on top of the dilution already happening (share count is heading to ~629M from ~579M). And Space Systems margins are being diluted by the lower-margin Mynaric mix and the scale-but-cheap Space Development Agency tranche work, which is why Q2 GAAP gross margin is guided *down* to 33–35% from 38.2% in Q1.

**How to think about it.** This is **infrastructure**, not software subscriptions: project-based, lumpy, capital-intensive revenue with negative margins today and a binary hardware catalyst (Neutron) ahead. The business is genuinely good and strategically positioned. The entire investment debate is whether a stock trading at ~107x trailing sales already prices three flawless years of execution from a company with a four-time-slipped flagship program.

## Situation Summary

Rocket Lab is the most credible Western orbital launch and space-systems company behind SpaceX, and Q1 2026 (reported May 7) was a clean beat across the board: record revenue of $200.3M (+63.5% YoY, above the $185–200M guide), record GAAP gross margin of 38.2%, record backlog of $2.2B (+108% YoY), and ~$2B+ liquidity after raising $450M via an at-the-market equity offering. New wins were stacked: selection for Golden Dome / Space Based Interceptor with Raytheon, a record MACH-TB hypersonic contract, five new dedicated Neutron launches, and the Mynaric (laser comms) and Motiv (space robotics) acquisitions. The stock has responded by ripping ~+48% in the last month and ~+78% in three months to $125, a $72.6B market cap on ~$679M trailing revenue — **P/S ~107x, EV/Revenue ~104x** — while the company posted a 2025 operating loss of $229M and burned $322M of free cash flow. The tell is in management's own mouth: Q2 GAAP gross margin guided *down* to 33–35% (Mynaric drag plus lower-margin SDA scale work), share count rising to ~629M, and Peter Beck refusing to narrow Neutron's debut beyond "later this year." Sell-side mean target is ~$100.84, roughly 19% *below* the current price — analysts who love the company are openly flagging the valuation.

## Variant Perception

- **Consensus view:** ~17 analysts, consensus Buy, mean target ~$100.84 (≈ −19% vs price); post-earnings revisions rising (Deutsche Bank to $120, Morgan Stanley OW $105). Short interest ~5.8% of float (moderate, not a crowded short). Institutions ~59%. The market narrative is "best-in-class space compounder, Neutron is the next leg, buy the breakout" — and momentum/retail flow has pushed the price past where the sell-side fundamentals models sit.
- **Our view:** The disagreement is *within* the bull camp, and it is about timing, not quality. Analyst price targets ($60 low, $100.84 mean, $127 high) say the stock has front-run two to three years of flawless execution; momentum flow says it doesn't matter. The variant call is that **the sell-side targets are roughly right and the price mean-reverts toward fundamentals before Neutron flies**, because (a) management itself guided gross margin *down* and share count *up* in the same quarter the stock rerated, (b) Neutron's own history is four consecutive slips, so the base rate on a clean Q4 2026 orbital debut is materially below what a 107x sales multiple assumes, and (c) at this multiple the asymmetry is poor — a clean Neutron success roughly justifies today's price, while any slip or anomaly opens a 30–50% drawdown.
- **Trigger:** Neutron first launch attempt, Q4 2026 (FAA window Jul–Dec; management won't narrow it). Binary. A clean orbit on attempt one could sustain or extend the multiple; a slip into 2027 or a flight anomaly forces another year of cash burn and likely a sharp de-rating. Secondary triggers: Q2/Q3 2026 prints showing the guided gross-margin compression actually landing, and any further ATM equity raises.

## Inflection Point

Yes — Rocket Lab is approaching a genuine inflection, which is precisely why it is hard. Two inflections are in play: (1) the **Neutron debut** (Q4 2026), which if successful converts the company from a sub-scale small-launcher into a Falcon-9-class competitor with access to constellation and national-security launch dollars; and (2) a slower **Space Systems scale inflection** as SDA tranches and Golden Dome ramp. The catch is that the *observable evidence the inflection has begun* is mixed: revenue acceleration and backlog are real and visible, but the headline catalyst (Neutron flying) has not yet happened, and management will not even narrow the quarter. The protocol's "enter after initial recovery starts" logic argues for waiting for first-flight evidence rather than paying 107x sales for the option ahead of it.

## Bull Case

- **Neutron flies cleanly in Q4 2026**, unlocking medium-lift launch revenue, constellation deployments, and national-security contracts that re-rate the company to a Falcon-9 competitor — the single biggest value driver and the whole thesis.
- **Space Systems scales hard**: $2.2B backlog (+108% YoY), SDA tranches, Golden Dome / Space Based Interceptor with Raytheon (management's "could be the most important national-security program"), and MACH-TB hypersonics — a multi-year defense growth runway largely independent of Neutron.
- **Vertical integration compounds**: Mynaric (laser comms) and Motiv (robotics + in-housing supply-constrained motion-control parts) deepen the moat and should lift Space Systems margins toward target once integrated ("no reason it can't be at or above target margins").
- **Best-in-class growth + fortress balance sheet**: revenue accelerating to $225–240M guided next quarter, ~$2B liquidity, net cash, founder-led — a rare combination in space.

## Bear Case

- **Valuation prices perfection**: ~107x trailing sales / ~104x EV/Revenue for a company losing $229M operating and burning $322M FCF. The sell-side mean target (~$100.84) sits ~19% *below* the price; the upside case rests almost entirely on multiple persistence + flawless Neutron execution, which is a low-quality (multiple-dependent) thesis.
- **Neutron base rate**: four consecutive schedule slips and a Stage 1 tank rupture in January 2026; management refuses to narrow Q4 2026 ("not enough visibility to nail it to a couple of weeks"). A slip into 2027 or a flight anomaly is a 30–50% drawdown risk into a binary event.
- **Margin compression is management-disclosed, not just a fear** *(under-covered by the bullish news flow)*: Q2 2026 GAAP gross margin guided DOWN to 33–35% from 38.2% in Q1, driven by the Mynaric mix (~$15M/qtr run-rate at below-segment margin) and lower-margin SDA tranche-2/3 scale work. The headlines lead with "record revenue and backlog"; the company's own guide says blended gross margin gets *worse* near-term — a quality-of-growth signal the price is ignoring.
- **Serial dilution funds the dream**: $450M ATM in Q1 plus $24M in April; weighted shares heading to ~629M (incl. ~46M preferred) from ~579M. Continued burn through Neutron means more raises are plausible — each one a quiet transfer from existing holders.
- **SpaceX overhang**: Starship maturing could compress the medium-lift price umbrella Neutron is being built to slot under, eroding the economics before Neutron even reaches cadence.

## Verdict

**PASS** (existing holders: trim into strength) — Conviction: **MEDIUM**

The variant perception is clear and is the rare case where it is *not* about disliking the company: Rocket Lab is a genuinely high-quality, well-managed, well-capitalized growth business, but at ~107x trailing sales the price has front-run a flawless Neutron debut from a program that has slipped four times, while management itself guided gross margin *down* and share count *up* in the same quarter the stock rerated +48%. Expected value is negative (≈ −13%) because the binary Neutron catalyst is asymmetric to the downside at this multiple — a clean success roughly justifies today's price, a slip opens a 30–50% drawdown — and the stock is technically overbought into it. The right setup is to wait for observable Neutron de-risking (a successful debut, or a meaningful pullback) rather than pay up for the option ahead of a catalyst the CEO won't even date precisely.

**If WATCH/BUY:** Would upgrade toward BUY on either (a) a clean Neutron orbital debut that de-risks the binary, or (b) a pullback to roughly $80–90 (near the 50-day MA and the upper end of analyst fundamentals) where the risk/reward into the catalyst turns favorable. Thesis-break for the bear case is a clean Neutron flight + Space Systems margin recovery to target.
