# Molina Healthcare, Inc. (MOH)

## Business Primer

**What they do today.** Molina is a managed-care insurer that runs health plans for people the government pays to insure — not corporate employees, not individual retail buyers. Three segments. **Medicaid (~85% of premium)**: states hand Molina a fixed per-member-per-month capitation payment to manage care for low-income enrollees in roughly 20 states; Molina pays providers, takes the spread. Big states are California, Texas, Florida, New York, Illinois, Washington. **Medicare (~8%)**: dual-eligible special-needs plans for seniors who are simultaneously poor and old (Medicare-Medicaid overlap), plus a shrinking Medicare Advantage Prescription Drug ("MAPD") book the company has decided to exit by 2027. **Marketplace (~7%)**: ACA exchange plans, mostly for low-subsidy near-Medicaid populations. Revenue is roughly $42B annualized in 2026; "premium revenue" is the right top-line figure, not "sales", because the entire model is collecting capitation and paying claims. The single most important number is the **Medical Care Ratio (MCR)** — medical claims divided by premium revenue. Anything in the 87-89% range is healthy; above 92% means losses.

**Where they want to grow.** Management's three-year Investor-Day plan (May 8 2026) sets premium revenue at ~$48B by 2027 and ~$64B by 2029, with adjusted EPS climbing from "at least $5" in 2026 to $25 in 2029 — a 5x earnings build over three years driven by margin restoration, not revenue growth alone. The strategy has three concrete bets: (1) **Win contracts** — new state Medicaid procurements (Florida's recently-awarded CMS contract is the biggest near-term win, expected to be earnings-accretive in 2027) plus reprocurements where Molina is the incumbent; (2) **Margin recovery** — pretax margins from below 1% in 2026 back to ~2.5% by 2029 as state capitation rates catch up to elevated medical-cost trend; (3) **Operating leverage** — administrative cost rationalization, AI-driven claims and care-management automation, and the runoff of one-time costs (Florida implementation, MAPD exit) that drained 2026. No transformational M&A is contemplated in the official plan; capital allocation tilts toward buybacks at trough prices and tuck-in deals.

**What could go wrong (business risks).** This is fundamentally a regulated spread business with government counterparty risk, and the spread can stay compressed for years. **Rate-trend gap persistence**: if states underfund Medicaid in 2026-2027 (a real possibility given federal pressure on Medicaid via DOGE-era spending cuts and FMAP changes), capitation rates won't catch up to 5-6% medical-cost trend and MCR stays north of 91%. **Acuity adverse selection**: redeterminations have removed ~6% of enrollees, and the healthier ones leave first — the remaining pool is sicker, and if state actuaries are slow to credit that acuity in capitation rates (a routine 12-18 month lag), MCR stays elevated even after headline rate hikes. **Marketplace tail risk**: ACA enhanced Premium Tax Credits expired January 1 2026; if Congress doesn't restore them, the 305K Marketplace book shrinks further into 2027 and the survivors are sicker. **Concentration**: California, Texas, Florida, and Washington collectively are most of Medicaid premium — a single bad re-procurement loss is material. **Litigation**: a Central District of California securities class action (Hindlemann, filed 2025) alleges management misrepresented cost-trend assumptions in 1H 2025; settlement risk is modest (<$200M plausibly), but discovery surfacing internal warnings could threaten the CEO/CFO.

**How to think about it.** This is a **commodity government-insurance spread business at a cyclical trough**, not a quality compounder. The right frame is **regulated-utility-with-a-rate-case**: claims trend ran ahead of rate increases for 18 months, the spread compressed to near-zero, and now the rate cycle is mechanically rebuilding the spread as state actuaries refresh their data on 2025 medical inflation. The investment question is not "is this a great business?" (it isn't — operating margins peak around 4-5%, ROE in good years is mid-teens) but "is the market pricing the cyclical trough as the new normal?" Compare to peers: **Centene (CNC)** is the cleanest comp (Medicaid-heavy, also trough-trading); **Elevance (ELV)** is more diversified with a large commercial book; **UnitedHealth (UNH)** is the high-quality multi-line incumbent. MOH at 36x trough EPS is "expensive" only if you anchor on the trough; on Investor-Day 2029 targets it trades at ~7x, which is the variant-perception bet.

## Situation Summary

The thesis pivot since my March 2026 analysis was completed by two events: the **April 23 Q1 print** (adjusted EPS $2.35 vs $2.17 consensus, consolidated MCR 91.1% — well clear of the 93% bear-case threshold) and the **May 8 Investor Day** (multi-year targets crystallized: $25 EPS by 2029, $64B premium revenue by 2029, pretax margin expansion 1% → 2.5%). The stock has run from $142 in March to $185 today (+30%), and the sell-side has lurched higher — Mizuho $200, Bernstein $208 from $173, Morgan Stanley $146 from $128 — but consensus 2029 EPS is still $17.10 vs. management's $25 target. The de-risking from "is the trough real?" has happened; the open question is whether the recovery path is straight-line ($25 by 2029) or interrupted by another bad year (the FY2026 guide implies 2H MCR running ~93%, worse than Q1). Stock dropped 5% on Investor Day itself as management trimmed long-term segment margin assumptions, suggesting the market is still policing the bull case.

## Variant Perception

- **Consensus view:** Hold rating (16 analysts, mean PT $182.88 — essentially at spot). Street believes the 2026 trough is real but lacks conviction on the 2029 build. Consensus 2027 EPS clusters at $9-11 (vs. management's implicit ~$13-15 trajectory to hit $25 by 2029), and consensus 2029 EPS of $17 implies the Street is haircutting management's plan by ~30%. Short interest at 7.95% of float is elevated but not extreme.
- **Our view:** The Street's $17 by 2029 is reasonable risk-adjustment of the $25 plan, but if Molina hits even $18-20 EPS by 2029 — a path that requires only ~70bps of MCR improvement plus the Florida contract layering in — the stock at 14-15x deserves $260-300. The key disagreement is **timing** of the rate cycle and **credibility** of the operating-leverage story, not the trajectory itself. Insider behavior supports management's framing: Director Zoretic bought at $125 in February, and the Investor Day was unusually specific about quarter-by-quarter cadence (rare for a turnaround pitch). The +30% run from $142 has already monetized the bear-case removal; the next leg requires evidence the recovery is on the 2029 path, not just the 2026 trough.
- **Trigger:** Three sequential gates, each dateable. (1) **Late May-June 2026** — preliminary July rate notices from FL/CA/TX state Medicaid agencies; clustered ≥4% increases would validate the rate cycle. (2) **Late July 2026 — Q2 earnings (~July 23)** — first clean print to test sequential MCR. Consolidated ≤91.5% would force consensus 2027 EPS revisions higher. (3) **Q3 2026 print (Oct-Nov)** — first full quarter under new July rates; the historical capitulation point where managed-care recoveries get fully priced.
