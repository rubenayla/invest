# Hims & Hers Health (HIMS)

**Sector:** Healthcare | **Industry:** Drug Manufacturers - Specialty & Generic
**Price:** $26.15 (2026-05-31) | **Market Cap:** $6.05B | **EV:** $6.43B
**Analysis Date:** 2026-05-31

## Business Primer

**What they do today.** Hims & Hers is a direct-to-consumer telehealth and online pharmacy. A customer goes to the website or app, fills out a medical questionnaire, and a licensed clinician (employed or contracted through the platform) reviews it asynchronously and, if appropriate, writes a prescription. The medication is then shipped to the customer's door on a recurring subscription, usually monthly. The whole loop — consult, prescription, fulfillment, refills — happens online without the customer ever visiting a doctor's office or a pharmacy counter. Revenue is overwhelmingly subscription-based: roughly 2.6 million paying subscribers as of Q1 2026, each paying a recurring monthly fee. The product categories are erectile dysfunction (the original cash cow), hair loss (finasteride/minoxidil), mental health (generic antidepressants, anxiety meds), skincare, and — the category that made the stock famous — weight loss using GLP-1 drugs (the semaglutide/tirzepatide class, the same molecules in Ozempic, Wegovy, and Zepbound). Newer categories include testosterone, menopause, and lab testing. For most of 2024-2025 a large slice of weight-loss revenue came from *compounded* semaglutide — pharmacy-made copies of the branded drug that were legal to sell only while the branded versions were in official FDA shortage.

**Why customers choose them (the value proposition).** The traditional path to one of these prescriptions is: book a doctor's appointment (days to weeks of wait), take time off work, pay a copay or full visit fee, get the script, then drive to a pharmacy and often discover the drug is expensive or out of stock. For conditions that carry social stigma — erectile dysfunction, hair loss, mental health, obesity — many people simply never make that appointment at all. Hims removes the friction and the embarrassment: the entire interaction is text-based, private, and finished in minutes, and the medication arrives discreetly packaged with automatic refills so the customer never runs out. For the GLP-1 weight-loss drugs specifically, the older draw was price — compounded semaglutide sold for a few hundred dollars a month versus $1,000+ list price for branded Wegovy, and Hims bundled the clinical oversight and dose titration into the subscription. The concrete benefit a customer gets is convenience plus privacy plus (historically) a much lower out-of-pocket price than the branded drug through insurance or a normal pharmacy.

**Where they want to grow.** Management's bets are: (1) ride the GLP-1 weight-loss wave but transition from compounded to *branded* drugs via supply deals with Novo Nordisk (Wegovy) and Eli Lilly (Zepbound), trading higher margins for legal durability and a much bigger addressable market; (2) expand internationally — the planned ~$240M acquisition of Eucalyptus brings telehealth operations in Australia, UK, Germany, Japan, and Canada, and international revenue already grew nearly tenfold year-over-year to $78M; (3) launch "proactive health" categories like testosterone, menopause, and lab testing that lengthen customer tenure beyond a single acute complaint. Success in 3-5 years looks like a diversified multi-category, multi-country subscription platform doing $5B+ in revenue where no single drug class dominates.

**What could go wrong (business risks).** The weight-loss business is now structurally dependent on supply agreements with the two pharma giants (Novo, Lilly) who are also competitors and who can change pricing or terms — Novo already terminated a deal once (June 2025) and sued Hims (Feb 2026) before settling. The branded-GLP-1 economics are worse than compounded: higher drug acquisition cost, lower gross margin, and Hims is partly a pass-through reseller. The core non-GLP-1 US business (ED, hair, mental health) actually contracted 8% year-over-year in Q1 2026, suggesting the legacy engine is maturing or being cannibalized by marketing spend redirected to weight loss. Regulatory and legal overhang is real: FDA warning letters across the telehealth-compounding industry, a pending SEC inquiry, and securities class actions. Customer churn is a chronic question for any subscription health product where people quit once the acute problem is solved. And the balance sheet now carries ~$1.1B+ of convertible debt to fund the international push.

**How to think about it.** It is a high-growth consumer-subscription challenger that scaled fast on a regulatory loophole (compounded GLP-1) and is now being forced to re-platform onto more durable but lower-margin branded supply while it diversifies into new categories and geographies. The bull frames it as the Amazon-of-healthcare brand compounding subscribers; the bear frames it as a marketing-driven reseller whose best margins are behind it and whose growth just decelerated to single digits in its home market.

## Situation Summary

Hims just reported a messy Q1 2026 (May 11): revenue $608M grew only ~4% YoY — a sharp deceleration from ~59% in FY2025 — and the company swung to a $92M net loss versus a $49M profit a year earlier, with adjusted EBITDA halving to $44M. The cause is a deliberate, disruptive pivot: after the FDA shortage of semaglutide ended, Hims wound down its high-margin compounded GLP-1 business (taking ~$33M in restructuring/inventory write-downs) and re-platformed onto branded Wegovy via a March 9, 2026 partnership with Novo Nordisk (which had terminated an earlier deal in June 2025 and sued Hims in Feb 2026 before settling) plus a Lilly Zepbound arrangement. Branded drugs carry higher customer-acquisition cost and lower margin, so gross margin compressed to 65% GAAP (70% adjusted) from 73%. Crucially, the core US non-GLP-1 business contracted ~8% YoY — the +4% headline was carried by international revenue growing ~10x to $78M. Management nonetheless *raised* full-year 2026 guidance to $2.8-3.0B revenue (19-28% growth) and $275-350M adjusted EBITDA, implying a large H2 reacceleration as monthly Wegovy cohorts compound. The stock sits at $26, down ~63% from its $70 ATH, with extreme 33% short interest, a pending SEC inquiry, class actions, fresh $350M convertible notes (May 18), and the CEO and C-suite having sold stock in April.

## Variant Perception

- **Consensus view:** Hold. Mean analyst target ~$26.6 (14 analysts: 3 Strong Buy, 9 Hold, 2 Sell), range $21 (BofA, Neutral) to $35 (JPMorgan, Overweight). The Street sees a high-growth DTC platform mid-transition: the compounding loophole is closing, branded GLP-1 is structurally lower-margin, the core US business has stalled, and legal/regulatory overhangs cap the multiple — but the raised guidance and international optionality keep it from being an outright short for most.
- **Our view:** Largely aligned with consensus — no strong edge. The bull-vs-bear gap hinges on one binary: does the H2 2026 reacceleration baked into guidance ($2.8-3.0B implies Q3/Q4 growth jumping back toward 30%+ from Q1's 4%) actually materialize as branded-GLP-1 and international cohorts compound? Management has every incentive to guide optimistically into a transition quarter, and the core US contraction (-8%) is the genuinely under-appreciated signal: if the legacy ED/hair/mental-health engine is structurally maturing, the whole growth story rests on commoditized branded GLP-1 reselling and an unintegrated international acquisition. We lean slightly more cautious than the JPMorgan bull case but see fair value roughly at the current price.
- **Trigger:** Q2 2026 earnings (early-mid August). Q2 guidance of $680-700M (+25-28%) is the first quarter that must show the reacceleration. A clean beat with stabilizing margins re-rates the stock toward $33+; a miss or another margin air-pocket confirms the bear case and breaks $20. Secondary triggers: SEC inquiry resolution and Eucalyptus close (H2 2026).

## Inflection Point

Yes — HIMS is squarely at a **negative-then-uncertain inflection**. The compounded-GLP-1 wind-down forced a step-down in margin and a Q1 loss; the question is whether the branded-GLP-1 + international model reaccelerates in H2. Observable evidence is mixed: 125k+ Wegovy shipments in the first 6 weeks and ~10x international growth are real green shoots, but the -8% core US contraction and the margin reset are red flags. This is *not* a clean turnaround you buy after confirmation — the confirmation (or refutation) comes at Q2 earnings in August. Buying now is buying the guidance on faith.

## Bull Case

- **H2 reacceleration is real:** raised FY26 guidance ($2.8-3.0B, +19-28%) implies Q3/Q4 growth snapping back toward 30%+ as monthly Wegovy cohorts compound; a clean Q2 beat re-rates the stock.
- **GLP-1 TAM is massive and Hims has distribution:** branded partnerships with both Novo and Lilly give legal, durable access to the largest drug category of the decade with a 2.6M-subscriber funnel to cross-sell.
- **International optionality:** Eucalyptus brings instant scale across 5 countries; international already +10x to $78M, a credible second growth engine the US-focused bears under-weight.
- **New high-tenure categories** (testosterone, menopause, labs) lengthen subscriber lifetime and diversify away from GLP-1 dependence.
- **33% short interest** sets up a squeeze on any positive surprise; the stock is down 63% from its high and sentiment is washed out.

## Bear Case

- **Core US business contracted 8% YoY in Q1 2026** — UNDER-COVERED by headlines that led with the "+4% revenue / GLP-1 pivot" story. If the legacy ED/hair/mental-health engine is structurally maturing, the entire growth thesis rests on commoditized branded-GLP-1 reselling + an unintegrated acquisition. This is the single most important data point and it is buried beneath the GLP-1 narrative.
- **Structural margin reset:** branded GLP-1 carries higher CAC and lower gross margin (65% GAAP vs 73%); management itself guides continued compression into Q2 from weight-loss and international scaling. The high-margin compounding profit pool is gone for good.
- **Supplier dependence:** weight-loss revenue now relies on Novo and Lilly — competitors who control pricing and have already shown willingness to terminate/sue. Hims is partly a pass-through reseller with little leverage.
- **Guidance credibility risk:** management raised FY guidance into a transition quarter while the CEO and C-suite sold stock in April; a Q2 miss would shatter the H2-reacceleration thesis and the multiple.
- **Legal/regulatory overhang:** pending SEC inquiry, securities class actions, industry-wide FDA scrutiny of telehealth compounding — tail risk that is hard to handicap.
- **Leverage:** ~$1.1B+ converts (incl. fresh $350M) and ~5.6x net debt/EBITDA fund an unproven international bet, removing the balance-sheet cushion it had a year ago.

## Verdict

**WATCH** — Conviction: MEDIUM

The thesis is a binary bet on whether H2 2026 reaccelerates as guided, and the answer arrives at Q2 earnings in August — so there is little reason to commit ahead of it. Expected value is mildly positive (+12%) but the dispersion is wide and the most important fact (core US -8%) argues the growth engine is narrower and lower-margin than the headline suggests. With the stock sitting right at the analyst mean and a clear dateable catalyst two months out, waiting costs little and removes most of the guess-the-guidance risk.

**If WATCH:** Would upgrade to BUY on a clean Q2 2026 beat (revenue at/above $700M) *with* core US revenue stabilizing (contraction narrowing toward flat) and adjusted gross margin holding ~70% — that combination validates the reacceleration and de-risks the lower-margin pivot. Conversely, a Q2 miss or worsening core US contraction triggers a downgrade toward PASS. Entry of interest below ~$22 (BofA bear target) on weakness pre-catalyst for risk-tolerant buyers; thesis-break / stop-thinking-bull level is a confirmed Q2 revenue miss or break below ~$18.
