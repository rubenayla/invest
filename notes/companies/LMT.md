# Lockheed Martin Corporation (LMT)

## Business Primer

**What they do today.** Lockheed Martin is the largest pure-play United States defense prime contractor, generating $75B of revenue in 2025 across four segments. **Aeronautics** (~37% of revenue) is dominated by the F-35 Lightning II — the only fifth-generation fighter in full-rate production globally — with the F-16, C-130 Hercules, and classified programs alongside. **Missiles and Fire Control** ("MFC," ~19% of revenue) builds the PAC-3 (Patriot Advanced Capability) interceptor, the HIMARS (High Mobility Artillery Rocket System) launcher and its GMLRS/PrSM rockets, the JASSM/LRASM family of standoff missiles, the Javelin antitank missile (joint venture with RTX), and the THAAD strategic missile defense system. **Rotary and Mission Systems** ("RMS," ~22% of revenue) is Sikorsky helicopters (Black Hawk, CH-53K, Seahawk) plus naval combat systems including the Aegis combat system fitted on US and allied destroyers. **Space** (~22% of revenue) builds military satellites, the Orion deep-space crew capsule, and increasingly missile-warning and missile-tracking satellites for the Space Force. Roughly 73% of revenue comes from the United States Department of Defense, ~25% from foreign military sales (largest customers: Israel, Saudi Arabia, Japan, South Korea, Australia, NATO/Europe), and ~2% commercial/civil.

**Where they want to grow.** Three explicit growth bets. First, **missiles and munitions replenishment** — management announced framework agreements with the Department of Defense to 3-4x production rates of PAC-3, THAAD, and PrSM (Precision Strike Missile), funded by multi-year demand commitments arising from Ukraine, Israel, and Indo-Pacific deterrence drawdowns. Second, **Golden Dome (homeland missile defense)** — Lockheed is one of 12 vendors selected for the Space Force space-based interceptor prototype (aggregate $3.2B program), plus part of the Palantir/Anduril-led consortium on the broader $185B Golden Dome integration architecture. Third, **F-35 sustainment and Block 4/TR-3 upgrade** — even after losing the next-generation F-47 fighter contract to Boeing in March 2025, Lockheed is pitching the F-35 Block 4 upgrade as a "bridge to F-47," extending the platform's $1.7T lifetime sustainment opportunity through the 2070s. Classified Aeronautics and Space programs (likely involving hypersonics, classified intelligence/surveillance/reconnaissance satellites, and the Space Development Agency proliferated constellation) are an additional growth vector but with bumpy execution.

**What could go wrong (business risks).** Three real risks. **F-35 cost overruns and production pace** — Q1 2026 deliveries fell to 32 aircraft versus 47 a year earlier (-32%); F-16 ($125M charge) and C-130 ($55M charge) program adjustments and the absence of prior-year favorable booking rates triggered the Q1 earnings miss. **Classified program write-downs** — Lockheed booked a ~$950M loss on a classified Aeronautics program in 2025 and a $66M Next Generation Air Dominance (NGAD) asset write-off after losing F-47 to Boeing, exposing the structural risk that fixed-price classified development contracts can blow up unpredictably. **Department of Defense budget pressure and tariffs** — DoD topline growth is slowing under deficit-control pressure; China's hypersonic and counterspace capabilities raise the stakes on classified programs Lockheed is not winning; and tariffs on subcontracted parts (titanium, rare earths, electronic components from allied and adversary jurisdictions) pressure already thin defense margins where the customer caps the price. Customer concentration on a single buyer (United States government) means political and budget shifts hit everything at once.

**How to think about it.** This is a United States defense prime concentrated in F-35 plus missiles — the investment thesis is a bet on (a) missile and munitions demand sustaining through Ukraine, Israel, and Indo-Pacific replenishment cycles, (b) F-35 sustainment revenue compounding as the installed base grows past 1,000 jets, and (c) classified upside from Golden Dome and proliferated low-earth-orbit constellations offsetting the F-47 loss. Versus peers: RTX is a missile and engine peer with more commercial aerospace exposure (Pratt and Whitney engines); Northrop has B-21 and Sentinel intercontinental ballistic missile; General Dynamics has Marine ships and combat vehicles; Boeing has F-47 plus commercial. LMT is the most concentrated in air dominance and missile defense, the least diversified in non-defense, and the most exposed to F-35 lifecycle outcomes.

## Inflection Point

Yes — earnings inflection visible in H2 2026. The Q1 2026 miss largely captures (a) absence of prior-year favorable adjustments ($80M classified completion, $125M Space commercial) and (b) one-time program charges (F-16 $125M, C-130 $55M, CH-53K $50M). The framework-agreement multi-year missile contracts begin contributing material revenue in H2 2026 and accelerate through 2027-2028. ## Bull Case

- **Missile replenishment supercycle accelerates** — PAC-3, HIMARS munitions, JASSM, Javelin demand from Ukraine, Israel, and Indo-Pacific deterrence pulls forward years of orders. Missiles and Fire Control revenue inflects from $14B (FY25 run rate) to $18-20B by 2028. Operating leverage drops 15%+ margins to the bottom line.
- **Golden Dome architecture awards land** — Lockheed captures meaningful share of the $185B aggregate program across space-based interceptors, missile-warning satellites, Aegis integration, and command and control. Could add $5-15B in cumulative backlog by 2027.
- **F-35 Block 4 / TR-3 sustainment compounds** — installed base passes 1,200 jets globally; sustainment revenue grows mid-single-digits with very high incremental margins. Classified Aeronautics charges roll off the comparison base.
- **Multiple re-rates from 16x to 19x forward** — historical mid-cycle multiple for a defense prime with growing, visible backlog and 5%+ free cash flow yield plus 2.6% dividend.

## Bear Case

- **Another classified program blow-up** — primary-source disclosure showed the 2025 $950M classified Aeronautics loss came as a surprise; the underlying fixed-price classified contract structure remains. A second blow-up in 2026-27 would shatter remaining management credibility. **(Underweighted by news — most headlines focus on F-47 loss as the past event, not classified contract structure as the recurring exposure management itself flagged in Q1 2026 disclosure.)**
- **DoD budget topline plateaus or cuts** — deficit pressure under the fiscal year 2027 budget could trim the Pentagon topline; missile and space spending may grow but Aeronautics legacy platforms (F-16, C-130) get squeezed. Foreign Military Sales delays (Saudi Arabia, Turkey) add risk.
- **F-35 production rate cuts** — if Block 4 / TR-3 software delays persist, customers (Pentagon especially) could defer or cancel orders, dropping deliveries further from 32 per quarter.
- **Tariffs on subcontracted parts compress margins** — titanium (Russian and Chinese sources historically), rare earths, electronic components. Lockheed cannot pass through to fixed-price contracts; absorbs as charges.
- **Tax and policy shifts** — Section 174 research and development capitalization tax treatment and any defense windfall taxes would hit free cash flow.
