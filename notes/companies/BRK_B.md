# Berkshire Hathaway Class B (BRK-B)

**Sector:** Financial Services | **Industry:** Insurance — Diversified (operating holding company)
**Price:** $488.38 (2026-05-19) | **Market Cap:** ~$1.05T
**Analysis Date:** 2026-05-19

## Business Primer

**What they do today.** Berkshire Hathaway is an operating holding company, not a fund. It earns money four ways. (1) **Insurance**: GEICO (auto), Berkshire Hathaway Reinsurance Group (large-limit catastrophe and life reinsurance), General Re, and a stack of primary specialty insurers. The combined float — premiums collected before claims are paid — is roughly $172 billion of insurance liabilities at Q1 2026, which Berkshire invests for its own account. (2) **Railroad**: BNSF, one of the two big Western US Class I freight railroads, hauling intermodal, agricultural products, coal, and industrial goods. Contributed $1.38B in Q1 2026 operating earnings. (3) **Berkshire Hathaway Energy (BHE)**: regulated utilities (PacifiCorp, MidAmerican, NV Energy), natural-gas pipelines, and renewables. Contributed $1.11B in Q1 2026. (4) **Manufacturing, Service & Retail**: a sprawling collection including Precision Castparts (aerospace parts), Lubrizol (chemical additives), Marmon (industrial), Shaw Industries (carpet), See's Candies, Dairy Queen, NetJets, plus the newly-acquired OxyChem (closed January 2, 2026, $9.5B). On top of all that, Berkshire owns a public equity portfolio worth ~$288B (Apple, American Express, Bank of America, Coca-Cola, Chevron as the big anchors) and is sitting on ~$391B in cash and Treasury bills.

**Where they want to grow.** Greg Abel became CEO on 2026-01-01 and used his first shareholder letter (2026-02-28) to commit explicitly to continuing the existing capital-allocation framework "into perpetuity": (a) reinvest in the operating businesses where returns are attractive; (b) make large opportunistic acquisitions of whole businesses when prices are sane (OxyChem is the first under his watch); (c) hold and add to public equities only at attractive prices, sized for the long term; (d) repurchase Berkshire stock when it trades below conservatively-estimated intrinsic value (Q1 2026 buyback was $235M, small but the program is alive); (e) never pay a dividend so long as retained earnings produce more than $1 of market value per $1 retained. Growth in **intrinsic value per share** is the explicit objective, not headline revenue or assets. Concretely, the next 3-5 years look like: deploy some portion of the cash pile into 1-2 large acquisitions if a dislocation arrives; let BNSF and BHE compound at low-to-mid single digits on regulated capital; let GEICO keep recovering its underwriting margin; let the equity portfolio compound; resume meaningful buybacks if the stock drops to ~1.2x book or so.

**What could go wrong (business risks).** (1) **Succession execution risk** — Abel inherits the framework but not Buffett's deal flow or his standing with private sellers; whether sellers still call Berkshire first for sub-$50B deals is unproven. (2) **Cash drag** — at $391B and growing, the cash pile earns ~4% on T-bills, which is below the long-run return on equity Berkshire targets; every year without a fat-pitch acquisition compounds the opportunity cost. (3) **Insurance reserve and catastrophe volatility** — Q1 2026 had no major catastrophes; one bad hurricane season or wildfire cluster can wipe out a year of underwriting profit. PacifiCorp's cumulative estimated probable wildfire losses are already $2.9B through Q1 2026 with management explicitly noting "additional losses beyond current accruals are reasonably possible" — this is a real, ongoing legal exposure, not a one-time charge. (4) **Concentration in the equity book** — Apple alone is still a double-digit-percent slice of the marked-to-market portfolio, so a tech derating hits reported earnings even though Berkshire's intrinsic-value math ignores quarterly mark-to-market. (5) **Regulatory exposure on the utilities** — BHE depends on constructive rate cases; PacifiCorp's wildfire saga has soured the political climate in Oregon. (6) **Law-of-large-numbers** — $1.05T market cap means even successful capital deployment moves the needle slowly; double-digit per-share compounding is mathematically harder than it was 20 years ago.

**How to think about it.** Berkshire is **an insurance-funded holding company with a permanent public-equity portfolio bolted on** — not a closed-end mutual fund and not a "Buffett ETF." The right peer set isn't Vanguard or BlackRock; it's other diversified holdcos (Markel, Fairfax, Loews) plus the regulated railroad and utility comps for the operating pieces. Price-to-book has been the right anchor for 20+ years (current ~1.47x vs. ten-year median ~1.42x), with the variant in any given year being whether float is growing, whether buybacks are active, and whether the cash pile is being deployed.

## Situation Summary

Q1 2026 was Greg Abel's first quarter as CEO. Operating earnings rose ~18% YoY to $11.35B, helped by an absence of major catastrophes (insurance underwriting $1.72B vs $1.34B) and steady contributions from BNSF ($1.38B) and BHE ($1.11B). The cash + Treasury pile hit a record **~$391B** ($51.5B cash + $339.3B in short-term Treasuries in the Insurance & Other segment). The equity portfolio is $288B, down modestly from $297.8B at year-end 2025. Abel's first capital-allocation moves: closed the **$9.5B OxyChem acquisition** on 2026-01-02, restarted **share buybacks** ($235M in Q1 — small but symbolic), and personally bought $15M of BRK in March. His first annual letter (2026-02-28) explicitly committed to the existing framework. The 2026-05-02 annual meeting was the first without Buffett at the helm; market reception was constructive ("Abel scores good marks").

## Variant Perception

- **Consensus view:** Steady, slightly-undervalued compounder; "fortress balance sheet" but huge cash drag; new CEO is competent but lacks Buffett's deal-sourcing edge; trades at fair P/B (~1.47x); analyst targets $481-570 (mean $520, ~7% upside) with a tepid "buy" lean and very few analysts covering it (3).
- **Our view:** Consensus is roughly correct on the static math but underweights two asymmetric setups. (a) The cash pile is **optionality, not waste**: at ~$391B and growing it represents ~37% of the market cap, so a single recession-driven 20-30% drawdown in equity markets would let Abel deploy at distressed prices with virtually no execution risk — and the buyback restart shows he is willing to pull the trigger. (b) The OxyChem deal is a **proof-of-concept that the deal pipeline still works post-Buffett**: sellers still bring Berkshire deals, and Abel was willing to write a $9.5B check on day one. If a second large acquisition lands in 2026-2027, the "Abel can't source deals" discount narrows materially.
- **Trigger:** Either (a) a second >$5B whole-business acquisition by year-end 2026, or (b) buyback authorization scaling beyond ~$1B/quarter (a signal Abel sees BRK itself below intrinsic value), or (c) a market dislocation that lets the cash pile deploy. Reasonable window: 6-18 months.

## Inflection Point

There is a **mild but real** inflection here: the Buffett-to-Abel handoff is the first generational transition since 1965. The observable evidence that it's working (not just hoping):
- Closed the $9.5B OxyChem deal on day one (proof deal pipeline survives).
- Restarted buybacks in Q1 ($235M) after a long pause — Abel willing to repurchase, which Buffett was reluctant to do at recent prices.
- Abel personally bought $15M in March 2026 (insider signal).
- 2026-05-02 annual meeting was well-received.

This isn't a "turnaround" inflection (the company was never broken). It's a "regime continuity" inflection — and that uncertainty is the cheapest piece of the thesis to own, because it resolves slowly and asymmetrically (one good acquisition closes the discount).

## Bull Case

- **Cash deployment in a dislocation.** A 2026-2027 recession or credit event lets Abel deploy $50-100B+ into either whole-business acquisitions or public equities at distressed prices; this alone could add 10-15% to IVPS in a 12-18 month window.
- **Buyback acceleration.** If the stock dips toward 1.25-1.30x book, expect buybacks to scale to multi-billion-per-quarter; per-share value compounds even without market action.
- **OxyChem proves deal pipeline intact.** A second $5B+ acquisition before YE 2026 closes the "Abel discount" narrative.
- **Insurance underwriting cycle.** GEICO's auto-insurance turnaround continues; BHRG's catastrophe pricing stays firm; underwriting profits stay above $5B/yr.
- **Tax/structural backstop.** Even doing nothing, ~4% T-bill yields on the cash pile = ~$16B/yr risk-free interest income, a real earnings floor.

## Bear Case

- **PacifiCorp wildfire tail risk (under-covered).** The 10-Q explicitly states "additional losses beyond current accruals are reasonably possible" with $2.9B already booked and a recent Oregon Court of Appeals reversal of the Phase I verdict adding legal uncertainty. News headlines have largely moved on from this story but management hasn't.
- **Cash drag compounds.** Another 2 years without a fat-pitch acquisition = $30B+ of opportunity cost; the "wait for a fat pitch" posture has a half-life.
- **Equity book concentration.** Apple, AmEx, BAC, KO, CVX are >60% of the equity book; a tech derating or financial-sector crisis hits reported earnings hard even if intrinsic value is unaffected.
- **Succession deal-flow attrition.** If sellers stop bringing Berkshire deals (because Buffett's personal brand was the draw), the moat narrows asymmetrically over 5-10 years.
- **Regulatory tightening on utilities** — BHE's growth depends on constructive rate cases, and the wildfire saga has hurt the political environment in some service territories.

## Verdict

**WATCH** — Conviction: **MEDIUM**

Berkshire is a high-quality compounder at fair valuation (~1.47x book vs. 10y median 1.42x) with genuine optionality from a $391B cash pile and a credible-but-unproven new CEO. The variant perception — that consensus underweights the asymmetric value of cash optionality and the OxyChem-proves-pipeline signal — is real but not screaming. Expected value of ~+7-10%/yr is acceptable for a low-vol anchor but not compelling enough to chase here.

**Would upgrade to BUY at $450 or below** (P/B ~1.32x) — that level gives a clear margin of safety and historically marks the zone where Berkshire itself buys back stock.

**Would upgrade conviction on:** (a) a second >$5B whole-business acquisition announcement; (b) quarterly buybacks scaling to >$1.5B (signals Abel sees the stock below intrinsic); (c) a broad market drawdown that pushes the stock to ~1.30x book.

**PASS triggers (would downgrade):** PacifiCorp wildfire reserve balloon to >$10B, two consecutive years with no large acquisitions AND no meaningful buybacks, or insurance underwriting turning structurally unprofitable.
