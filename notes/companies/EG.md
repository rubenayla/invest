# Everest Group, Ltd. (EG)

**Sector:** Financial Services | **Industry:** Insurance - Reinsurance
**Price:** $354.96 (2026-05-21) | **Market Cap:** $14.0B
**Analysis Date:** 2026-05-21

## Business Primer

**What they do today.** Everest Group sells insurance to insurance companies. That is the core business: when a regular insurer (say, a Florida homeowners insurer) writes thousands of policies and worries that a single hurricane could wipe it out, it buys "reinsurance" from Everest to offload part of that risk. Everest collects a premium up front, holds and invests that money, and pays out when covered catastrophes or large losses occur. The biggest segment, **Reinsurance Treaty** (~$12B+ of premium), covers property-catastrophe, casualty, and specialty risks for hundreds of cedents (the insurers buying cover) worldwide. The second segment, **Global Wholesale & Specialty**, writes insurance directly for hard-to-place commercial risks (energy, construction, professional liability) through brokers. A third **Legacy** bucket is the run-off of the U.S. commercial retail insurance book that Everest is exiting (it sold the renewal rights to AIG for roughly $2B in 2025). On top of underwriting, Everest earns a large and growing stream of investment income on its ~$40B float — the premium cash it holds before paying claims.

**Why customers choose them (the value proposition).** An insurer buying reinsurance is buying two things: balance-sheet protection and capital efficiency. Without reinsurance, a mid-sized insurer must hold enormous capital reserves to survive a tail event like a Category 5 hurricane; that capital sits idle and drags returns. By ceding the tail to Everest, the insurer can write more policies on less capital and smooth its earnings. The customer picks Everest over a competitor (Munich Re, Swiss Re, RenaissanceRe, Arch) on three concrete things: financial strength rating (a cedent needs to know the reinsurer will actually pay after a $50B industry catastrophe — Everest carries A+ ratings), capacity (Everest can write very large lines, so a cedent can place a whole program with one counterparty instead of stitching together ten small ones), and relationship continuity (cedents value a reinsurer that renews through hard and soft markets rather than fleeing after a bad loss year). Reinsurance is a commodity in price but a relationship in practice — Everest's scale lets it lead programs and set terms rather than follow.

**Where they want to grow.** The explicit bet is to become a cleaner, higher-return reinsurance-led franchise. Management (CEO Jim Williamson, with new CFO Elias Habayeb, ex-Corebridge, starting May 2026) is (1) running off the loss-making U.S. retail commercial book via the AIG deal, which removes a ~110%+ combined-ratio drag; (2) reallocating capital toward property-catastrophe and specialty reinsurance where terms and attachment points are still favorable even as headline rates soften; (3) deliberately shrinking U.S. casualty pro-rata premium (down $1.2B+ since Jan 2024) because ceding commissions there are too rich; and (4) returning capital aggressively — buybacks were raised to a $300M/quarter floor with stated willingness to exceed it, repurchasing below book value. Success in 3-5 years looks like mid-teens-plus operating ROE on a smaller, sharper book with a re-rated multiple closer to peers.

**What could go wrong (business risks).** Reinsurance is cyclical and Everest writes catastrophe risk, so a single bad year of hurricanes, wildfires, or earthquakes can produce a large loss (Q1 2026 already absorbed $130M of cats including a $58M Iran-conflict provision). The pricing cycle is turning **softer**: property-cat rates fell ~13% at the April 2026 renewals and Florida is expected down mid-teens at 6/1, so the margin tailwind that drove 2023-2024 is fading. The deepest structural risk is **reserve adequacy in U.S. casualty** — long-tail liability claims (e.g. social-inflation-driven jury awards) can develop adversely years after the premium is booked; Everest took painful casualty reserve strengthening in 2023-2024, and any repeat would hit book value directly. Execution risk on the AIG transition (restructuring charges ~$150M expected through 2026) and a brand-new CFO add transitional uncertainty.

**How to think about it.** This is a cyclical, capital-return-driven reinsurer in the back half of a self-help turnaround — shedding a bad insurance book, buying back stock below book value, trading at a discount to tangible book despite a clean ~87% reinsurance combined ratio. The label is "post-restructuring reinsurer at sub-book value with improving underlying ROE, into a softening price cycle." The bull case is normalization of ROE plus buyback-driven book-value-per-share growth; the bear case is the cat/casualty cycle turning against it before the multiple re-rates.

## Situation Summary

Everest reported a strong Q1 2026 on 2026-04-30: consolidated combined ratio of 91.2% (89.3% excluding the run-off Legacy book), down sharply from 102.7% a year earlier, with Treaty Reinsurance at 87.2%. Net operating ROE annualized to 16.7% and book value per share rose to $393.02 (up ~4% from year-end after dividends) — a far cleaner picture than the blended 10.8% ROE that depressed the stock through 2025. Crucially, the feared reserve charge did NOT materialize: Everest reported $33M of *favorable* prior-year development and "no material movements in U.S. casualty," while management said it continues to hold elevated loss picks. The stock has recovered to ~$355 (near its 52-week high of $362.60), still only ~0.92x book value, as buybacks accelerated to a $300M/quarter floor with the CEO publicly stating the price "does not accurately reflect either current value or true earnings power." The offsetting concern is a clearly softening reinsurance market — property-cat rates down ~13% at April renewals, Florida expected down mid-teens at 6/1.

## Variant Perception

- **Consensus view:** A solid-but-cyclical reinsurer at the wrong point in the cycle. The hard market that lifted 2023-2024 results is over; property-cat rates are falling 10-20%, casualty stays uncertain, and the AIG transition adds restructuring noise. Analysts rate it a tepid "buy" with a $387 mean target (~9% upside), pricing it close to book value because softer pricing caps return-on-equity upside from here. The sub-book multiple is "deserved" for a peak-cycle cat writer.
- **Our view:** The market is anchoring on the 2025 *blended* ROE (10.8%) and the softening-rates narrative, and under-weighting two things the company just demonstrated. First, the *underlying* franchise already runs at a 16.7% operating ROE and an 87% reinsurance combined ratio once the bad insurance book is stripped out — that gap closes mechanically as Legacy runs off through 2026. Second, with the stock at ~0.92x book and the buyback floor raised to $300M/quarter, every dollar repurchased below book is instant, low-risk per-share book-value accretion — a self-funding compounding engine that does not require the cycle to cooperate. The reserve fear (the single biggest reason reinsurers de-rate) was explicitly tested this quarter and came back clean. The disconnect: a 16.7% operating-ROE business does not normally trade below tangible book.
- **Trigger:** H2 2026 results showing clean reinsurance-only economics as Legacy runs off, the AIG capital release becoming visible (management guided "back half of 2026"), and continued below-book buybacks compounding BVPS. New CFO Elias Habayeb (ex-Corebridge, a capital-return specialist) could articulate a sharper capital-return framework. Next dateable catalyst: Q2 2026 earnings, ~2026-07-29.

The **trustworthy cluster is the GBM models and autoresearch ($378-$481)**, which collectively imply roughly +10% to +40% upside, centering around $400-$420. **simple_ratios ($578)** sits in between and is plausible if EG re-rates toward peer book multiples. Net: the reliable models point to modest-to-good upside (+10-40%), consistent with a sub-book reinsurer normalizing — not a multi-bagger, not a value trap.

## Inflection Point

**Yes — a visible, evidence-backed turnaround inflection.** This is not a hopeful "things will get better"; the Q1 2026 print is observable evidence the inflection has begun: (1) combined ratio collapsed from 102.7% to 91.2% YoY; (2) operating ROE jumped to 16.7%; (3) the reserve fear that haunted 2023-2025 was explicitly tested and came back *favorable* ($33M positive development, no U.S. casualty deterioration); (4) the loss-making Legacy book is in active run-off with the AIG deal "progressing as planned" and capital release guided for H2 2026. The corporate-event layer (insurance-book divestiture + new CFO with a capital-return pedigree + raised buyback floor) is exactly the "new management with a clear plan" inflection pattern. The remaining question is durability against a softening cat cycle, not whether the turn has started.

## Bull Case

- Legacy run-off completes through 2026, lifting the *reported* group ROE toward the 16.7% underlying level, and the market re-rates a clean reinsurer from 0.92x to 1.1-1.3x book.
- Below-book buybacks at a $300M+/quarter floor compound book value per share regardless of the cycle — a self-funding engine that creates value mechanically while the stock stays cheap.
- AIG capital release in H2 2026 unlocks an additional buyback augmentation, accelerating BVPS accretion.
- New CFO Elias Habayeb (ex-Corebridge, capital-return specialist) articulates a sharper return-of-capital framework that catalyzes a re-rating.
- Despite softer headline rates, terms/conditions and attachment points held at the April renewals — underwriting margins stay healthy rather than collapse.

## Bear Case

- **Reinsurance pricing cycle turns decisively soft** — property-cat down ~13% at April, Florida down mid-teens at 6/1, and capital is flooding back ("the great reinsurance thaw"), compressing the very margins the bull case relies on through 2027.
- **U.S. casualty reserve adequacy (the under-covered risk).** Headlines focus on cat pricing, but management's own commentary — "continue to maintain elevated loss picks," "build risk margin," citing "uncertainty in loss cost trends" — signals casualty is *not* fully resolved. A future adverse-development charge (as in 2023-24) would hit book value directly and break the sub-book thesis. This is the disclosed risk the news under-weights.
- A single major catastrophe year (the Q1 $130M cat load including $58M Iran is a reminder) can swamp a quarter's underwriting income.
- AIG transition restructuring (~$150M charges through 2026) plus the CFO transition create execution risk; Legacy guided above 110% combined ratio for 2026.
- The re-rating thesis partly depends on multiple expansion (0.92x → 1.1-1.3x book), which is sentiment-driven and may not happen while rates soften.

## Verdict

**WATCH (leaning BUY)** — Conviction: MEDIUM

The variant perception is clear and well-supported: the market is pricing the 2025 blended ROE and the softening-rates story, while the underlying franchise already earns a 16.7% operating ROE at an 87% reinsurance combined ratio and trades below book — with the reserve fear (the usual reinsurer-killer) explicitly tested clean this quarter. Quality is solid (18/25), the inflection is observable rather than hoped-for, and below-book buybacks provide a self-funding margin of safety. What holds it back from a full BUY is genuine timing/cycle risk: reinsurance pricing is decisively softening into 2027, and management's own elevated-loss-pick language flags that U.S. casualty reserves are not fully de-risked. Reliable models (GBM cluster) point to +10-40% upside; scenario EV is +18%.

Existing holders: hold; the below-book buyback and improving underlying ROE justify staying long, but resist adding at ~0.92x book just below the 52-week high until the second clean print or a better entry.
