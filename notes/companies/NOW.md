# ServiceNow, Inc. (NOW)

**Sector:** Technology | **Industry:** Software - Application
**Price:** $103.42 (2026-05-19, post-Dec-2025 5-for-1 split) | **Market Cap:** $107B
**Analysis Date:** 2026-05-19

## Business Primer

**What they do today.** ServiceNow sells the Now Platform, a workflow-automation cloud used mostly by very large enterprises and governments to digitize internal operating processes. The flagship use case is IT Service Management (ITSM) — the system employees ticket through when their laptop breaks, when they request software access, or when an incident takes down a production system — together with IT Operations Management (ITOM) for monitoring infrastructure. On top of that core, the platform sells employee workflows (Human Resources case management, onboarding), customer workflows (call-center case routing, field service), and a growing creator/low-code layer used to build custom internal apps. Customers are roughly 8,400 enterprises including most of the Global 2000, US federal agencies, and large state and local government bodies. Revenue is overwhelmingly subscription (Subscription revenue of $3.67B in Q1 2026 versus a few hundred million of professional services), priced historically per named user per year with multi-year contracts. About half of net new annual contract value (ACV) is now non-seat-based — token usage for the AI features, connectors for the data fabric — which is a deliberate shift in how the company sells.

**Where they want to grow.** Management has framed five hyper-growth wedges. First, monetize artificial intelligence agents on top of the existing seat base via Now Assist, the AI assistant bundled into a higher-priced Pro Plus tier and sold as consumption alongside seats. Now Assist's internal 2026 ACV target was just raised from $1.0B to $1.5B after one quarter, with customers spending more than $1M on Now Assist growing 130% year over year. Second, push into AI security by integrating Armis ($7.75B all-cash acquisition announced in 2025, closed in 2026) for agentless asset discovery across information technology (IT), operational technology (OT), Internet-of-Things (IoT), and medical devices, plus Veza for identity governance — selling a unified "see-decide-act" security operations stack. Third, take share in customer relationship management (CRM) with an AI-native CRM that grew net new ACV five-fold year over year. Fourth, scale EmployeeWorks, the human-resources case-and-experience layer, to 200M end employees. Fifth, sell Workflow Data Fabric, a federated data layer that lets large language models reason over enterprise data without copying it. Bill McDermott, the chief executive officer, has openly positioned the platform as "ERP for IT" — i.e., the system of record for how work gets done across the technology stack.

**What could go wrong (business risks).** The central business risk is also the central thesis: AI agents could compress the seat-based licensing that has historically driven roughly half of revenue growth. If a customer can automate ninety percent of help-desk tickets, they buy fewer seats — and the Pro Plus uplift plus token consumption needs to more than backfill that. ServiceNow itself has demonstrated this dynamic internally, resolving 90% of employee IT requests autonomously and capturing roughly $500M in productivity gains, which validates the technology but is exactly what makes the seller-of-AI-that-replaces-seats trade nervous. Other real risks: federal and US public-sector exposure is meaningful and sits inside an unpredictable Department of Government Efficiency (DOGE)-driven contracts environment plus continuing-resolution and shutdown noise; Armis integration adds $7.75B of goodwill plus the standard execution risk of merging a high-growth security business into a workflow company that historically grew organically; consolidation pressure from Microsoft (Copilot in Dynamics, the Power Platform), Salesforce, Atlassian, and pure-play AI agent vendors; and a macro enterprise IT slowdown that hit on-premise Middle East deals for 75 basis points of Q1 subscription revenue and could broaden.

**How to think about it.** ServiceNow is an enterprise workflow-automation incumbent monetizing AI agents on top of a deeply sticky platform — not a security company even after Armis, and not a pure AI play. The right frame is "system of record for enterprise work, now selling the AI layer that runs on top of itself," with the variant question being whether Pro Plus plus consumption plus new wedges (security, CRM, Workflow Data Fabric) outgrow seat compression, or whether AI re-rates the entire SaaS workflow category lower. Management's job is to prove the per-customer wallet keeps expanding even when seats stagnate; the market has not yet decided.

## Situation Summary

NOW reported a clean beat-and-raise Q1 2026 on April 22, 2026: subscription revenue $3.67B (+22% YoY, +19% constant currency), current remaining performance obligations (cRPO) of $12.64B (+22.5%), non-GAAP operating margin 32%, free-cash-flow margin 44%, and a $205M midpoint raise to full-year 2026 subscription revenue guidance ($15.735B–$15.775B). Now Assist's 2026 ACV target was raised 50% from $1.0B to $1.5B one quarter in. Despite this, the stock sold off ~17% post-print and is down roughly 50% from its 52-week high of $211 (post-split), part of a broader "AI eats SaaS" software derating that also hit IBM and other workflow vendors. Management quantified only one explicit headwind — 75 basis points drag from delayed Middle East on-premise deals — and the US public sector actually outperformed in Q1 with ten deals over $1M, contradicting the consensus federal-freeze fear. The Armis $7.75B acquisition closed in 2026 and adds ~150 basis points of operating-margin headwind that management says normalizes by 2027. Net renewal rate held at 97%. Next major catalyst: Financial Analyst Day in Las Vegas (May 2026), where management deferred detailed forward guidance — the market is waiting for it to back the agentic-AI ramp into a multi-year ARR trajectory.

## Variant Perception

- **Consensus view:** AI agents will compress the seat-based licensing model that drove the prior decade of ServiceNow growth; even with a beat-and-raise Q1, the stock deserves to derate because ARR cannot accelerate to AI-lab-comparable levels and the Armis deal piles security execution risk on top. Trade as "structurally challenged SaaS."
- **Our view:** The 50% non-seat-based mix of net new ACV already proves the hybrid token-plus-consumption monetization is working; Now Assist's $1.5B raised target one quarter in is the early evidence of the transition, not the late evidence. ServiceNow is the workflow incumbent that monetizes AI on top of its own platform, with 97% renewals and 130% growth in $1M+ Now Assist customers — the seat-compression bear case is already pricing a steeper deceleration than the disclosed unit economics support. The market is mistaking multiple compression for an earnings risk that the data does not yet show.
- **Trigger:** Financial Analyst Day (May 2026) with multi-year ARR / Now Assist disclosure, followed by Q2 2026 print (late July) sustaining the Now Assist ramp and showing operating-margin defense post-Armis close.

## Inflection Point

Yes — this is a re-rating setup, not a turnaround. The fundamental business is accelerating into AI monetization (Now Assist $1.5B raised target, 50% non-seat-based NNACV mix, 130% growth in $1M+ Now Assist customers) while the multiple has compressed from peak ~17x sales to ~7.6x sales — a roughly 55% derating. The observable evidence of the inflection has begun: Now Assist is past the experimentation phase ($1M+ customers more than doubling), Pro Plus is taking on the AI premium, and the post-print drawdown looks like overshoot. Catalyst path is dateable: Financial Analyst Day (May 2026) → Q2 print (late July 2026) → Armis integration milestones.

## Bull Case

- Now Assist exceeds the raised $1.5B 2026 ACV target and management discloses a multi-year ARR ramp at Financial Analyst Day, breaking the "AI compresses seats" bear narrative
- 50% non-seat NNACV mix continues to scale, proving consumption monetization more than backfills any seat compression
- Armis integration delivers cross-sell into the 8,400-customer base, adding meaningful security ACV in 2027 and proving the "unified see-decide-act" pitch
- Federal and public sector inflects positively as DOGE modernization budgets convert into new contracts (US public sector already outperformed in Q1 with 10 deals over $1M)
- Multiple recovers from ~7.6x sales toward the 10–12x sales range typical for >20% growing top-tier software, on top of continued 20%+ subscription growth

## Bear Case

- AI agents materially compress seat-based licensing across the customer base in 2027 — the productivity case ServiceNow itself proves internally (90% IT requests resolved autonomously) becomes the competitive case its customers use to renegotiate
- **Federal IT exposure deteriorates if DOGE-driven contract reviews or a fiscal-year-2026 shutdown stalls public-sector ACV** — this was NOT in the post-print headlines (they led with AI displacement); management did not quantify this risk, but the under-covered FY2026 appropriations / continuing-resolution overhang plus DOGE-era contract terminations is a real headwind that could surface in late 2026
- Armis $7.75B acquisition adds ~150 basis points of operating-margin headwind in 2026 with normalization "by 2027" — slip risk is real, and integrating a high-growth security business is unlike anything ServiceNow has done before
- Middle East on-premise deal delays (the only explicit headwind management quantified, 75 basis points in Q1) signal broader macro enterprise IT softness that could compound
- Multiple stays compressed if the market continues to treat workflow SaaS as structurally challenged by AI for 12+ months, regardless of fundamentals

## Verdict

**BUY** — Conviction: **MEDIUM**

The variant perception is clear: the data shows Now Assist monetization is accelerating (130% growth in $1M+ customers, 50% of NNACV non-seat-based, $1.5B target raised one quarter in) while the multiple has compressed as if seat compression were already happening. The model consensus from the most reliable GBM and autoresearch models clusters in the $145–$165 zone versus today's $103. The catalyst is dateable: Financial Analyst Day in May 2026 plus Q2 print in late July. Conviction is MEDIUM not HIGH because the seat-compression bear case is structurally hard to disprove ex-ante — only multiple quarters of sustained ARR growth at >20% on a $15B base will settle it, and Armis integration adds an execution swing factor.

**If BUY:** Entry favorable up to ~$115 (still meaningful margin to base case $140 and bull $180). Scale-in is rational given catalyst clustering — half on initial entry, half after Financial Analyst Day if the multi-year ARR disclosure confirms the thesis. Thesis-break trigger: $75 (below the 52-week low of $81) AND two consecutive quarters of sub-17% constant-currency subscription growth.
