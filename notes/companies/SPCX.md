## Business Primer

**What they do today.** SpaceX is two businesses bolted together. (1) **Launch** — the only operator flying a fully reusable orbital rocket fleet (Falcon 9, Falcon Heavy, and the in-ramp Starship), which has made it the dominant launch provider on Earth and the transport layer for nearly everyone else's satellites. (2) **Starlink** — a constellation of thousands of low-orbit satellites selling broadband internet directly to consumers, enterprises, maritime/aviation, and increasingly to phones (direct-to-cell). Starlink is the part that actually makes money: **$11.4B revenue in 2025 (61% of company total, rising to ~69% in Q1 2026), $4.4B operating profit, ~10.3M subscribers** — and it is the *only* consistently GAAP-profitable segment. The consolidated entity also now carries **xAI** (Musk's AI company), which is deeply lossmaking.

**Why customers choose them (the value proposition).** For launch: reusability makes SpaceX structurally cheaper and higher-cadence than any competitor (ULA, Arianespace, Blue Origin, the Chinese state programs), so if you need mass to orbit, they're the default. For Starlink: it delivers real broadband where cable/fiber/cellular don't reach — rural land, ships, planes, disaster zones, militaries — and is now signing flagship distribution (e.g. **American Airlines installing Starlink on 500+ narrow-body aircraft from early 2027**). The direct-to-cell roadmap (V2 Mobile sats from 2027) aims Starlink at the global mobile market, i.e. ordinary smartphones with no dish.

**Where they want to grow.** Three legs: (1) **Starlink Mobile** — broadband + IoT direct to phones, framed in the prospectus as a future wireless challenger. (2) **Starship** — if it reaches cheap, high-cadence heavy lift, it collapses the cost of putting *anything* in orbit and unlocks mega-constellation refreshes, lunar/Mars cargo, and (the long-tail bull case) orbital infrastructure. (3) **xAI / compute** — the consolidated AI bet, currently a cash furnace.

**What could go wrong (business risks).** (1) **Valuation** — the ask is ~94x 2025 revenue on a company posting multi-billion GAAP losses. (2) **Key-man / governance** — Musk holds **~42% equity and ~85% of voting power** via dual-class stock; public holders have almost no control and are exposed to his attention being split across Tesla, xAI, X, Neuralink, Boring, and politics. (3) **xAI drag** — ~$6B of 2025 losses and $2.5B burned in Q1 2026 sit inside the same entity you'd be buying. (4) **Starship execution** — the entire "trillions of upside" narrative leans on Starship working at scale and cost; it isn't proven yet. (5) **Regulatory / geopolitical** — spectrum, orbital-debris rules, export controls, and Musk's political entanglements are all live risks. (6) **Competition** — Amazon's Kuiper, Chinese state constellations, and terrestrial 5G all chip at Starlink's edge over time.

**How to think about it.** A genuinely dominant, irreplaceable launch monopoly wrapped around a fast-growing, profitable satellite-broadband business — priced at a multiple that already assumes flawless execution of several not-yet-proven moonshots, with a controlling shareholder and a lossmaking AI unit consolidated in. The question is never "is it a great company" (it is) — it's "what are you paying, and when."

## Situation Summary

SpaceX filed its **S-1 on 2026-05-20** and is targeting a **2026-06-12 Nasdaq listing as SPCX**, raising up to **$75B** at **$1.75T–$2T**. The headline tension is the **valuation jump**: the last private round (Dec 2025) was **~$800B**, so the IPO asks the public to pay **more than double in six months**. The financials underneath: **2025 revenue $18.7B (+33% YoY), GAAP net loss –$4.94B** (adjusted EBITDA +$6.6B), **Q1 2026 loss –$4.28B**, accumulated deficit $41.3B. Starlink is the profit engine ($11.4B rev, $4.4B op profit, 10.3M subs); xAI is the anchor (–$6B in 2025). At $1.75T that's **~94x 2025 revenue.**

## Variant Perception

- **Consensus view:** Two camps. Bulls: "irreplaceable launch monopoly + 33%-growing profitable Starlink + Starship optionality = generational compounder, pay up." Bears: "2x markup over the Dec round in six months, GAAP-lossmaking, 94x revenue, xAI cash furnace inside, Musk controls 85% of votes — this is priced for perfection into a lockup cliff." The widely-repeated meme is that SPCX will **pop briefly then fade** after listing.

- **Our view:** The "fade" thesis is *not* purely a self-fulfilling prophecy and *not* simply wrong — it rests on two concrete structural facts, not vibes. **(1) The pop is mechanically capped.** IPO pops come from *scarce float*; a **$75B raise is the largest supply event in IPO history** — there is no scarcity to squeeze. The only counterweight is Musk's retail army (the Tesla playbook), fighting a mountain of supply. So "brief pop, if any" is the base case for *mechanical* reasons, independent of crowd psychology. **(2) The re-rate risk is real and dateable.** Underwriters are asking the public to pay 2x the most recent private round, six months later, for a GAAP-lossmaking entity at 94x revenue — that's the classic setup for a post-IPO drift lower (cf. Aramco fading, Facebook ~-50% before recovering), and the **lockup expiry (~Sep–Dec 2026)** is the predictable supply wave when sub-$800B-cost early investors can finally sell into a 2x markup.

- **Trigger (near-term):** First public earnings print as a listed company, and the **lockup expiry window ~Sep–Dec 2026** (90–180 days post-list). Either could be the dip entry.

- **"Won't shorts pre-empt the dip and erase it?"** No — the mechanics run the other way. (1) **You can't short what isn't trading** — pre-IPO there's no borrow/locate, so none of the anticipated dip can be arbitraged forward before day one. (2) **Early borrow is scarce and expensive** — the float is held by allocated institutions and locked-up insiders, so "hard to borrow" fees throttle shorting *exactly* in the early window, which is part of why richly-priced IPOs hold up until the lockup. (3) **The dip is a *supply* event** (lockup unlock = billions of insider shares hitting the market), and shorting can only *borrow against* that future supply, not pre-consume it; anticipatory shorting tends to smear the decline into a drift-into-expiry rather than erase it — same or better for a patient buyer. **The genuine risk to the wait-for-dip plan is the *opposite*: a short squeeze to the upside.** SPCX has every ingredient — rabid retail base, a controlling holder (Musk) who can signal "not selling," and a constrained *available* float despite the huge nominal raise. If bears pile in early and insiders/retail don't sell, borrow gets recalled and shorts scramble to cover (the GME/Tesla dynamic). That melt-up — not arbitrage-to-fair-value — is the scenario where waiting costs you.

## Owner's Long-Term Thesis (the moonshot case)

*This is the bull-of-bulls scenario — the reason to own it at all on a 10–20yr horizon, held separately from the near-term trade.*

If Starship reaches cheap, indefinitely-scalable heavy lift, the compounding loop is: cheap launch → **AI datacenters in orbit** (unlimited solar power, radiative cooling, no land/water/grid constraints) → compute scales toward effectively unbounded → that intelligence designs and directs the next stage → **autonomous asteroid capture and resource extraction** (redirect bodies to lunar/Mars orbit, mine metals/volatiles), in-space manufacturing, and a self-reinforcing industrial base off-planet. At the limit, the binding constraint on the economy stops being capital and becomes physics/energy — abundance so extreme that *money itself loses meaning* as the scarcity it measures collapses. SpaceX is the only company with all three primitives in hand at once: the launch monopoly, the orbital-constellation operating experience, and (via xAI) the compute/AI arm. **No claim this is priced, probable, or near** — it's the fat right tail, and it's the structural reason the long-term conviction is "super bullish" even while the near-term posture is "wait for the dip."

## Bull Case

- Launch monopoly is durable for years; Starship lowers cost-to-orbit by another order of magnitude and unlocks constellation refresh + new markets.
- Starlink keeps compounding 30%+ — direct-to-cell (V2 Mobile, 2027) opens the global mobile TAM; flagship distribution (American Airlines 500+ aircraft, maritime, enterprise, defense) validates the platform.
- The $75B war chest funds the next decade of capex without dilution pressure.
- xAI losses eventually convert into a real compute/AI franchise rather than pure burn.
- Musk's retail following sustains a premium multiple far longer than fundamentals justify (the Tesla precedent).
- **Tail (owner's thesis):** orbital compute → off-planet industrial base → post-scarcity. Unpriced, improbable-near-term, enormous if real.

## Bear Case

- **You're paying 2x the Dec-2025 round, six months later, at 94x revenue, for a GAAP-lossmaking entity** — the margin of safety is negative on day one.
- **Mechanical pop ceiling:** $75B of fresh supply is the opposite of a scarce float; the classic first-day moonshot is structurally unlikely, so buying the open risks buying the top.
- **Lockup cliff ~Sep–Dec 2026:** early investors at sub-$800B cost can finally sell into a 2x markup — the most predictable supply wave on the calendar.
- **xAI is a consolidated cash furnace** (–$6B/yr) dragging the blended P&L; its losses are *your* losses as a holder.
- **Governance:** 85% Musk voting control means zero minority-holder leverage; capital allocation, related-party dealings (xAI/X/Tesla), and strategy are entirely at his discretion.
- **Key-man + distraction risk:** Musk's attention and political entanglements are a live, unhedgeable variable.
- **Competition** (Kuiper, Chinese constellations, terrestrial 5G) compresses Starlink's edge over a 5–10yr window.

## Verdict

**WAIT FOR DIP** — Long-term conviction: **HIGH / super-bullish**. Near-term entry posture: **patient.**

The business quality is not in question — this is the widest moat in the universe with a profitable, fast-growing Starlink engine and a fat-tailed moonshot optionality the owner rates as civilization-altering. But three structural facts make day-one buying the low-expected-value move: **(1)** retail can't get the offer price; you'd buy the open after whatever pop exists; **(2)** a $75B raise mechanically caps the pop — there's no scarce float to squeeze; **(3)** the price is 2x the Dec-2025 round at 94x revenue on a GAAP-lossmaking entity, heading into a predictable **Sep–Dec 2026 lockup supply wave.** The asymmetric play is to let the lockup-driven volatility do the work and accumulate on weakness, sizing small enough that a Musk-driven melt-up can't hurt and a re-rate can't wreck.

**Plan:**
- **Skip the IPO day and the first few weeks.** Watch where it settles after the first public earnings print.
- **Primary entry window: ~Sep–Dec 2026 lockup expiry**, on the supply-driven weakness.
- **Start small, scale in thirds** on confirmed weakness or on Starship/Starlink milestone proof — not on hype.
- **Pre-commit a hard max position size** so missing a melt-up can't cause FOMO and a dip can't force a panic.

**Would accelerate entry if:** SPCX trades *below* the IPO offer price with no fundamental deterioration; OR a Starship cost/cadence milestone materially de-risks the long-term thesis; OR xAI is structurally separated from the consolidated P&L (removes the blended-loss drag).

**Thesis-break / avoid signals:** Musk materially disengages from SpaceX operations; OR Starlink subscriber growth stalls below ~15% with no direct-to-cell traction; OR Starship program suffers a multi-year setback; OR governance actions visibly extract value from minority public holders (related-party transfers to xAI/X/Tesla on non-arm's-length terms).
