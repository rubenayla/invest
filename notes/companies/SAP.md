## Business Primer

**What they do today.** SAP sells the software that large organizations use to run the back-office plumbing of their business — the system that books every sale, pays every supplier, schedules every factory shift, closes the monthly accounts, and tracks every employee record. The flagship product is **S/4HANA**, an Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) suite — meaning a single integrated database and application stack that handles accounting, supply chain, procurement, manufacturing, sales orders, inventory, and reporting for one company across all its countries and legal entities. Around the ERP core, SAP sells specialized modules: **SuccessFactors** for human resources (payroll, performance reviews, recruiting), **Ariba** for supplier negotiations and procurement, **Concur** for travel and expense reimbursement, and **Business Technology Platform (BTP)** which is where customers build their own extensions, where SAP's data fabric **DataSphere** lives, and where the AI assistant **Joule** runs. Roughly two-thirds of revenue is now cloud subscriptions (€26B target for 2026), with the rest being legacy on-premise software support contracts (declining as customers migrate) and shrinking new-license sales. The customer base is concentrated in large enterprises — about 75% of the Forbes Global 2000 run SAP, and a typical SAP customer is a multinational bank, pharmaceutical company, automaker, or consumer goods firm with thousands of users and processes that have been customized over 10-30 years.

**Why customers choose them (the value proposition).** A Fortune 500 manufacturer running SAP keeps running SAP because ripping it out would mean rebuilding the entire mechanical operation of the company from scratch. Concretely: when a customer in Singapore places an order in Salesforce, that order has to flow into the ERP, reserve inventory at a specific warehouse, trigger a production run, debit the right currency-denominated account, pay GST/VAT under the right tax regime, generate a compliant invoice, and feed into the consolidated quarterly report under International Financial Reporting Standards — all in seconds, across maybe 80 countries with different tax codes and labor laws. SAP has spent 50 years pre-building this plumbing for every industry (chemicals, automotive, banking, utilities, pharma) and certifying it with regulators — FDA validation for drug manufacturers, GxP compliance for clinical trials, Sarbanes-Oxley audit trails for US filers, GDPR data residency for European personal data. The alternative is either Oracle (similar incumbent, similar lock-in, similar cost) or stitching together 15-20 best-of-breed apps (Workday for HR, NetSuite for finance, Coupa for procurement, Snowflake for data, custom code for the rest), which works for a 500-person software startup but breaks down at 50,000 employees in 60 countries because nothing reconciles to a single source of truth. The concrete benefit a customer gets: one general ledger that closes the books in 5 days instead of 25, one inventory number that supply chain and finance both agree on, one HR record per employee across every country, and audit trails that satisfy regulators in a single inspection rather than 40. Switching costs are measured in years and hundreds of millions of dollars per customer — a typical S/4HANA migration takes 18-36 months and costs $50-500M depending on complexity, which is also why customers, once on, stay 20-40 years.

**Where they want to grow.** Three explicit bets. First, **forced migration of the installed base from on-premise ECC to cloud RISE/GROW** — SAP's previous-generation ERP (ECC 6.0) loses mainstream maintenance on 31 December 2027 with paid extensions through 2030 (and a special private-cloud option to 2033 for the most complex customers). That deadline is dragging roughly 30,000 ECC customers into either S/4HANA Cloud Private Edition (RISE, hosted by SAP on AWS/Azure/Google Cloud) or the multi-tenant public edition (GROW). This is the single biggest revenue engine — every migration converts a low-priced support contract into a 2-5x larger cloud subscription. Second, **AI monetization via Joule**, the embedded copilot that lets a procurement clerk type "find me three alternative suppliers for this component with lower lead times" instead of clicking through 40 screens; SAP is selling Joule agents on a consumption basis on top of seat licenses, and Q1 2026 reported a 9x year-over-year increase in customers using it. Third, **Business Technology Platform plus DataSphere** to keep the customer's analytics and AI workloads running on SAP-controlled infrastructure rather than getting siphoned off to Snowflake or Databricks. Success in 3-5 years looks like cloud revenue at €40B+, operating margins at 32-35%, and Joule contributing measurable per-seat uplift rather than just being a free feature.

**What could go wrong (business risks).** The migration window is the risk and the opportunity at the same time — every customer renegotiating their contract is a chance to lose them. **Workday** is winning HR mandates that used to default to SuccessFactors; **Salesforce** has eroded the front-office side; **ServiceNow** is creeping into workflow automation that touches ERP territory. Hyperscalers (AWS, Microsoft, Google) host RISE but are also building their own composable enterprise stacks with AI agents that could let new entrants assemble a "good enough" ERP without SAP at the center. The Q1 2026 disclosure that customers are shifting toward larger, slower-ramping deals and sovereign-cloud configurations driven by geopolitical tension (EU customers wanting EU-only data residency) means revenue is being pushed right on the calendar even when bookings are healthy. Software support revenue — the high-margin legacy annuity — is now in faster decline as customers leave ECC, which depresses near-term growth optics even though it's a healthy outcome. Execution risk on Joule is real: if the AI copilot turns out to be a checkbox feature that doesn't move productivity needles, the multiple expansion thesis dies. And the €408M Q1 litigation settlement is a reminder that SAP carries antitrust and IP exposure from a sprawling product portfolio.

**How to think about it.** Mission-critical enterprise infrastructure with a contractually mandated upgrade cycle — closer to a regulated utility than a discretionary software vendor — currently being repriced as a slowing SaaS name during a broader AI-disruption derate.

## Situation Summary

SAP reported Q1 2026 on 23 April: cloud revenue +27% constant currency, Cloud ERP Suite +30%, operating profit +24% cc, margin 30% (+2.9pp). On the surface excellent — but the stock has dropped from a 2025 high of $313 to $178 (-43%), most of it after the January FY2026 outlook that signaled current cloud backlog growth would *decelerate* from 25% in 2025 and that total revenue growth would "remain at similar levels as in 2025" rather than accelerate as previously implied. Management cited customers shifting to larger projects with longer ramps and rising sovereign-cloud demand (geopolitical-driven) as the reasons. Q2 2026 cloud revenue growth is explicitly guided to decelerate because Q1 had quarter-specific tailwinds. Software support revenue dropped 11% reported (-6% cc) and management warned the decline will accelerate as ECC customers migrate ahead of the December 2027 deadline. The narrative has flipped from "AI-accelerated SaaS compounder" to "decelerating legacy in the middle of a painful transition" — even though absolute cloud growth is still 25%+ and operating profit is compounding double-digits.

## Variant Perception

- **Consensus view:** Cloud growth has peaked; SAP is a slowing SaaS company caught between AI disruption (potential agentic-AI replacements for ERP modules) and the messy ECC-to-cloud transition. The de-rating from ~38x to ~24x forward earnings is justified by decelerating backlog. Sovereign-cloud demand is a margin headwind. Joule is a feature, not a revenue line.
- **Our view:** The market is conflating *short-term optics* (Q2 cloud decel, support revenue decline) with *structural deterioration*. The ECC sunset (mainstream 2027, extended 2030, edge cases 2033) is a contractually anchored multi-year migration tailwind — 30,000+ customers must move and every migration is a 2-5x revenue uplift per account. Operating margin expanded 2.9pp in Q1 with cloud gross margin holding at 75%. Joule is monetized on consumption *on top of* seat licenses, so it's net-additive even if conversion is gradual. At ~19x forward earnings for a 30% operating margin business with a regulated upgrade cycle and €10B FCF target, the multiple is closer to a slow-growth industrial than infrastructure software.
- **Trigger:** Q2 2026 earnings (late July) — if cloud growth decelerates but to a still-strong 22-24% cc and FY guidance is held or raised, the "structural slowdown" narrative breaks. Concurrent triggers: Joule paid-tier disclosure at SAP Sapphire (annual conference, May/June each year), and any incremental ECC migration win announcements.

## Inflection Point

Yes — **forced ECC migration deadline is the inflection**. Mainstream maintenance ends 31 December 2027; extended maintenance 2030; private-cloud edge cases 2033. Approximately 30,000 ECC customers must migrate, and each migration converts a low-priced support contract into a 2-5x larger cloud subscription. The market is currently fixated on the *deceleration rate* of cloud growth from 27% to ~22-24%, missing that the migration window itself has 4+ years of structural runway. ## Bull Case

- ECC migration is contractually forced; ~30,000 customers must convert by 2027-2030 with 2-5x revenue uplift per migration, locking in cloud growth through ~2029
- Joule monetization on consumption basis (separately from seat licenses) materializes as a 5-10% incremental revenue layer; 9x YoY customer growth already disclosed
- Q1 operating margin +2.9pp shows the cloud transition is now self-funding; €10B FCF target for 2026 is roughly +20% YoY, and FCF compounded 91% over 2022-2025
- At ~19x forward earnings for a 30% operating margin business with the highest switching costs in enterprise software, the rerate-to-25x scenario alone is +30%
- Sovereign cloud demand (EU data residency, geopolitical hedge) actually *favors* SAP over US hyperscaler-native alternatives — pricing power on configurable hosting

## Bear Case

- Q2 2026 cloud growth will decelerate explicitly per management — if the decel goes from 27% to under 20%, the "still-strong growth" thesis breaks
- Software support revenue declining 11% (will accelerate per guidance) compresses near-term total revenue optics even as cloud accelerates — a "valley of death" optical period
- **(Under-covered in news)** €408M Q1 litigation settlement hit cash flow and reflects ongoing antitrust/IP exposure that the news cycle has largely ignored; further settlements could pressure FCF guidance
- Workday continues winning HR mandates that should renew on SuccessFactors; Salesforce/ServiceNow erode adjacent workflow real estate; agentic-AI startups could assemble "good-enough" ERP without SAP at the center, eroding the new-logo pipeline (existing base remains sticky but TAM shrinks)
- Customer shift to "larger, slower-ramping projects" + sovereign-cloud preferences pushes revenue right on the calendar — could cause 2-3 quarters of disappointing prints before backlog converts
