**Sector:** Utilities | **Industry:** Utilities - Regulated Electric

## Situation Summary

AEP is one of the largest regulated utilities in the US, serving 5.6M customers across 11 states. The stock is riding a powerful secular tailwind: AI-driven data center demand is transforming load growth for utilities after decades of flat demand. AEP doubled its contracted large-load pipeline to 56 GW (mostly data centers) and unveiled a $72B+ capex plan through 2030, guiding for 7-9% long-term EPS growth. The stock has rallied ~29% off its 52-week low of $97.46 but remains below the analyst mean target of $137.47. Key near-term regulatory risk centers on West Virginia rate cases and the Arkansas $114M rate case outcome.

## Variant Perception

- **Consensus view:** AEP is a solid data center beneficiary utility with above-average EPS growth (7-9%), trading roughly at fair value (~19x forward). Analysts rate it a Buy with a mean target of $137 — about 9% upside. The market sees it as a "steady compounder" with a known data center story already priced in.

- **Our view:** The consensus may be UNDERESTIMATING the magnitude of load growth. A contracted pipeline of 56 GW and projections of 24 GW of new load by 2030 are unprecedented for a regulated utility. AEP's transmission-heavy asset base gives it a structural advantage — transmission earns regulated returns with formula rates (minimal regulatory lag) and the $4.2B Ohio transmission investment is just the beginning. However, the negative FCF profile (-$1.6B in 2025) and 5.3x net debt/EBITDA mean equity dilution is coming, which caps upside. The variant perception is WEAK — this is a well-covered consensus long.

- **Trigger:** Q1 2026 earnings on May 5, 2026 — management expected to quantify financing for the $5-8B incremental project pipeline. Also: West Virginia rate case ruling (hearing mid-June) and April 28 annual meeting vote on expanding authorized shares.

## Inflection Point

**Moderate inflection — demand-driven.** AEP is at the beginning of a structural demand increase from data centers. After decades of flat utility load growth (~0.5% annually), AEP now projects 24 GW of new load by 2030, mostly from data centers. This is a generational shift for the utility sector.

However, this is NOT a hidden story — every utility analyst is talking about data center demand. The inflection is real but already recognized by the market. The question is whether execution will EXCEED the already elevated expectations.

Evidence the inflection has begun: 4.7 GW of new data center capacity brought online in 2025, Bloom Energy fuel cell partnership for 1 GW, $4.2B Ohio transmission investment announced.

## Bull Case

- Data center demand accelerates beyond 56 GW pipeline — hyperscalers (MSFT, AMZN, GOOG, META) are in an AI capex arms race and AEP serves key geographies (Ohio, Virginia data center corridor)
- Transmission capex earns attractive regulated returns with minimal regulatory lag via formula rates — AEP's transmission-heavy mix is a structural advantage
- Rate case outcomes exceed expectations (Arkansas $114M, West Virginia $250M) — strong ROE approvals support earnings growth above 9%
- Dividend growth of 6-7% annually with 59% payout ratio provides safe, growing income stream
- Beta of 0.57 provides portfolio ballast in a recession — defensive with growth characteristics

## Bear Case

- Equity dilution is coming — negative FCF of -$1.6B and $72B capex plan REQUIRES significant capital markets access. The April 28 share authorization vote signals intent. EPS growth could be diluted by 1-2% from equity issuance
- West Virginia regulatory drag — APCo reconsideration filing unresolved, negative ruling could compress ROE below 9.5% target
- Interest rate sensitivity — at 5.3x net debt/EBITDA, rising rates directly pressure earnings. Each 50bps rate increase materially impacts refinancing costs
- Data center demand could decelerate — hyperscaler capex plans are volatile, AI efficiency gains (model distillation) could reduce power needs, or customers could self-generate with on-site nuclear/solar
- Wildfire/weather liability risk — regulated utilities face increasing climate-related costs (see PG&E, Hawaiian Electric)

## Verdict
