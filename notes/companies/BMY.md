# Bristol-Myers Squibb (BMY)

**Sector:** Healthcare | **Industry:** Drug Manufacturers - General
**Price:** $58.32 (2026-05-19) | **Market Cap:** $119.1B
**Analysis Date:** 2026-05-19

## Business Primer

**What they do today.** Bristol-Myers Squibb is a large global pharmaceutical company that develops and sells prescription medicines for serious diseases — primarily cancer, blood clots, immune-system disorders, and (increasingly) psychiatric and cardiovascular conditions. Doctors prescribe the drugs, patients take them, and insurance companies, government health programs (Medicare, Medicaid), and patients themselves pay for them. The portfolio splits roughly into two halves. The **Legacy Portfolio** (~$5.1B/quarter, shrinking ~15%) is the older blockbusters approaching the end of their patent protection: **Eliquis**, a daily oral blood thinner (apixaban) co-marketed with Pfizer; **Revlimid**, a blood-cancer pill that lost exclusivity in 2022 and is now in steep decline; and various older immunology and cardiovascular drugs. The **Growth Portfolio** (~$7.4B/quarter, growing ~16%) is the newer launches: **Opdivo** (nivolumab) cancer immunotherapy, **Orencia** for rheumatoid arthritis, **Reblozyl** for anemia, **Camzyos** for heart-muscle disease, **Breyanzi** CAR-T cell therapy, **Opdualag** combination cancer immunotherapy, and the newest bet, **Cobenfy** for schizophrenia. Full-year 2026 revenue is guided to $46.0–$47.5B, with management saying they are tracking toward the upper end.

**Why customers (patients and doctors) choose them — the value proposition, drug by drug.** **Eliquis** is the doctor's default prescription for a patient with atrial fibrillation (irregular heartbeat) at risk of stroke. The older alternative, warfarin, works fine but requires monthly blood draws to keep the dose in a narrow safe window, banned foods (leafy greens change the blood level), and frequent dose adjustments — a logistical nightmare for elderly patients. Eliquis is a fixed twice-a-day pill, no monitoring, no food restrictions, and clinical trials showed *fewer* major bleeds than warfarin at equal or better stroke protection. That is why ~80% of new anticoagulant prescriptions in atrial fibrillation now go to direct oral anticoagulants like Eliquis. **Opdivo** is a PD-1 inhibitor — an antibody infused into the blood that blocks a "stop signal" cancer cells use to hide from the immune system. Once that brake is released, the patient's own T-cells recognize the tumor as foreign and kill it. Before PD-1 drugs, metastatic melanoma was essentially a death sentence; today a meaningful fraction of patients are alive and tumor-free five years later. Opdivo is approved across more than ten cancers (melanoma, lung, kidney, head/neck, bladder, esophageal, liver, etc.) and is standard-of-care alongside Merck's Keytruda. **Cobenfy** is the first genuinely new mechanism for schizophrenia in 70 years: instead of blocking dopamine receptors (which every other antipsychotic does, causing weight gain, diabetes, sedation, and tardive dyskinesia — involuntary face and body movements that can become permanent), it activates muscarinic acetylcholine receptors, indirectly modulating brain circuits. Trials showed comparable symptom control without the weight gain or movement side effects, which is the entire reason ~50% of schizophrenia patients stop taking their medication. **Reblozyl** lets myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS) patients with anemia avoid weekly blood transfusions. **Camzyos** mechanically shrinks the over-thickened heart muscle in obstructive hypertrophic cardiomyopathy — patients who could barely climb stairs return to normal activity, replacing open-heart surgery for many of them. **Breyanzi** is a CAR-T cell therapy: a patient's own T-cells are extracted, genetically engineered to recognize a cancer marker (CD19), expanded, and infused back — a one-shot treatment that has driven long remissions in lymphoma and leukemia patients who had failed everything else. In every case, the value proposition is "this works where the older option failed, or works with fewer side effects so patients actually keep taking it."

**Where they want to grow.** The whole company is reorganized around a single problem: **the patent cliff**. Eliquis loses U.S. exclusivity in 2028 and Opdivo follows in 2028–2029. Together these two drugs are roughly half of revenue and a larger share of profit. Management's strategy has three pillars. First, **stretch the legacy** — get a subcutaneous (injection under the skin, takes minutes vs. an hour-long IV infusion) version of Opdivo approved to differentiate vs. biosimilars after patent loss, and similar lifecycle moves. Second, **scale the growth portfolio** — get Reblozyl, Camzyos, Breyanzi, Opdualag, and Cobenfy each above $1B/year and ideally $3–5B/year, so the combined growth portfolio (~$30B run-rate target by 2030) offsets the legacy decline. Third, **bet big on Cobenfy** as the single largest replacement: $14B paid for Karuna Therapeutics in 2024 to acquire it, with peak sales potential analysts put at $5–10B if it works across schizophrenia, Alzheimer's-related psychosis (ADEPT-2 readout late 2026), bipolar mania, and autism irritability. Capital allocation: the $10B post-Karuna debt paydown was completed ahead of schedule in 2025, dividend was raised for the 17th consecutive year to $2.52 annualized (~4.3% yield), and ~$5B in buyback authorization remains.

**What could go wrong — the business risks.** First, **the patent cliff math may simply not work**. Generic Eliquis in 2028 typically takes 70–90% of revenue within 12–18 months, and that is $13B+ of high-margin revenue gone. If the growth portfolio is not yet scaled enough to fill the hole, 2028–2030 revenue could be flat or shrinking even on management's plan. Second, **Cobenfy is no longer a slam dunk**. The ARISE trial (Cobenfy added on top of existing antipsychotics) *failed* in 2025, taking out one of the larger commercial opportunities; the ADEPT-2 trial for Alzheimer's psychosis was extended in early 2026 after "data irregularities" were found and the readout pushed out, denting confidence. Real-world Cobenfy uptake has been steady but unspectacular ($163M in Q1 2026, on track for ~$650M annualized — short of the original launch trajectory). If Cobenfy peaks at $2–3B instead of $5–10B, the entire $14B Karuna acquisition becomes a value-destructive bet and the cliff replacement story falls apart. Third, **the Inflation Reduction Act keeps biting**. Eliquis is already on the first Medicare negotiation list, with a "Maximum Fair Price" of $231/month from January 2026 (down from $521 list — a 56% cut). Opdivo is likely to follow on a future negotiation list. Fourth, **biosimilar Opdivo** is coming alongside the patent expiry; Merck's Keytruda also faces a patent cliff but has been gaining share, and the subcutaneous Opdivo gambit may not differentiate enough. Fifth, **continued large IO portfolio dependence** — if a competitor's next-generation immunotherapy (CTLA-4, LAG-3, TIGIT, bispecifics) leapfrogs PD-1, Opdivo's tail revenue post-LOE could be smaller than modeled.

**How to think about it.** A classic "big-pharma patent-cliff trough" setup: deeply discounted (forward P/E 9.5, dividend yield 4.4%) because the market sees a 2028–2030 revenue cliff and is rationally skeptical that Cobenfy + the rest of the growth portfolio can plug the $13B+ hole. The shareholder return case is built on dividend + buyback + the option value of Cobenfy and the growth portfolio executing, not on multiple expansion. Variant perception only exists if you think the cliff is more priced-in than the actual cash flows justify, or if you have a contrarian view on a specific pipeline catalyst.

## Situation Summary

BMS reported Q1 2026 revenue of $11.49B (up 2.6% YoY), beating consensus of $10.88B, with Eliquis +13% (boosted by a one-time U.S. wholesaler inventory build ahead of the January 2026 IRA price reset — this reverses next quarter), Opdivo -8% (wholesaler drawdown), and the growth portfolio +16% to $7.4B. Management reaffirmed 2026 guidance of $46.0–$47.5B revenue and $6.05–$6.35 non-GAAP EPS, saying performance is tracking toward the upper end of both ranges. The legacy portfolio is guided to decline 12–16% in 2026. Cobenfy printed $163M for the quarter (annualizing ~$650M); the ARISE adjunctive-schizophrenia trial failed in 2025, and the ADEPT-2 Alzheimer's psychosis readout was pushed to late 2026 after data irregularities surfaced. The $10B post-Karuna debt-paydown commitment was completed ahead of schedule in 2025, and the dividend was raised for the 17th consecutive year (annual rate $2.52, yield ~4.3%). Stock has rebounded from the $42 lows in early 2025 but still trades near 9.5x forward earnings.

## Variant Perception

- **Consensus view:** Patent cliff is real and largely unavoidable; Cobenfy is the marquee replacement but launch is unspectacular and the ARISE failure + ADEPT-2 delay have dented blockbuster expectations. Stock is a low-growth, high-yield "value trap candidate" that you collect a 4%+ dividend on while waiting to see whether the growth portfolio can offset the cliff. 25 analysts cover, consensus "Hold," mean target $62.96 (~8% upside).
- **Our view:** At 9.5x forward earnings and a 4.3% dividend, the market is pricing in a meaningful revenue/EPS decline through 2028–2030. The actual numbers may be less catastrophic: (a) Eliquis IRA negotiation impact is already in 2026 guidance, (b) subcutaneous Opdivo can preserve a meaningful share post-LOE, (c) the growth portfolio is real and compounding (Camzyos, Reblozyl, Opdualag, Breyanzi all crossed $1B annualized in 2025), (d) management has actually been buying back stock and paying down debt rather than chasing more $14B acquisitions. If 2026 EPS lands at $6.35 (top of guide) and trough EPS in 2029 is ~$5.50 (not $4 as bears fear), the stock at $58 is paying ~10% earnings yield through the cliff. The variant view: this is a "trough multiple on trough earnings" setup where the cliff is in the price, and you are essentially being paid a 4.3% dividend to own the option on Cobenfy's pipeline (ADEPT-2 H2 2026, bipolar, autism, Alzheimer's cognition).
- **Trigger:** ADEPT-2 Alzheimer's psychosis Phase III readout (H2 2026) is the single biggest dateable catalyst — a positive readout opens a $3–5B peak-sales indication and reframes Cobenfy from "stumbling launch" to "platform drug." A negative readout largely confirms the bear case but is partly priced in.

## Inflection Point

Sort of, in two ways. (1) **Cobenfy commercial trajectory** — the launch is mid-curve, not failing but not blockbuster, and the next 4–8 quarters tell us whether $5–10B peak is real or whether $2–3B is the ceiling. (2) **The 2028 cliff transition** — BMS is in the awkward "approaching cliff" period where the market discounts cash flows aggressively but the actual erosion hasn't started yet. The classical pharma-cliff playbook says the stock often bottoms 12–18 months *before* the LOE and recovers as post-cliff base earnings become visible. We are roughly at that pre-cliff trough now. **Observable evidence of a turn would be**: (a) 2027 Eliquis revenue holding above $13B (i.e., IRA impact already absorbed), (b) ADEPT-2 positive readout in H2 2026, (c) Cobenfy run-rate crossing $1B/year by Q4 2026, (d) growth portfolio crossing $32B annualized.

## Bull Case

- **Cliff is in the price.** Forward P/E 9.5 and 4.3% dividend yield bake in a sharper revenue decline than is likely to actually occur if the growth portfolio keeps compounding at 15–20%.
- **Growth portfolio is real.** Reblozyl, Camzyos, Opdualag, Breyanzi all crossed $1B annualized in 2025; Cobenfy at $650M run-rate and ramping. The $30B+ growth portfolio target by 2030 doesn't require heroics — just continued execution.
- **ADEPT-2 readout (H2 2026)** is a free call option. Positive = Cobenfy peak sales reset from $3B → $7B+; expands TAM into Alzheimer's psychosis, bipolar, autism. Negative = mostly priced in.
- **Capital return.** 4.3% dividend yield (17 consecutive raises), ~$5B buyback authorization remaining, $10B debt paydown done ahead of schedule. You get paid to wait.
- **Subcutaneous Opdivo** can preserve a meaningful share post-LOE — sub-Q administration takes minutes vs. an hour, a real workflow advantage that biosimilar IV nivolumab can't match.

## Bear Case

- **Cliff math is brutal.** Eliquis alone is ~$13B revenue; even with the IRA negotiation cut already in 2026 numbers, generic entry in 2028 typically removes 80%+ of remaining revenue within 18 months. If growth portfolio can't fill the hole, EPS could decline 25–35% peak-to-trough.
- **Cobenfy is no longer a slam-dunk.** The ARISE failure took out the adjunctive opportunity, and the ADEPT-2 trial was extended for "data irregularities" in early 2026. Real-world launch is steady but unspectacular. Karuna's $14B price tag looks like it might not be earned back.
- **IRA negotiation expands** — Eliquis already on list 1 (56% list-price cut); Opdivo is a high-probability candidate for a future round, and Pomalyst/Revlimid follow-ons are at risk. Drug pricing pressure is structural, not cyclical.
- **(Risk under-covered by news headlines)** Higher U.S. government channel rebates were called out in Q1 2026 as part of the legacy portfolio's 15% decline — not just patent expiry. This is the **Medicaid inflation rebate + IRA combination** quietly compressing margin on the entire legacy book, beyond what generic competition alone explains. The headlines focus on the 2028 patent dates; the actual margin pressure is already happening today.
- **Pipeline gap beyond Cobenfy.** No obvious second $5B+ asset in the pipeline. If the lifecycle bets (Opdivo subQ, Sotyktu in additional indications, Cendakimab) don't pan out, the early-2030s look thin.

## Verdict

**WATCH** — Conviction: MEDIUM

The risk/reward at $58 is reasonable but not exceptional: ~+11.5% expected value plus a 4.3% dividend over a multi-year horizon, with a meaningful 25% downside scenario. The variant-perception edge ("cliff is more priced-in than the cash flows justify") is real but modest, and the easy contrarian entry was at $42 in early 2025. The ADEPT-2 readout in H2 2026 is the asymmetric event — positive = significant re-rating, negative = mostly priced in. Existing holders: hold through the readout, collect the dividend. New buyers: smaller scale-in OK at $58; better entry on either a pullback toward $50–52 (Cobenfy disappointment, IRA news flow) or a clean ADEPT-2 positive readout where you pay up for confirmed cliff replacement.

**Would upgrade to BUY at:** $50–52 absent further fundamental deterioration (gives ~+30% EV with the same dividend), OR at $58–62 on a confirmed positive ADEPT-2 readout (Cobenfy peak sales reset).

**Would downgrade to PASS if:** ADEPT-2 fails AND Cobenfy Q4 2026 run-rate stays below $900M annualized.
