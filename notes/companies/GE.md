# GE Aerospace (GE)

**Sector:** Industrials | **Industry:** Aerospace & Defense
**Price:** $315.65 (2026-05-28) | **Market Cap:** $330B
**Analysis Date:** 2026-05-28

## Business Primer

**What they do today.** GE Aerospace designs, builds, and services the jet engines that power most of the world's airliners and a large share of military aircraft. Revenue splits into two segments. The big one is **Commercial Engines & Services (CES, ~75% of revenue, $8.9B in Q1 2026)**: GE and its 50/50 joint venture with France's Safran (called CFM International) make the engines bolted under the wings of Boeing 737s and Airbus A320s — the LEAP engine — plus larger engines like the GE9X on the Boeing 777X and the GEnx on the 787. Airlines and aircraft leasing companies pay for the engines, but the real money comes afterward: every time one of those engines comes off the wing for a mandatory overhaul ("shop visit"), GE sells the spare parts and does the labor at high margins. The second segment is **Defense & Propulsion Technologies (DPT, ~25%, $3.2B in Q1 2026)**: military engines like the F110 (fighters), T700/T901 (helicopters), and propulsion R&D for the U.S. and allied governments.

**Why customers choose them (the value proposition).** An airline buying a narrowbody jet does not really get to pick its engine the way a car buyer picks a trim — the engine family is locked to the airframe program, and switching means re-certifying everything, retraining mechanics, and rebuilding a parts inventory. So the choice is made once, at the platform level, and then it sticks for the 25-30 year life of the aircraft. GE wins that platform slot because CFM's LEAP burns roughly 15-20% less fuel than the previous-generation CFM56 it replaced — on a jet that flies thousands of hours a year, fuel is the single biggest operating cost, so a 15% fuel saving is enormous money. Once the engine is flying, the airline is effectively a captive customer for parts and overhauls: only GE-approved parts keep the warranty and the airworthiness certificate valid, so the aftermarket is a recurring, high-margin annuity that the airline cannot shop around for. That captive-aftermarket dynamic is why services revenue (+39% in Q1) is the profit engine, not new-engine sales.

**Where they want to grow.** The core bet is the **aftermarket ramp**: the installed base of LEAP engines is projected to roughly triple between 2024 and 2030, and each engine eventually enters a multi-decade cycle of overhauls. As the fleet matures, high-margin services revenue compounds for years with little new capital. Management is also pouring money into capacity ($1B U.S. manufacturing investment, ~5,000 jobs) to lift LEAP output and shorten MRO turnaround times, and pushing defense (T901 helicopter engine, next-gen adaptive-cycle propulsion). The 2030s bet is **RISE** — an open-fan next-generation engine targeting another ~20% fuel-burn reduction — to defend the platform slot into the next aircraft cycle. Success in 3-5 years looks like 17%+ "normalized" earnings growth on a backlog that gives multi-year visibility.

**What could go wrong (business risks).** Backlog is worthless if you cannot deliver — the binding constraint is the supply chain (titanium, specialized castings, skilled labor), and any sustained LEAP delivery slippage caps revenue regardless of orders. A demand shock to air travel is the other big one: GE's services revenue scales with how many hours the global fleet flies, so anything that grounds capacity (recession, fuel spike, geopolitical disruption of major air corridors) directly slows the aftermarket annuity. Defense budgets and program wins/losses swing DPT. Longer term, RISE is a $10B+ development program with uncertain payoff, and legacy GE pension/insurance liabilities still sit on the balance sheet.

**How to think about it.** This is a razor-and-blade installed-base compounder in a structural commercial-aerospace upcycle — but one whose "blade" (flight-hours-driven services) is unusually exposed right now to a live geopolitical shock to global air traffic.

## Situation Summary

Q1 2026 (reported April 21) was operationally excellent — GAAP revenue $12.4B (+25%, +29% adjusted), adjusted EPS $1.86 (+25%), orders $23.0B (+87% YoY), FCF $1.7B (+14%), CES services +39%, and commercial engine deliveries +43%. But management **held** full-year 2026 guidance unchanged (adj EPS $7.10-$7.40, op profit $9.85-$10.25B, FCF $8.0-$8.4B trending to high end) despite a beat strong enough to justify a raise. CEO Larry Culp said explicitly that "were it not for current events" they likely would have raised. The "current event" is the **2026 Iran war / Strait of Hormuz crisis**: the conflict began in late February, oil spiked ~64% on the Hormuz closure, jet fuel roughly doubled, and Middle East air corridors and airports (~15% of global traffic) were disrupted. A fragile ceasefire has held since April 8, and as of late May Trump says a deal to reopen Hormuz is "largely negotiated." Guidance now bakes in elevated Brent through Q3 and softer departures growth. The stock fell 4-6% on the print (margin compression + no raise) but has since recovered to ~$316, near the prior trading range.

## Variant Perception

- **Consensus view:** GE Aerospace is a premium-quality compounder in a structural aerospace upcycle, temporarily clouded by a geopolitical fuel shock. Wall Street rates it Strong Buy (21 analysts, mean target ~$350, +11%), 81% institutional ownership, near-zero shorts. The market treats the held guidance as conservatism that will be unwound once the war resolves, and prices ~36x forward guided EPS expecting double-digit earnings growth for years.

- **Our view:** Consensus is right on the business quality and probably right that the war is transient. Where we differ is on margin of safety and the *composition* of the risk. (1) The held guidance is being read as sandbagging, but it embeds real assumptions — elevated Brent through Q3 and softer departures — that, if the war's air-traffic damage proves stickier than a clean ceasefire implies, could turn the high end into the low end rather than a beat. The aftermarket annuity scales with global flight hours, and a fuel-driven demand slowdown hits the highest-margin revenue line directly. (2) Q1 margins actually compressed ~200bps at the group level (CES -230bps) even as revenue surged — operating leverage was not visible this quarter, which the bull thesis assumes is permanent. (3) At 36x forward / 39x trailing with the price near range-highs again ($316 vs $348 52w high), the starting valuation still leaves little cushion. The edge here is modest and is about *timing/price*, not hidden value — this remains one of the best-understood industrials longs.

- **Trigger:** A confirmed Hormuz reopening / durable Iran ceasefire (Trump signaled late-May) would likely prompt GE to raise guidance at Q2 earnings (~late July 2026) — the catalyst that re-rates. Conversely, a ceasefire breakdown that keeps jet fuel elevated into peak summer travel is the downside trigger.

## Inflection Point

GE's structural inflection (the conglomerate breakup → aerospace pure-play) is long done; this is the execution/harvesting phase. The only candidate for a near-term inflection is *negative and external*: the 2026 Iran war is a potential demand-side disruption to flight hours that has not yet fully played through guidance. There is **no new positive inflection** — it remains a "continuation of excellence, at a full price" story, now with a transient macro overhang.

## Bull Case

- Hormuz reopens / durable Iran ceasefire holds; jet fuel normalizes, air traffic recovers into summer, and GE raises FY26 guidance at Q2 (late July) — the explicit catalyst Culp teased.
- LEAP installed base triples to 2030, driving a high-margin aftermarket annuity that pushes services revenue +15% for years; CES margins resume expanding.
- Record orders ($23B in Q1, +87%) and ~$170B services backlog convert as supply-chain output ramps; deliveries +43% sustains.
- Defense wins (T901, adaptive-cycle propulsion) plus rising global defense budgets lift DPT margins off the soft 11.8%.
- Strong FCF ($8.0-8.4B 2026 guide) funds buybacks that compound EPS.

## Bear Case

- **Margin compression is structural, not transient** — Q1 group op margin fell ~200bps and CES -230bps *while revenue grew 25%+*. The market under-covered this; headlines led with the fuel/war story, but the disclosed margin give-back undercuts the "operating leverage compounds forever" core of the bull thesis. If mix/cost pressure persists, the premium multiple is unjustified.
- The Iran war / Hormuz disruption proves sticky: elevated jet fuel into peak summer slows departures, airlines cut capacity and defer flying, and the aftermarket (highest-margin line) decelerates — turning the FY26 high end into the low end.
- Supply-chain limits (titanium, castings, labor) cap LEAP deliveries; backlog is meaningless if undeliverable.
- Valuation de-rating: at ~36x forward, a guidance trim or any growth-deceleration scare triggers institutional selling; 5 turns of PE compression ≈ -14% even with earnings growth.
- Boeing/Airbus build-rate slippage reduces near-term engine demand.

## Verdict

**WATCH** — Conviction: MEDIUM

GE Aerospace remains an outstanding business (21/25), but at $315.65 (~36x forward guided EPS) the scenario-weighted expected value is roughly flat (~0%), with a clearly asymmetric *downside* if the Iran-war fuel/traffic shock lingers or the Q1 margin compression proves structural. The stock has recovered the post-earnings dip and now sits near range-highs, having already partly discounted a clean war resolution — so the asymmetry favors patience. There is no fresh variant perception; this is the most-understood industrials long, and the reliable (GBM) models, recalculated against today's higher price, now imply only low-teens upside at best. The new fact since March is the live 2026 Iran war: it caused management to hold a beat-worthy guidance, and it injects genuine demand-side risk into the highest-margin aftermarket line that the consensus is treating as transient noise.

**Would upgrade to BUY on:** (1) a pullback to ~$255-$275 (~33-35x forward) for a real margin of safety and +12-18% EV; or (2) a confirmed Hormuz reopening / durable Iran ceasefire *combined with* GE reaffirming margin expansion at Q2 — i.e., the guidance-raise catalyst with services growth holding above mid-teens. A broader market correction dragging GE down with its 1.35 beta is the most probable entry. **Thesis-break / avoid-adding signal:** FY26 EPS guidance cut below $7.00 or a second consecutive quarter of CES margin compression.
