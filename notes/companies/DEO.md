## Business Primer

**What they do.** Diageo is the largest premium-spirits company on earth, ~$20B annual net sales across an unmatched brand stable: **Johnnie Walker** (the world's best-selling Scotch), **Guinness** (a structurally growing beer franchise), **Don Julio** and **Casamigos** (the two fastest-premiumizing tequilas), **Tanqueray/Gordon's** gin, **Smirnoff** and **Cîroc** vodka, **Captain Morgan** rum, **Baileys**, **Crown Royal** Canadian whisky, and a 34% stake in Moët Hennessy. It sells in ~180 countries; North America is ~39% of sales, Europe ~24%, with the rest split across LAC, Asia-Pacific and Africa.

**Why it's a wide moat.** Three reinforcing layers: (1) **aged liquid inventory** — you cannot conjure 12-year-old Scotch or añejo tequila on demand, so Diageo's barrel banks are a literal time-moat competitors can't replicate; (2) **brand equity + global distribution** — century-old marques with shelf and on-premise placement no new entrant can buy; (3) **premiumization mix** — ~60% gross margin and ~31% operating margin reflect genuine pricing power. Free cash flow runs ~$3B even in this trough year.

**Why it's cheap (the de-rating).** A multi-year unwind, not a single shock:
1. **Post-pandemic destocking.** The November 2023 Latin America profit warning exposed channel over-shipment; the US then went through its own multi-year inventory and consumption correction. Net sales have been *flat-to-down* (~$20B) for four years while costs rose.
2. **Structural demand fears.** GLP-1 weight-loss drugs, Gen-Z drinking less, and a broad "moderation" trend cast doubt on long-run spirits volume in developed markets — the bear's core argument for a *permanently* lower multiple.
3. **US tariffs.** A large share of Diageo's US revenue is *imported* (Scotch from the UK, tequila from Mexico, Canadian whisky), so US import tariffs hit the cost base directly with limited near-term mitigation.
4. **The flush of 2026.** In Feb 2026 the new CEO **cut the interim dividend to $0.20/share from $0.405** (a ~50% rebase to a 30–50% payout policy, ~50c/share floor) to prioritize balance-sheet repair, *and* trimmed FY26 guidance. The income holders who owned DEO for its ~4% progressive dividend sold the news.

## Situation Summary

DEO sits at **$78.67**, near its 52-week low ($72.45) and **~60% below its January 2022 ADR peak of ~$196**. The business is *stable, not collapsing* — the discount is sentiment + margin, not demand evaporation:

- **Revenue is flat, not falling off a cliff:** FY22 $20.5B → FY23 $20.6B → FY24 $20.3B → FY25 $20.25B. The "decline" narrative is about *price/mix and margin*, not volume implosion.
- **FY25 reported EPS ($4.23/ADR) was artificially depressed** by impairments (notably the Chinese baijiu brand Shui Jing Fang). FY24 was $6.91; consensus forward EPS is **~$6.45**. Underlying earnings power is ~$6.3–6.5, so the screen's trailing "18x" overstates the real multiple — **forward P/E is ~12.2x**, versus Diageo's historical 20–24x.
- **H1/Q3 FY26 inflection signs:** Q3 returned to *modest organic net-sales growth*, with Europe, LAC and Africa offsetting still-soft US and Asia-Pacific. FY26 guidance: organic net sales **−2 to −3%**, organic operating profit **flat to up low-single-digit** (including ~$300M Accelerate cost savings net of tariffs), **$3B FCF reiterated**.
- **Active deleveraging:** net debt $21.7B at Dec 2025 (~3.5x EBITDA). Diageo agreed to sell its East African Breweries / Kenya stakes to Asahi for ~$2.3B net, cutting leverage ~0.25x; the dividend rebase frees further cash for debt paydown.
- **New leadership + datable catalyst:** Debra Crew was ousted (mid-2025); **Sir Dave Lewis** — the operator who turned around Tesco — is now CEO, running a strategic review. **A full Strategy Update lands Aug 6, 2026 with FY26 results.** Beta ~0.30 (defensive).

The precise debate: *is ~12x forward earnings for the irreplaceable #1 global spirits franchise too cheap — given that the worst news (dividend cut, guidance cut, CEO churn) is already out and a credible turnaround operator now has a near-term reset catalyst — or is moderation/GLP-1 a structural ceiling that keeps this a value trap?*

## Variant Perception

- **Consensus view:** A melting premium brand. Spirits is in secular decline in its richest markets (US/Europe) as GLP-1s and sobriety curb consumption; tariffs structurally raise Diageo's cost base; the dividend cut confirms the balance sheet was stretched; and there's no quick fix. Cheap for a reason, dead money until volumes turn.
- **Our view:** The market is extrapolating a *cyclical destock + a one-time margin/tariff hit + a prudent balance-sheet reset* into *permanent secular decline*. Revenue has held at ~$20B through the whole "collapse," gross margin is still ~60%, and the aged-inventory moat is untouched. Crucially, **the de-risking events are now behind the stock, not ahead of it**: the dividend is rebased (so the payout overhang is gone and FCF redirects to deleveraging), guidance is reset low enough that Q3 already beat back to growth, and a proven turnaround CEO owns a public reset moment in ~two months. You're paying a decade-trough ~12x for a franchise that historically commanded 20x+, with a still-respectable (rebased) ~3.4% yield while you wait.
- **Trigger / what re-rates it:** The **Aug 6 2026 strategy update** (cost program, brand-portfolio focus, capital-allocation clarity from Dave Lewis), continued US-spirits stabilization, tariff mitigation/exemptions, and visible deleveraging from the Asahi disposal. Any one shifts the narrative from "structural decline" to "trough cyclical with self-help." The mirror risk that breaks it: US/China weakness deepens, tariffs escalate, and GLP-1/moderation proves to be a genuine volume ceiling rather than a scare — in which case 12x is fair, not cheap.
