# Halliburton Company (HAL)

**Sector:** Energy | **Industry:** Oil & Gas Equipment & Services
**Price:** $41.76 (2026-05-21) | **Market Cap:** ~$34.9B
**Analysis Date:** 2026-05-21

## Business Primer

**What they do today.** Halliburton is one of the two largest oilfield services companies in the world (the other is SLB, formerly Schlumberger). Oil and gas producers (Exxon, Chevron, national oil companies, US shale independents) own the rights to drill, but they mostly do not own the specialized equipment or crews to actually get oil out of the ground. They hire Halliburton to do it. Revenue splits into two roughly equal divisions. **Completion and Production (~$3.0B/quarter, ~55%)** is the bigger, more North-America-weighted business: this is "hydraulic fracturing" (frac/stimulation) — pumping water, sand, and chemicals at high pressure into a drilled well to crack the shale rock and let oil flow — plus the tools, pumps, and chemicals that finish a well and keep it producing. **Drilling and Evaluation (~$2.4B/quarter, ~45%)** is the more international business: drill bits, the directional-drilling and steering systems that point a well sideways through a thin oil layer two miles underground, plus the sensors and software that measure what the rock contains. Customers pay per job, per day, or per project, so revenue tracks how busy the drilling industry is — i.e., it tracks the oil-price cycle with a lag.

**Why customers choose them (the value proposition).** A shale producer's economics live or die on how much oil each frac job recovers and how fast wells get completed. Halliburton's pitch is that its integrated package — its **Zeus electric fracturing fleet** plus **Octiv AutoFrac** automation — pumps sand into the rock more precisely and runs closer to non-stop than a conventional diesel-pump crew. Concretely, Zeus can "measure where the sand is going, move the sand around, and create a closed-loop fracturing environment," meaning the operator places proppant where the oil actually is instead of guessing, and the electric fleet runs on cheaper field gas instead of diesel with less downtime. For an oil company choosing between Halliburton and a cheaper regional frac contractor, the question is "more barrels per dollar and fewer days to first oil," and Halliburton sells measurable answers to both. In drilling, its directional and logging tools let an operator steer a horizontal well precisely through the productive layer — the difference between a great well and a dry hole. The catch: in commodity service lines (basic pressure pumping during a downturn) the product is close to a commodity, and pricing collapses when too many crews chase too little work.

**Where they want to grow.** Three explicit bets. First, **international and offshore** — management is pushing hard outside North America (Latin America +22% YoY, Europe/Africa +11% YoY in Q1 2026), with offshore wins in Guyana, Suriname, West Africa, and a YPF Argentina contract that is the first international deployment of the Zeus electric frac system. Second, **technology and automation** — selling Zeus/Octiv as a premium, higher-margin, stickier offering rather than competing on price for commodity frac. Third, **capital discipline and per-share value** — instead of chasing market share by adding equipment, management is holding capex roughly flat (~$1.1B/year) and returning cash via buybacks and a $0.17/quarter dividend, betting that disciplined supply lets pricing recover. Success in 3-5 years looks like mid-to-high-single-digit international growth, a North American recovery lifting frac pricing, and steadily rising free cash flow per share.

**What could go wrong (business risks).** This is a deeply cyclical commodity-linked business: when oil falls, producers slash drilling budgets first, and service prices fall faster than activity because crews compete for scraps — exactly what happened in 2025 (net income halved). North America frac is structurally oversupplied in normal times, capping pricing power. Geopolitics cuts both ways: a Middle East conflict raises oil prices (good for activity) but also directly disrupts Halliburton's own Saudi/Qatar operations (management flagged a $0.07-$0.09/share Q2 hit from the conflict). Longer term, the energy transition and US shale maturing (the best acreage already drilled) cap the North American growth runway. And SLB is a formidable, larger competitor especially internationally.

**How to think about it.** Halliburton is a high-quality, capital-disciplined cyclical — the #2 global oilfield-services franchise whose earnings swing with the oil-drilling cycle. It is not a compounder you buy and forget; it is a cycle instrument where the entire return depends on correctly reading where you are in the completions/drilling cycle. Right now it is at an early-recovery inflection coming off a 2025 trough, with a Middle East oil-supply shock as the accelerant.

## Situation Summary

Halliburton sits at a cyclical inflection. After a weak 2025 (full-year net income $1.28B, down ~49% YoY, ROE collapsing from 28% in 2023 to 12%), Q1 2026 showed flat revenue ($5.4B) but a sharp earnings rebound ($461M / $0.55 EPS vs $204M / $0.24 a year ago, operating margin 13% vs ~8%). The dominant macro event: a Middle East conflict has shut in roughly 10.5 mb/d of crude via the Strait of Hormuz, spiking WTI to ~$99.5/bbl as of late May 2026. That supply shock is reigniting US drilling into a service sector that cut capacity through 2025 (US frac spread count ~160, down 50 spreads YoY; horizontal rig count down 18% YoY). CEO Jeff Miller's framing is explicit and bullish: "the supply overhang is no longer a concern... structural demand remains intact," "white space for Q2 is all but gone," and "the world is fundamentally tighter in oil and gas than it was sixty days ago." The market narrative is constructive — 25 analysts skew Buy (12 Strong Buy, 9 Hold, 1 Strong Sell), mean target ~$43.5 — but the stock has already nearly doubled off its 52-week low of ~$19 to $41.76, sitting just under its 52-week high.

## Variant Perception

- **Consensus view:** Analysts are broadly bullish (Moderate-to-Strong Buy, mean target ~$43.5, only modestly above spot). The market accepts that 2025 was the trough and a recovery is underway, and has already re-rated the stock ~115% off the low. forwardPE 14.4 vs trailingPE 23.1 means consensus already prices a ~60% earnings rebound.
- **Our view:** We agree with the *direction* but the variant edge is narrow and time-sensitive. The non-consensus element is the asymmetry created by the Hormuz supply shock colliding with a supply-constrained service sector (capacity was cut in the 2025 downturn and cannot be rebuilt quickly — frac fleets and crews are effectively "sold out"). If the shock persists even a few quarters, frac pricing inflects up faster than the Street models, because tight *equipment availability* — not just activity — drives Halliburton's pricing power. That is an earnings-driven, not multiple-driven, upside. The offsetting variant risk the bulls under-weight: this is now a *priced* recovery near 52w highs whose tail entirely depends on the oil price holding, and management itself flagged a direct conflict cost ($0.07-$0.09/share in Q2) that the "oil is tight = pure positive" narrative under-covers.
- **Trigger:** Q2 2026 earnings (~mid/late July 2026) — first quarter to show whether frac pricing actually inflects up ("constructive pricing conversations" turning into realized 50-100 bps C&P margin expansion) and whether the closing of frac "white space" translates to revenue. Secondary trigger: resolution (or escalation) of the Strait of Hormuz disruption, which directly sets the oil price that drives the whole thesis.

## Inflection Point

**Yes — early-recovery inflection with observable evidence.** This is the textbook setup the protocol favors: a cyclical coming off a trough where the turn has *begun and is observable*, not hoped-for. Evidence: (1) Q1'26 EPS +129% YoY and operating margin +500 bps; (2) management reporting frac "white space for Q2 all but gone" and rising spot work — a real-time activity signal; (3) WTI ~$99.5 driven by a 10.5 mb/d Hormuz supply shock into a service sector with capacity cut (frac spreads -50 YoY) — the demand-up/supply-tight combination that drives pricing inflections; (4) forward PE (14.4) well below trailing (23.1), confirming earnings recovery is expected. The risk is that the inflection is *already substantially priced* — the stock has run ~115% off its $19 low to near its 52w high — so you are buying after the initial recovery (good for value-trap avoidance) but late enough that much of the easy upside is gone.

## Bull Case

- Hormuz supply shock persists, WTI holds $90-100+, US producers ramp drilling into a service sector that physically cannot add frac capacity fast — frac pricing inflects up hard, lifting C&P margins well beyond the guided 50-100 bps and re-rating normalized EPS toward $3+.
- International/offshore momentum (LatAm +22%, Norway, Guyana, Suriname, Argentina Zeus) compounds independent of the US cycle, giving a second growth engine.
- Capital discipline (flat capex, buybacks accelerating H2) converts the activity recovery into per-share FCF growth rather than reinvestment, supporting a higher multiple.
- GBM/autoresearch cluster (most reliable models) points to ~$48-52, +18-25%.

## Bear Case

- **The thesis is oil-price-dependent and possibly already over its skis.** Stock has nearly doubled off the low to near 52w highs; the recovery is largely priced. A US-Iran deal reopening the Strait of Hormuz would crater WTI back toward $60-65 (search confirmed traders are pricing this exact scenario — oil already fell ~6% in two sessions on deal hopes), stalling the US drilling ramp and de-rating the stock.
- **Under-covered primary-source risk:** management explicitly guided a **$0.07-$0.09/share negative Q2 hit** from the very same Middle East conflict the bull narrative treats as a pure positive — and Drilling & Evaluation margins are guided *down* 75-125 bps sequentially in Q2 (seasonal software decline). The conflict is a double-edged sword the headlines under-weight, and the Middle East segment (Saudi/Qatar) was already -13% YoY before the conflict escalated.
- North American pressure pumping remains structurally oversupplied in any normal environment; absent a sustained price shock, frac pricing power evaporates and the company is back to a low-return commodity business (the 2025 reality: NI halved, ROE 12%).
- DCF/RIM models scream overvalued (-21% to -77%); even discounting their trough-anchoring bias, they flag that *static, no-recovery* fundamentals do not support $42.
- Energy-transition / shale-maturity structural cap on long-term North American growth.

## Verdict

**WATCH** — Conviction: MEDIUM

Halliburton is a genuine, observable early-cycle inflection in a quality #2 oilfield-services franchise (quality 19/25), and the GBM/autoresearch models point to ~+20-25% with the DCF cluster mis-firing on trough earnings. But expected value is only ~+10% because the recovery has already been priced (stock near 52w high, +115% off the low) and the bull tail rests entirely on a Hormuz oil-supply shock that markets are actively betting may resolve via a US-Iran deal — a binary the bullish headlines under-weight, and one management itself flagged as a near-term cost ($0.07-0.09/share Q2). This is a good business at a fair-to-full price into a binary catalyst, not a fat-pitch entry.

**If WATCH:** Would upgrade to BUY on either (a) a pullback to ~$36-37 (back to the 50-day/pre-spike level) that restores margin of safety while the inflection is intact, OR (b) Q2 2026 earnings confirming frac pricing is actually inflecting up (realized C&P margin expansion + closed white space converting to revenue) with WTI holding above ~$80 — which would de-risk the structural-recovery read and justify chasing strength.
