# Fidelity National Information Services (FIS)

**Sector:** Technology / Financials | **Industry:** Financial technology — core banking software, capital-markets tech, and (since Jan 2026) card-issuer processing
**Price:** $39.50 (2026-06-17) | **Market Cap:** ~$20.4B | **Shares:** ~517M | **Total Debt:** ~$21B (leverage 3.6x adj. EBITDA)
**Analysis Date:** 2026-06-17
**Standalone rating: BUY LOW** (6.3x forward EPS, ~10% FCF yield, 4.3% dividend, organic ~5% growth + clear deleveraging catalyst — but management's capital-allocation record is genuinely bad and the equity is geared)

## Situation Summary
FIS is one of the "big three" financial-technology incumbents (alongside Fiserv/FI and the now-split Global Payments). It sells the plumbing banks run on: **core banking software, payments/digital, and capital-markets technology** — multi-year, contractually recurring, high-switching-cost revenue. On **2026-01-09 FIS closed a transformative asset swap** with Global Payments (GPN): it handed GPN its remaining 45% stake in Worldpay (~$6.6B value) plus ~$7.7B cash, and in return acquired GPN's **Issuer Solutions** business (~$13.5B), now rebranded **FIS Total Issuing Solutions** — the back-end that processes credit/debit cards for banks. FIS is now a pure-play banking-and-issuer technology company; the messy, value-destroying Worldpay merchant-acquiring chapter is finally closed.

The stock sits at **$39.50, its absolute 2-year low** (down ~55% from the $86 high, and a fraction of its ~$150 2021 peak). On the screen it is statistically cheap: **~6.3x forward adjusted EPS, ~10% free-cash-flow yield, ~6.7x EV/EBITDA, 4.3% dividend.** The bear case is not that the business is melting — **revenue is growing** ($9.7B → $9.8B → $10.1B → $10.7B FY2022-25, ~5%/yr) and FY2026 is guided to **~5% pro-forma organic growth**. The bear case is (1) **management has destroyed enormous capital** here before, (2) the deal **re-levered** the balance sheet to 3.6x right after FIS spent 2024 deleveraging, and (3) **buybacks are paused**, removing the main EPS-growth lever until leverage falls to 2.8x.

## Variant Perception
- **Consensus view:** "Serial value-destroyer. FIS overpaid $43B for Worldpay in 2019, wrote off ~$23B of goodwill in 2022-23, sold Worldpay at a massive loss to deleverage — and then immediately re-levered to 3.6x to buy a *mature* card-issuer business that Global Payments was happy to sell. Capital allocation is incoherent, buybacks are frozen, and the whole payments/fintech complex is derating on stablecoin and real-time-payments disruption fears. Cheap for a reason. Avoid."
- **Our view (partial pushback):** The *current* FIS is the cleanest version of this business in years, and you are paying a trough multiple for ~5% organic growth, 42% EBITDA margins, and revenue that banks cannot easily switch away from. At ~6.3x earnings you do **not** need a re-rating to win: ~10% FCF yield + 4.3% dividend + ~5% organic growth is a low-teens return on its own. The deleveraging path is explicit and self-funding (FCF $2.05-2.15B in 2026), and when leverage hits 2.8x (~18-24 months) buybacks resume — at 6x earnings, those buybacks are violently accretive. The asymmetry is favorable: you're buying a sticky, recurring-revenue, investment-grade compounder at the multiple of a structurally declining business.
- **Why it is NOT a slam-dunk:** Management has earned its discount. The 2019 Worldpay deal was one of the worst large-cap M&A decisions of the decade, and the swap-back-and-forth (sell Worldpay to deleverage, then re-lever for Issuer Solutions) reads as strategy-by-banker. Issuer Solutions is a mature business with concentrated large-bank clients who can in-source or defect (to Fiserv/TSYS). 3.6x leverage geared onto a 6.7x EV/EBITDA equity means a stalled EBITDA hurts the stock disproportionately. This has been a value trap for four years running — "cheap and getting cheaper."
- **Trigger / what re-rates it:** Two or three quarters proving (a) Issuer Solutions retention holds and integration is clean, (b) leverage marching toward 2.8x on schedule, and (c) buybacks resuming. Even a re-rate from 6.3x to a still-cheap 8.5x on flat EPS is ~+35%; analysts' mean target is **$58 (~+47%)**.

## Bull / Bear

**Bull**
- **Trough multiple on a growing business:** ~6.3x forward EPS, ~6.7x EV/EBITDA, ~10% FCF yield for ~5% organic growth and 42% EBITDA margins. You're paying melting-ice-cube prices for a high-switching-cost recurring-revenue franchise.
- **Self-funding deleveraging catalyst:** $2.1B FCF drives 3.6x → 2.8x in ~18-24 months; buyback resumption at 6x earnings is highly accretive and is the obvious re-rating trigger.
- **Clean story at last:** Worldpay merchant-acquiring (the source of all the pain) is fully gone. FIS is now banking software + capital markets + issuer processing — sticky, defensive, low-beta (0.80).
- **Income while you wait:** 4.3% dividend, ~27% of FCF, comfortably covered even with buybacks paused.
- **Analyst support / valuation gap:** mean target $58 (~+47%); a re-rate to a still-modest 8.5x is ~+35% on flat EPS alone.

**Bear**
- **Management's capital-allocation record is genuinely bad:** $43B Worldpay (2019) → ~$23B in write-offs → sold at a loss → re-levered again for a business GPN was selling. The market's distrust is *earned*, and the next deal could destroy value too.
- **Re-leveraged equity:** 3.6x net leverage geared onto a 6.7x EV/EBITDA enterprise — if EBITDA stalls, equity holders absorb it amplified. Rising-rate refinancing pressures FCF.
- **Buyback pause removes the lever:** without repurchases, EPS growth is just ~5-8% organic; the cheap-stock-buying-itself flywheel is off for 1-2 years.
- **Issuer Solutions quality risk:** mature, large-bank-concentrated card processing; client in-sourcing or defection to Fiserv/TSYS would hit the very business FIS just paid $13.5B for.
- **Sector + secular overhang:** the whole payments/fintech complex (PYPL, GPN, FI) is derating on stablecoin / real-time-payments / AI-disruption narratives. FIS has been a value trap for four years; cheap can stay cheap.

## Entry / Exit Framework
- **Entry:** attractive here at **$39.50** (2-year low, 6.3x). Add aggressively **sub-$36** (sub-6x, ~11% FCF yield).
- **Thesis-break price / stop-thinking level:** a sustained move **below ~$32** on *fundamental* deterioration (not sector beta) — i.e. Issuer client losses, organic growth turning negative, or a leverage/dividend scare — invalidates the deleveraging-catalyst thesis.
- **What would change my mind (bull→bear):** (1) Q2/Q3 2026 showing pro-forma organic growth decelerating below ~3% or Issuer Solutions revenue shrinking; (2) leverage *not* tracking toward 2.8x (deal-funded debt costlier than planned); (3) another large debt-funded acquisition before buybacks resume — confirming the capital-allocation bear case.
- **What would change my mind (bear→more bull):** buyback resumption announced ahead of schedule, or a couple of clean integration quarters with reaffirmed/raised guidance → justifies pressing toward the base/bull case.
