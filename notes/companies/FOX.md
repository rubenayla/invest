# Fox Corporation (FOX)

**Sector:** Communication Services | **Industry:** Entertainment
**Price:** $58.09 (2026-05-19) | **Market Cap:** $24.4B
**Analysis Date:** 2026-05-19

## Business Primer

**What they do today.** Fox Corporation is what was left at home after the Walt Disney Company bought most of 21st Century Fox in 2019 — specifically, the news, sports, and live-broadcast pieces that the Murdoch family kept. Today Fox makes money in three buckets. The biggest is the **Cable Network Programming** segment, which is mostly Fox News Channel (cable news leader in the United States by a wide margin), plus Fox Business, FS1, FS2, and the Big Ten Network. The second bucket is **Television**, which is the over-the-air Fox broadcast network (the one you can pick up free with an antenna) and the 29 local Fox-owned stations in cities like New York, Los Angeles, Chicago, and Houston. The third bucket is **Tubi**, a free streaming service where viewers watch movies and old TV shows without paying anything and Fox sells the ad slots. Roughly half of Fox's revenue is *affiliate and distribution fees* — every cable, satellite, and streaming pay-TV operator (Comcast, Charter Spectrum, DirecTV, YouTube TV, Hulu+Live TV) writes Fox a per-subscriber-per-month check to be allowed to carry Fox News and the Fox broadcast network. The other half is *advertising* — 30-second slots sold around NFL Sunday afternoon games, Fox News primetime, MLB playoffs, college football, and Tubi streams. Latest twelve months: $16.3B revenue, $2.3B net income, $3.0B free cash flow, ~21% operating margins.

**Why customers choose them (the value proposition).** Fox has two very different sets of customers — the distributors who pay carriage fees, and the advertisers who buy ad slots — and the answer is different for each. **Distributors keep Fox in the bundle because if they drop it, customers cancel.** Fox News averages 2-3 million primetime viewers, which is more than CNN and MSNBC *combined*; for the roughly 40% of pay-TV households that lean Republican, a cable package without Fox News is unusable, so the moment Spectrum or YouTube TV drops the channel they bleed subscribers fast. The same logic applies even harder to the Fox broadcast network: it owns the NFL Sunday afternoon NFC package and the World Series in odd years, which means dropping Fox would also mean telling football fans they cannot watch their team. A cable operator that resists a Fox rate hike faces a binary choice — accept a 5-8% per-sub annual fee increase, or lose the customers who came for sports and news. They almost always pay. **Advertisers pick Fox because it is one of the few places left to reach a live, simultaneous, large audience.** When a brand wants 50 million Americans watching the same screen at the same moment — for a Super Bowl, an NFL Sunday late afternoon game, the World Series, a presidential debate on Fox News — there are only two or three places to buy that, and Fox owns the inventory for many of those events. Streaming services like Netflix can deliver scale, but not *live* scale concentrated in a few minutes, which is what political campaigns, beer brands, pickup truck makers, and pharma launches need. National cost per thousand impressions (CPMs) on Fox News rose 45% year-over-year because there is no real substitute for live cable-news ad space during a political cycle. For Tubi, the value proposition flips again: the customer is the *viewer*, and the pitch is "free movies, no signup required, no monthly bill" — Tubi is now the second-largest ad-supported streaming service in the United States behind the Pluto-Roku tier, growing revenue 23% with view time up 19%.

**Where they want to grow.** Three explicit bets. First, **Fox One**, the direct-to-consumer streaming bundle launched August 2025 that packages Fox News, Fox Sports, the Fox broadcast network, and entertainment shows for cord-cutters and cord-nevers who will not pay $90/month for a full cable bundle. As of Q3 FY26 it is exceeding expectations on churn (much lower than feared) and roughly half of viewing is news content — meaning Fox is finding political-news cord-cutters willing to pay direct. The October 2025 add-on bundle with ESPN Unlimited at $39.99/month puts Fox sports and ESPN live sports in one app and is the first real challenger to the YouTube TV / Hulu Live virtual bundles. Second, **Tubi scale** — keeping the free ad-supported video on demand (AVOD) flywheel spinning, holding break-even-plus profitability while still growing 20%+, and using it as the digital ad inventory that offsets cable-sub decline. Third, **disciplined capital return** — Fox has bought back 36% of all shares outstanding since 2019 (over $8.5B total), and is on pace for another $2B+ in fiscal 2026. The strategic narrative for fiscal 2027 is the political-midterm and FIFA World Cup advertising cycle: Fox owns 104 World Cup matches (US-hosted summer 2026) and political ad spend in the 2026 midterms is estimated at $11B industry-wide, much of it flowing through Fox News and Fox-owned local stations.

**What could go wrong (business risks).** The cable bundle is structurally shrinking — pay-TV households drop roughly 6-7% per year, and while Fox has so far offset that with rate increases of similar magnitude, that game cannot continue forever. If carriage-fee growth falls below subscriber loss, distribution revenue inflects negatively. Sports rights inflation is the other slow squeeze: the National Football League deal runs through 2033 with built-in escalators, Major League Baseball and college-football rights renewals continue to get more expensive, and a bidding war with Amazon, Netflix, or YouTube for the next Sunday-Ticket-style package could compress sports margins. Fox News political-cycle revenue is cyclical — fiscal years without a presidential or major midterm election (the "off" years) see materially lower ad pricing. Tubi sits in a crowded AVOD market against Pluto, Roku Channel, Freevee, and an emerging cohort of ad-tier services from Netflix, Disney+, and Max; share gains are not guaranteed and content licensing costs are rising. There is also a Murdoch-family governance overhang: Lachlan Murdoch now has consolidated voting control via the post-settlement family trust (36% voting), the dual-class share structure means public Class A and B holders have little say, and any future strategic combination (a re-merger with News Corp, a sale of Tubi, an asset swap) will be decided by one family. Finally, regulatory and reputational risk on Fox News specifically — defamation lawsuits (the 2023 Dominion settlement was $787M), advertiser boycotts, or political shifts in cable-carriage rules could hit the cash-cow segment disproportionately.

**How to think about it.** Fox is a *post-Disney-deal* media company with two of the strongest live-TV franchises in America (Fox News + NFL Sunday) inside a cable bundle that is slowly shrinking — and management is using the cash flow to aggressively buy back stock and seed a streaming option (Fox One + Tubi) before the bundle erodes. Think of it as "high-quality melting ice cube with a credible digital offset and an aggressive share-count reducer" — not a growth story, but a cash-return story with optionality on streaming working.

## Situation Summary

Fox reported a strong fiscal Q3 2026 (calendar Q1 2026, reported 2026-05-11): revenue ~$4.0B with adjusted EBITDA up 11% YoY to a record $954M, despite a tough comparison against prior-year Super Bowl LIX (>$800M of one-time advertising revenue in the year-ago quarter). Cable revenue grew 6%, Cable EBITDA $884M (+1%), Television EBITDA tripled to $191M. Tubi grew revenue 23% with view time up 19%, breakeven-plus for the third straight quarter and now self-funding. Fox One — launched August 2025 — is exceeding internal expectations on churn, with news content driving over half of viewing time, and the October 2025 ESPN Unlimited bundle ($39.99/month) opened a second distribution channel. Year-to-date FY26 buybacks reached $1.95B; cumulative buybacks since 2019 exceed $8.5B (about 36% of original shares outstanding). The stock is up after earnings but sits at ~$58 versus the 52-week high of $68 — the market is treating it as a melting cable asset; management is treating it as a cash machine that funds streaming optionality and share-count reduction. The fiscal 2027 setup is unusually favorable: 104 FIFA World Cup matches (US-hosted summer 2026), full United States midterm political-ad cycle (~$11B industry estimate), full NFL season, MLB playoffs.

## Variant Perception

- **Consensus view:** Linear-TV "melting ice cube." Cable subs declining 6-7%/year, Super Bowl was a once-in-three-years tailwind that will not repeat, Tubi is interesting but small, Fox One is unproven and late versus Disney+ and Netflix, Murdoch governance overhang. Analyst consensus is "Moderate Buy" with $66 average target (~14% upside) — modest optimism, no real conviction. Stock trades at 11x forward earnings and 1.5x sales — multiples for a structurally declining business.
- **Our view:** Three things consensus is mis-weighting. (1) The cable bundle's death has been forecast for a decade, and Fox keeps offsetting subscriber losses with mid-single-digit price increases because Fox News and NFL Sunday are non-negotiable in the bundle — this dynamic has more years left than the bear case assumes. (2) Tubi at +23% revenue growth and breakeven-plus is *already* the digital offset, and the market is not pricing it as a real asset (Tubi alone arguably worth $4-8B at peer AVOD multiples versus zero credit in the stock). (3) Fox One churn is running below internal plan and news-led viewing means Fox has found a paying cord-cutter audience that wants its product specifically — early evidence of pricing power in direct-to-consumer. Combined with fiscal 2027's once-a-decade convergence of World Cup, midterms, and full NFL, earnings power in fiscal 2027 looks materially above the trailing 12-month run-rate.
- **Trigger:** Fiscal 2027 earnings ramp through Q1-Q2 FY27 (Sep–Dec 2026) as World Cup, NFL, and midterm political ad spend compound; updated Fox One subscriber disclosure (likely first explicit number at Q4 FY26 earnings in August 2026) demonstrating that direct-to-consumer is real, not a science project.

## Inflection Point

Modest inflection — not transformational. Three things are turning at once: (1) Tubi crossed into sustained profitability and the digital-investment line is now declining year-over-year (no more cash drag); (2) Fox One has cleared the launch-risk hurdle (churn below plan, news-led usage); (3) fiscal 2027 is set up for a once-in-cycle earnings ramp (World Cup + midterms + full NFL/MLB). The "static value" case alone — 12% FCF yield, 36% of shares retired in 6 years, double-digit FCF compounding via buybacks — is sufficient to justify ownership without needing any inflection. The inflection just provides upside optionality.

## Bull Case

- Fiscal 2027 earnings ramp materializes: World Cup (104 matches) + midterm political ad cycle (~$11B industry) + full NFL season drive EBITDA 15-20% above LTM run-rate, justifying a 13-14x forward earnings multiple expansion.
- Fox One disclosed subscriber count (expected Q4 FY26 print, August 2026) surprises positively, market starts to credit Fox with a real direct-to-consumer business and re-rates the streaming optionality.
- Tubi continues +20% growth, hits $1.5B+ revenue, market starts valuing it as a discrete asset ($4-8B at peer AVOD multiples versus zero current credit).
- Continued aggressive buybacks at depressed multiple — at ~$2B/year against $24B market cap, Fox can retire another 8-9% of shares per year, mechanically growing per-share metrics even with flat revenue.
- Cable distribution revenue continues to grow despite subscriber declines (Q3 FY26 cable distribution +5%), proving the "carriage-fee pricing power" thesis has multiple more years.

## Bear Case

- Cable bundle finally inflects: subscriber declines accelerate beyond 7% and carriage-fee price increases plateau, putting distribution revenue (~50% of total) into negative growth — *this is the slow-moving structural risk the company itself flags in 10-K disclosures more directly than news coverage suggests, and the one no streaming offset fully solves.*
- Sports rights inflation outpaces ad-rate inflation; the next NFL rights cycle (post-2033) or a forced MLB renewal squeezes sports-segment margins.
- Political and news cycle reversion in fiscal 2028 (post-midterm) makes the FY27 ramp look like a peak; market re-rates back down on cyclical recognition.
- Fox One never scales meaningfully beyond a few million subscribers — too late, too narrow, dominated by news viewers who skew older, fails to capture cord-cutters at scale.
- Murdoch-family governance event: any forced strategic move (sale, merger, asset swap) decided by Lachlan alone, against minority-shareholder interest, with no recourse for public holders.
- Defamation or regulatory risk on Fox News: another Dominion-scale settlement ($800M was the 2023 precedent), advertiser exodus on political controversy, or hostile regulatory action on cable carriage rules.

## Verdict

**BUY** — Conviction: **MEDIUM**

Variant perception is real but moderate: the market is pricing Fox as a structurally declining cable business and giving zero credit to Tubi as a standalone asset, Fox One as a real direct-to-consumer offering, and the fiscal 2027 catalyst convergence. Quality is solid (18/25), the balance sheet is comfortable, capital return is best-in-class (36% of shares retired in 6 years). At 11x forward earnings with a 12% FCF yield, the static value case alone supports the stock; the FY27 ramp plus direct-to-consumer optionality provides upside. Expected value +20% across scenarios. Not a screaming bargain like a deep cyclical at trough, but a high-quality, cash-gushing, aggressively-buying-back-stock asset trading at a meaningfully discounted multiple to its earnings power.

**If BUY:** Entry favored below $60 (current $58 qualifies); scale in further on any pullback toward $52-54 (touch of the 52-week low region). Position can be built in 2-3 tranches across the FY27 ramp. Thesis-break signal: cable distribution revenue prints year-over-year negative for two consecutive quarters (would expect this around FY27 Q2-Q3, late 2026 to early 2027); trim or exit on that data.
