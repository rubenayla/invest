## The business: two segments, one moat

McCormick's fiscal year ends Nov 30; FY2026 is early (Q1 reported ~late March 2026).

- **Consumer (~60% of sales):** branded spices, seasonings, condiments, hot sauce. This is the moat — McCormick holds a dominant (~20%+) share of the US spice aisle, a category with thousands of low-ticket SKUs that retailers can't easily private-label across the whole set, sticky purchase habits, and real pricing power. Brands: McCormick, French's (mustard/fried onions), Frank's RedHot, Cholula, Old Bay, Lawry's, Zatarain's, Gourmet Garden, Cluck & Squeal.
- **Flavor Solutions (~40% of sales):** B2B flavorings sold to food manufacturers, quick-service restaurants, and packaged-goods companies. Higher-volume, lower-margin, and **more cyclical** — it tracks restaurant traffic and CPG production volumes, which is where recent softness has shown up.
- **Slow but real grower (Iron Rule check):** revenue rose FY23→FY25 ($6.66B → $6.84B, +2.7% total) — organic, not acquisition-driven. Operating income climbed faster ($917M → $1,092M, +19%) as inflation-era pricing stuck and input costs eased. This is **not** a melting ice cube like soup; it's a low-single-digit grower with expanding margins. The bull case needs *continuation*, not a turnaround.

## Why it's down (the bear's ammunition)

1. **Volume stagnation.** Growth has leaned on price, not volume; consumers trading down to private-label spices under budget pressure has capped unit growth. A spice brand's moat is real but not infinite when a generic bottle of paprika costs half as much.
2. **Tariff exposure on imported inputs.** McCormick sources globally — black pepper (Vietnam/India), vanilla (Madagascar), cinnamon, herbs. Tariffs and trade friction raise COGS, and there's a lag before pricing offsets it. This is the most credible 2026 margin risk.
3. **GLP-1 / health-shift narrative.** The blanket "people will eat less" trade hit all packaged food. Debatable for McCormick specifically — spices are near-zero-calorie and home cooking *helps* seasoning demand — but the sector de-rating dragged MKC with it.
4. **Bond-proxy de-rating.** Low-beta staples with 3–4% yields compress when long rates are elevated; a chunk of the −34% is multiple compression, not fundamental deterioration.
5. **Leverage.** ~$4.7B net debt at ~3.5x EBITDA with only $180M cash. Manageable and declining, but it limits buyback firepower and makes refinancing costlier at higher rates.
6. **Flavor Solutions cyclicality.** The B2B segment softens when restaurant traffic and CPG volumes slow — a near-term drag with no quick fix.

## Bull case

1. **Cheapest in ~15 years for a wide-moat compounder.** ~15–16x forward vs a 5-yr-average ~24x. Even a partial re-rate to 19x on ~$3.30 EPS (FY28) is ~$63 (+30%) before dividends. The market is treating a quality staple like a structurally challenged one.
2. **Margins are already inflecting up.** Operating income +19% over two years while revenue grew only ~3% — pricing held, input costs eased, and the Global Operating Effectiveness cost program is delivering. This is the opposite of a margin-collapse story.
3. **~4% yield, Dividend Aristocrat, ~65% payout.** ~39 straight years of increases; the dividend is well-covered by normalized EPS and FCF. You're paid to wait, and the raise streak gives the dividend strong institutional defense (unlike a flat-payer staple).
4. **Genuine pricing power + category resilience.** Spices are low-ticket, habitual, and recession-resilient; home-cooking trends and the "flavor exploration" secular tailwind support volume over time. Frank's RedHot, Cholula, and hot-sauce/condiment exposure are on-trend.
5. **Low beta (0.64) with optionality.** Defensive ballast that *also* has ~+26% to the analyst mean — a rare combination of downside protection and upside in the current tape.

## Entry, thesis-break, what would change my mind

- **Entry (starter):** the $45–49 zone (current price down to the 52-week low) is a reasonable accumulation range for a quality staple at a 15-year-low multiple; ideal adds **below ~$46**, where the yield pushes past 4.2% and you're near 5-year support.
- **Thesis-break / reassess:** a **dividend freeze or cut** (would break the Aristocrat thesis and signal balance-sheet stress), OR gross margin falling **sustainably below ~35%** (tariffs winning, no pricing offset), OR **2+ consecutive quarters of organic volume decline >3%** (private label structurally taking share). A break below the 5-year low (~$44) on any of those = the franchise is de-rating for real reasons, not sentiment.
- **What would change my mind to a high-conviction BUY:** evidence of **re-accelerating organic volume** (consumers not abandoning the brands) *plus* **continued operating-margin expansion** *plus* net-debt/EBITDA back below ~3.0x. That combination de-risks the dividend and unlocks the re-rating path toward the high-$50s/$60s — at which point ~15x for this moat is a clear bargain.

## Verdict

**WATCH, leaning BUY LOW as a quality-at-a-discount starter near $45–48.** McCormick is a wide-moat, pricing-power staple — French's, Frank's, Cholula, Old Bay, a dominant spice aisle — trading at its cheapest forward multiple in roughly 15 years, with a covered ~4% Aristocrat yield and a 0.64 beta that makes it portfolio ballast. Unlike most beaten-down food names (e.g. CPB's levered, shrinking soup business), MKC is *growing slowly with expanding margins*, so this is a valuation reset, not a broken franchise. The honest knock: it's a low-growth name, so the ~+30% (GBM-tempered) to +40% (fundamental) 3-year EV leans on multiple re-rating that the trusted models only partly credit, and tariffs are a live margin risk. Size it as a defensive starter, buy near the lows, hold a hard line on any dividend freeze or margin break below 35%. A better risk/reward than most income-staple "value traps" — but not a screaming, act-tonight bargain.
