# Linde plc (LIN)

## Business Primer

**What they do today.** Linde is the world's largest industrial gas company. The product set is mundane on its face — oxygen, nitrogen, argon, hydrogen, helium, carbon dioxide, and specialty gases — but the economics are extraordinary because customers cannot run their factories without these molecules and they cost almost nothing to ship as a percentage of the customer's bill of materials. Revenue (~$34B in 2025) comes from three modes of supply that ratchet down in customer stickiness: (1) on-site / pipeline — Linde builds a dedicated air-separation unit or hydrogen plant next to a steelmaker, refinery, or semiconductor fab and signs a 15–20 year take-or-pay contract with built-in price escalators; (2) merchant — bulk liquid gases delivered by tanker truck to mid-size industrial users; (3) packaged — high-pressure cylinders sold to thousands of small customers (welders, hospitals, food processors, labs). Roughly 60% of sales come from "process" industries (chemicals, refining, metals, electronics, healthcare, food and beverage), with the rest in manufacturing and general industrial. A fourth, smaller segment — Engineering — designs and builds the plants themselves (air separation units, hydrogen plants, liquefied natural gas (LNG) trains), often for third parties as well as for Linde's own gas business.

**Where they want to grow.** Management's stated strategy is to bolt three structural growth vectors on top of a GDP-plus base business. (1) Clean hydrogen — Linde holds a record ~$10B project backlog as of Q1 2026, roughly two-thirds of which is clean-energy-linked, including the world's largest blue hydrogen project on the US Gulf Coast for OCI, hydrogen supply to the NEOM project in Saudi Arabia, and dozens of smaller decarbonization deals; management plans to start up $2.5–$3B of backlog in 2026 alone, converting secured capital into long-duration take-or-pay cash flow. (2) Electronics gases — supplying nitrogen, neon, helium, and ultra-high-purity specialty gases to semiconductor fabs; this segment grew high-single-digits in Q1 2026 driven by new US and Asia fab startups (TSMC Arizona, Samsung Taylor, Micron, Intel Ohio). (3) Decarbonization carbon capture — providing carbon dioxide compression, purification, and sequestration services as oil and gas, cement, and steel customers retrofit existing assets. Linde also supplies liquid oxygen (LOX) to SpaceX's Starbase launch site for Starship, a small but symbolic vertical. Pricing power, productivity, and disciplined capital allocation (only signing projects with mid-teens or better returns on capital) round out the strategy; success in five years looks like ~10%+ annual earnings per share growth with mid-30s operating margins.

**What could go wrong (business risks).** First, macro industrial cyclicality — a global manufacturing recession in the merchant and packaged segments could pressure volumes (the on-site book is largely take-or-pay so it cushions, but does not eliminate, downside). Second, green-hydrogen project execution and electrolyzer economics — many announced clean-H2 projects depend on government subsidies (Inflation Reduction Act 45V tax credit in the US, EU Hydrogen Bank auctions) and on customers being able to pay the green premium; subsidy rollback, cost overruns, or stranded electrolyzer capacity could turn growth bets into write-downs. Third, energy price volatility — Linde's hydrogen economics depend on natural gas (blue H2) or electricity (green H2) prices; a structural increase in either compresses spreads on the captive portion not passed through to customers. Fourth, tariffs and engineering-segment friction — while the gas business is local-for-local with minimal direct tariff exposure (gases cost too little per unit volume to ship across borders), the Engineering scope often involves cross-border equipment shipments where new duties can delay final investment decisions on the very mega-projects that anchor the backlog. Fifth, customer concentration in specific verticals — semiconductor capex cyclicality and a handful of mega-refinery and petrochemical customers can move quarterly results.

**How to think about it.** Linde is the canonical industrial-gas oligopoly compounder — the global industry is essentially three players (Linde, Air Liquide, Air Products) with regional duopolies and near-utility-like cash flows from long-dated take-or-pay contracts. Own it as a base-rate compounder that earns mid-teens returns on capital employed, raises prices ahead of inflation, and now has three identifiable secular growth pillars (clean hydrogen, electronics, decarbonization) sitting on top of a GDP-plus chassis. The bet is not "this is mispriced today" — the multiple is full — but "earnings compound at 9–11% annually with very low variance, and the hydrogen/electronics/decarbonization backlog adds incremental growth that consensus may still be underestimating in 2028–2030."

## Variant Perception
- **Consensus view:** High-quality industrial-gas compounder fairly valued at ~26x forward EPS; analysts average $541 target (~6% upside) with "buy" recommendation; positioned as a defensive name with hydrogen optionality. The stock is widely owned (89% institutional) and a consensus quality long.
- **Our view:** Largely aligned with consensus on the business — Linde is a phenomenal compounder. The variant, if any, is on timing and multiple sustainability: the GBM ensemble flags 1–3 year price trajectories of +5% to +29%, but DCF and Residual Income Model (RIM) scream "overvalued by 80%+" because their terminal-value math punishes high multiples on long-duration cash flows. Reality is in the middle — durable compounder, no obvious mispricing today, valuation discipline matters before adding.
- **Trigger:** Capital Markets Day or 2027 guidance update articulating the post-2026 backlog conversion ramp could re-rate the stock if hydrogen project Internal Rates of Return (IRRs) are confirmed at mid-teens. Conversely, any delay or write-down on a flagship clean-H2 project (NEOM, OCI Gulf Coast) would compress the multiple.

## Inflection Point
No classic inflection — this is a steady-state compounder. The closest thing to an inflection is the **2026–2028 backlog conversion ramp** ($2.5–3B of project startups in 2026 alone, converting secured capex into long-duration take-or-pay cash flow). Whether this drives a multiple re-rate or simply meets consensus is unknowable in advance.

## Bull Case
- Backlog conversion: $2.5–3B of project startups in 2026, more in 2027–28, each adding 15+ year take-or-pay revenue at mid-teens IRR.
- Clean hydrogen scale: world's largest blue hydrogen project (OCI Gulf Coast), NEOM, multiple decarbonization wins — Linde is the clear hyperscaler-of-choice for industrial decarb.
- Electronics fab cycle: TSMC Arizona, Samsung Taylor, Intel Ohio, Micron — multi-year ramp of new fabs all need Linde gas supply.
- Pricing power: mid-single-digit pricing every year regardless of cycle, because gases are <1% of customer cost of goods sold but mission-critical.
- Defensive composition with cyclical kicker — beta 0.74, but with semis/H2 optionality.

## Bear Case
- Valuation: 34x trailing / 26x forward PE, 6.2x book — multiple compression in a higher-for-longer rate regime would hurt.
- **Hydrogen subsidy/execution risk (under-covered in news):** Q1 commentary referenced Autothermal Reformer (ATR) project execution and CO2 sequestration scope — a flagship project delay, cost overrun, or 45V tax-credit rollback could turn a backlog tailwind into a write-down. Headline coverage emphasizes the size of the backlog and underplays the execution complexity that management itself flagged.
- Capex stepping up ($5.0–$5.5B vs. $3.2B in 2022) compresses near-term FCF growth even as earnings grow.
- Industrial recession risk: merchant + packaged volumes (~40% of revenue) are cycle-sensitive; a 2026–27 manufacturing downturn would mute the algorithm.
- Tariff friction on the Engineering segment cross-border equipment shipments — could slow project Final Investment Decisions (FIDs) in the very backlog that drives the bull case.

## Verdict

**WATCH** — Conviction: MEDIUM

Linde is a world-class compounder with a fortress moat, three identifiable secular growth pillars, and best-in-class margins — but at $510 it trades near 52-week highs at a full multiple, leaving a base-case expected value of only ~3% over the next year. The GBM ensemble suggests modest single-digit to low-double-digit upside on 1–3 year horizons; this is a "hold forever" quality, not a "back up the truck today" setup.

**Would upgrade to BUY at:** $440 or below (a ~14% pullback bringing forward PE to ~22x and expected return into the mid-teens). Alternatively, upgrade if the 2027 backlog conversion outlook is materially raised at a future Capital Markets Day with confirmed mid-teens IRRs on clean-hydrogen mega-projects.
