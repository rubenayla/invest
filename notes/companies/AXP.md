# American Express Company (AXP)

**Sector:** Financial Services | **Industry:** Credit Services
**Price:** $305.27 (2026-05-20) | **Market Cap:** ~$208B

## Business Primer

**What they do today.** American Express runs a credit-and-charge-card business, but it is unusual because it owns the entire chain end-to-end. Unlike Visa and Mastercard, which only operate the network rails and let banks issue the cards, Amex issues its own cards, lends its own money, AND runs its own payment network. This "closed loop" means Amex sees both sides of every transaction — the customer who spends and the merchant who gets paid. Revenue comes from three buckets. The biggest is **discount revenue** — the fee (roughly 2-3%) Amex charges merchants every time a customer swipes an Amex card; this is the single largest line and scales directly with how much its cardholders spend. Second is **card fees** — the annual fees customers pay to hold premium cards (the Platinum card alone runs ~$895/year), a fast-growing and very sticky revenue stream. Third is **net interest income** — interest earned on the revolving balances of customers who carry a balance, which functions like a bank lending book. In Q1 2026 the company did $428B of billed business (total customer spending) in a single quarter and generated $18.9B of revenue.

**Why customers choose them (the value proposition).** A premium-spending customer picks an Amex Platinum over a plain 2%-cashback card because Amex bundles the card with a package of high-end perks that the alternative doesn't offer: access to Centurion airport lounges (Amex builds and owns these physical lounges, now expanding to Las Vegas and New Delhi), automatic hotel-elite status, hundreds of dollars in annual statement credits at specific partners (airlines, Uber, retailers), purchase and travel insurance, and concierge service. The mechanism that makes it work: a frequent traveler who would otherwise pay $50 per lounge visit, lose money on a delayed-trip, and get no hotel upgrades instead pays one annual fee and gets all of it bundled, plus rewards points on every dollar. For the merchant side, accepting Amex costs more than Visa/Mastercard, but merchants accept it because Amex cardholders spend far more per transaction than the average shopper — refusing Amex means turning away the highest-value customers. The closed-loop data also lets Amex target offers precisely. Net result: customers who spend a lot get a service experience a generic card can't match, and Amex captures a richer fee on that spending.

**Where they want to grow.** Management's central bet is on younger affluent customers: Millennials and Gen Z were 64% of new card signups after the 2025 U.S. Platinum refresh and are the fastest-growing cohort in both new accounts and spending. The strategy is to keep refreshing premium products (raising fees while adding perks, which lifts both fee revenue and spend), expanding into commercial/business cards (Q1 2026 launched the Graphite Business Cash Unlimited Card as the start of "the largest one-year expansion of our commercial product suite in our history"), deepening engagement through sports and dining partnerships (became the NFL's Official Payments Partner, extended the NBA deal, integrating Resy and Tock restaurant networks), and pushing into AI/agentic commerce (Amex Agentic Commerce Experiences developer kit, Agent Purchase Protection). Success in 3-5 years looks like sustained 9-10% revenue growth with mid-teens EPS growth, an even younger and larger premium base, and a bigger commercial franchise.

**What could go wrong (business risks).** Amex carries real credit risk because it lends its own money — in a sharp recession, write-offs and loan-loss provisions spike and earnings fall hard (this is the key difference from Visa/Mastercard, which take no credit risk). Its customer base skews affluent, which historically holds up better in downturns, but a deep recession still bites. Competition is intensifying: Chase relaunched the Sapphire Reserve to directly attack Amex's premium-card franchise, and there's an industry worry that the spiral of higher fees plus more perks ("coupon book" cards) eventually hits a ceiling where customers balk. Merchant acceptance and interchange/discount-rate pressure are a structural risk if regulators or large merchants push back on Amex's higher fees. And Amex is concentrated in U.S. consumer and travel-and-entertainment spending, so a travel slowdown (airline bookings already softened in late March/April 2026 from Middle East disruptions) hits a meaningful slice directly.

**How to think about it.** Amex is a hybrid: part payment network (Visa/Mastercard-like, fee-on-spending), part premium lender (bank-like, credit risk), wrapped in a luxury-membership brand. It is best understood as a "premium-spender flywheel" — affluent customers spend more, which earns higher merchant fees and supports rich perks, which attracts more affluent customers — distinct from the pure-toll-road networks (no credit risk, higher multiple) and from plain card lenders like Synchrony or Capital One (more credit risk, lower multiple, lower-quality customer). It is a core Berkshire Hathaway holding for exactly this reason.

## Situation Summary
Amex reported a strong Q1 2026 on 2026-04-23: revenue $18.9B (+11%, +10% FX-adjusted), EPS $4.28 (+18%), net income $3.0B, and billed business +10% — the highest quarterly spend growth in three years. Credit performance was excellent (net write-off rate 2.0%, down from 2.1% a year ago) and management reaffirmed full-year 2026 guidance of 9-10% revenue growth and EPS of $17.30-$17.90. Despite the clean beat, the stock dipped on the print and now trades at ~$305, near the lower end of its 52-week range ($281-$387), as the market frets about a maturing premium-card cycle, intensifying competition from Chase's relaunched Sapphire Reserve, a late-Q1 travel-spend softening, and management's decision to step up marketing and technology investment (which caps near-term margin expansion). The narrative tension: best-in-class fundamentals and a Berkshire seal of approval versus a fear that the spending and fee-growth cycle is peaking.

## Variant Perception
- **Consensus view:** Quality compounder, but the easy gains are behind it — premium-card fee hikes and spend growth are near a ceiling, Chase's Sapphire Reserve relaunch will pressure share, and a consumer/travel slowdown plus rising marketing spend will compress the growth rate. Analysts are mildly positive (mean target ~$360, 23 analysts, "buy") but the stock has de-rated from $387 to $305, implying skepticism that double-digit growth is durable.
- **Our view:** The market is treating a deliberate reinvestment cycle (more marketing/tech to extend the runway) as a sign of peaking, when the underlying engine — Millennial/Gen Z taking 64% of new signups, spend growth re-accelerating to a 3-year high, and the Platinum refresh lifting spend ~6 points mostly from *existing* members at higher retention even after a fee increase — looks like an earnings runway being extended, not exhausted. Credit is pristine (write-offs *down* YoY) at a time when peers are deteriorating. At a ~15x forward P/E for a business compounding EPS in the mid-to-high teens with a 34% ROE, the de-rating to near the 52-week low offers an entry where the multiple already discounts a slowdown that the Q1 data doesn't show.
- **Trigger:** Q2 2026 earnings (~late July 2026) — if billed-business growth holds near double digits and credit stays benign while EPS tracks the high end of the $17.30-$17.90 guide, the "peak cycle" thesis breaks and the multiple re-rates back toward the high-$300s.

## Inflection Point
Not a turnaround — Amex is a steady compounder, not a broken business inflecting up. The relevant "inflection" is sentiment/multiple: the stock has de-rated ~21% from its 52-week high to near its low on peak-cycle fears, while the operating data (spend growth at a 3-year high, write-offs down YoY, reaffirmed guidance) shows no fundamental deterioration. The observable evidence of a *positive* re-rating inflection would be Q2 confirming spend and credit trends hold — that has not yet printed, so this is currently a "buy the de-rating of a quality compounder" setup rather than a confirmed turn.

## Bull Case
- Spend growth re-accelerated to a 3-year high (+10% billed business) and Millennial/Gen Z are 64% of new signups — the premium base is getting younger and bigger, extending the runway the market thinks is closing.
- Platinum refresh lifted spend ~6 points, mostly from *existing* members, with high retention even after the fee hike — proof of pricing power and that fee increases aren't near a ceiling yet.
- Credit is pristine and *improving* (net write-off 2.0% vs 2.1% YoY, modest reserve release) while peers deteriorate — Amex's affluent mix is a structural advantage that the market under-credits.
- ~15x forward P/E for a 34%-ROE business compounding EPS mid-teens, near a 52-week low, with EPS guidance $17.30-$17.90 implying the stock trades ~17-18x current-year — cheap for the quality.
- Optionality from commercial-card expansion (largest-ever product suite expansion) and AI/agentic commerce that isn't in numbers yet.

## Bear Case
- **Under-covered disclosed risk (from the press release/call):** management explicitly *raised* marketing and technology investment and now guides marketing to grow mid-single-digits, with consolidated expenses already up 11% YoY (driven partly by Platinum-refresh benefit usage). Headlines focused on the competitive threat and travel softness, but the company's own disclosed near-term drag is the *deliberate expense ramp* that caps margin expansion — earnings growth in 2026 leans more on revenue than on operating leverage, and if revenue slows, the elevated cost base bites.
- Chase's relaunched Sapphire Reserve directly attacks the premium franchise; an industry "fee fatigue / coupon-book" backlash could slow new-account growth and pressure retention at renewal.
- Travel-and-entertainment spend (a high-margin category) softened in late March/April 2026 on Middle East disruptions — if that broadens into a consumer slowdown, billed-business growth falls below guidance.
- Amex bears credit risk: a recession spikes provisions and write-offs from today's benign 2.0%, and the loan book is at a cyclical-low loss rate that can only normalize upward.
- Already fairly valued on the most-reliable models (GBM 3y range from -20% to +35%); not a deep-value bargain — upside depends on continued execution, not mean-reversion.
