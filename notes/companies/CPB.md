# The Campbell's Company (CPB) — First Look

**Sector:** Consumer Defensive | **Industry:** Packaged Foods (Meals & Beverages + Snacks)
**Price:** $22.13 (2026-06-10) | **Market Cap:** ~$6.6B (~298M shares) | **EV:** ~$13.2B
**Analysis Date:** 2026-06-10
**Call:** WATCH (lean BUY LOW — income-while-you-wait starter only, conviction LOW)
**Conviction:** LOW (C+) — optically cheap (10–11x P/E, ~7% yield near a 6-year low), but it is a *levered, organically shrinking* packaged-food name and the sell-side mean target ($21.44) sits **below** the current price. The dividend is the whole bull case; the de-leveraging math is the whole bear case.

Campbell's at **$22** is **−55% from its 2022 peak (~$50)** and sitting just above its 52-week low (**$19.56**, range $19.56–$34.44). The renamed "Campbell's Company" (dropped "Soup" in 2024 to signal it's now half-snacks) yields **~7.0%** on a $1.56 dividend — roughly **2× its historic ~3.5% yield**. That is either a classic mean-reversion entry in a defensive staple, or the market correctly pricing a melting ice cube carrying **~4.2× net leverage** from the 2024 **$2.7B Sovos Brands (Rao's)** acquisition. This note argues it's closer to a fairly-priced, balanced bet than an actionable bargain: a modest **~+9% expected value** that is **almost entirely the dividend**, not re-rating.

## The setup: two businesses, one balance sheet

Campbell's fiscal year ends late July; FY2026 is ~halfway done (Q2 reported ~2026-03, ending 2026-01-31).

- **Meals & Beverages (~55% of sales):** soup (Campbell's, Chunky, Pacific), broth, Prego, V8, and the crown jewel **Rao's** (premium pasta sauce, acquired via Sovos 2024). Soup is a slow-secular-decline, cash-generative anchor; Rao's is a genuine double-digit grower and the strategic reason for the $2.7B deal + the debt.
- **Snacks (~45% of sales):** Goldfish, Pepperidge Farm, Snyder's of Hanover, Lance, Kettle, Cape Cod. This division has been the **disappointment** — volume/mix weak, partner-brand exits, margin pressure. Management has flagged snacking softness repeatedly; it was supposed to be the growth engine and instead is the drag.
- **Organic decline is real (Iron Rule check):** reported revenue rose FY23→FY25 ($9.36B→$10.25B) but that is the Sovos acquisition. On a like-for-like basis volumes are falling — Q2 FY26 net sales −4.5% YoY as the acquisition laps and core volumes soften. This is *not* a growth story; the bull case requires *stabilization*, not growth.

## Bull case

1. **Optically cheap on every multiple** — ~10–11x earnings and ~8.4x EV/EBITDA vs a historic ~15x P/E and ~11–12x EV/EBITDA. A re-rating to even 13x on ~$2.10 EPS is ~$27 (+22%).
2. **~7% dividend, covered by FCF** — $459M paid vs $705M free cash flow (~65% of FCF), so it is *currently* safe and pays you to wait. ~2× the historic yield is a mean-reversion signal in defensive staples.
3. **Rao's is a real asset** — premium, on-trend, double-digit growth; the one part of the portfolio that justifies a higher multiple and partially offsets soup's secular decline.
4. **Defensive demand** — soup/broth/sauce are recession-resilient, private-label-competitive but sticky; volumes don't fall off a cliff in downturns.
5. **Self-help margin levers** — Sovos synergy capture ($50M+ targeted) and cost programs can lift operating margin back toward mid-teens if input costs stay tame.

## Bear case (lead with this — try to kill it)

1. **~4.2× net leverage on a shrinking top line.** $6.6B net debt against ~$1.58B EBITDA. If EBITDA slips even 10% on continued volume declines, leverage pushes toward ~4.6× and de-leveraging stalls — every dollar of FCF then fights between the dividend, capex, and debt paydown. This is the single thing that turns a 7% yield into a value trap.
2. **Sell-side mean target ($21.44) is *below* the $22 price.** 18 analysts, consensus "Hold." The market is not mispricing a hidden gem; the people who model this closely think it's roughly fairly valued *or slightly rich*. That is the opposite of the setup you want in a "beaten-down bargain."
3. **Structural headwinds on processed/center-store food:** GLP-1 appetite suppression, private-label trade-down, and health-conscious shift all hit soup + salty snacks directly. These are secular, not cyclical.
4. **Snacks turnaround keeps slipping.** The division bought to be the growth engine is underperforming; management credibility on snacking guidance is low.
5. **No dividend growth.** Held flat — it is *not* an Aristocrat/King with a 50-year raise streak to defend. A flat (or cut) dividend under leverage stress has far less institutional support than Hormel's or a Dividend King's.
6. **Thin FCF cushion + heavy capex** (~$426M) means little room for buybacks (only $62M) or accelerated de-levering. Total-return upside beyond the dividend is therefore slow.

## Entry, thesis-break, what would change my mind

- **Entry (starter only):** ~$20 or below (the 52-week-low zone), where the yield pushes toward ~7.8% and you're buying near tangible support. At $22 there is no margin of safety vs the $21.44 analyst mean.
- **Thesis-break / sell:** a **dividend cut**, OR net-debt/EBITDA rising **above ~4.5×**, OR a second consecutive quarter of accelerating (>5%) organic volume decline. Any of those means the de-levering math has broken and the 7% yield was a trap.
- **What would change my mind to a real BUY:** evidence the snacks division has inflected (2+ quarters of flat-to-positive organic volume), *plus* visible net-debt reduction below ~3.8×. That combination would de-risk the dividend and open the re-rating path — at which point the 7% yield + cheap multiple becomes genuinely attractive.

## Verdict

**WATCH, leaning BUY LOW as an income-only starter near $20.** Campbell's is the textbook beaten-down staple: cheap multiple, fat covered yield, near a multi-year low. But it is also levered, organically shrinking, and the people who model it closely peg fair value *below* today's price. The ~+9% expected value is real but unexciting and is **all dividend carry** — there are higher-conviction, higher-EV beaten-down names on the watchlist (e.g. ADBE +46%). Own it for yield-while-you-wait if at all, sized small, bought near $20, with a hard stop on any dividend cut or leverage creep above ~4.5×. Not an "act now" bargain at $22.
