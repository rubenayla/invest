## What happened to the price

$90 (2015 peak, post-merger) → $34 (5y ago) → $31 (3y) → $30 (2y) → $24 today. A slow, relentless grind — ~73% below the 2015 high. This is not a sharp dislocation you can fade; it's a decade-long re-rating of a business the market no longer believes can grow.

## The bear case (lead with this — it's the base case)

1. **Secular volume decline.** FY2025 organic sales fell ~3.4%; revenue $26.6B (2023) → $24.9B (2025). Center-of-store packaged food is in structural retreat: GLP-1 appetite suppression, the "processed food" / MAHA political backlash (Oscar Mayer processed meat, Lunchables nutrition controversy, Kraft Singles), and private-label trade-down by a stretched low-end consumer. KHC's portfolio sits squarely in the crosshairs.
2. **The catalyst is gone.** The Sept-2025 plan to split into *Global Taste Elevation Co.* (Heinz, Philadelphia, Mac & Cheese — the good half) and *North American Grocery Co.* (Oscar Mayer, Lunchables, Capri Sun — the melting half) was **paused on 2026-02-11** after sales kept falling. Management instead committed ~$600M to a turnaround. The pause is itself a negative signal: the board lost confidence that a SOTP separation would unlock value in this tape, and removed the one clean near-term re-rate.
3. **Reinvestment eats the cushion.** The $600M turnaround spend pressures near-term margins/FCF just as the dividend (6.6% yield, 73% earnings payout) needs that FCF. Dividend is safe on FY25 numbers (1.9x FCF cover) but the cover thins if organic decline persists and reinvestment runs multi-year.
4. **Berkshire overhang.** Berkshire (~27%) has written its stake to market and is widely expected to be a future seller; that's a structural supply ceiling on any rally. 3G already exited.
5. **FY25 $5.85B net loss** = ~$9B goodwill/intangible impairment on the grocery brands — an admission that prior carrying values were fiction.

## The bull case (what would have to go right)

1. **Valuation is genuinely cheap on cash.** 7.9x EV/EBITDA, ~12% FCF yield, 0.68x book, 11.5x forward earnings — priced for terminal decline. FCF has been remarkably stable (~$3B+) despite falling sales; the brands still throw off cash.
2. **Dividend pays you to wait.** 6.6% yield, FCF-covered ~1.9x. If the business merely stops getting worse, the yield alone is a decent return.
3. **Sauces/spreads half is a real franchise.** Heinz ketchup, Philadelphia, and the global condiment business grow and travel well; a future (resumed) separation could still unlock a premium multiple on that segment.
4. **Mean reversion in staples.** If rates fall and the market rotates to defensives, deeply-hated staples with fat yields can re-rate fast off a low base.

## Entry / exit discipline

- **Entry of interest:** below ~$21 (the 52-wk low / ~14% FCF yield) for an income-led starter only. At $24 it's fair-to-cheap but not a fat pitch.
- **Thesis-break (kill it):** organic sales decline *accelerates* below −5%, OR dividend cut announced, OR Berkshire begins visibly selling — any of these turns the value-trap into a value-destroyer; the yield support evaporates with the dividend.
- **Thesis-confirm (add):** two consecutive quarters of flat-to-positive organic volume, or a credible relaunch of the separation — that's when the optionality becomes real.

## What would change my mind

A return to *volume* growth (not just price-led revenue) would flip this from value-trap to value. So would a Berkshire stake resolution that removes the overhang (e.g. a buyback absorbing it). Absent those, this is dead money with a coupon.
