## Business Primer

**What they do.** Baxter makes the unglamorous, essential plumbing of hospitals. Three continuing segments after the 2025 reshaping:
1. **Medical Products & Therapies (MPT)** â€” IV solutions and the bags/sets that deliver them, infusion pumps (Novum IQ / Spectrum), nutrition, and surgical/anesthesia (advanced surgery). This is the crown jewel: Baxter is the dominant US supplier of sterile IV fluids, a quasi-utility with high switching costs and regulatory barriers.
2. **Healthcare Systems & Technologies (HST)** â€” the former **Hillrom** business: hospital beds, patient-monitoring/connected-care, and the "Care & Connectivity Solutions" + "Front Line Care" lines. Capital-equipment-heavy and hospital-capex-sensitive.
3. **Pharmaceuticals** â€” injectable generics, drug-compounding, inhaled anesthesia.

**Why there's a moat (in parts).** The IV-fluids/infusion franchise is genuinely defensible: sterile-manufacturing scale, FDA-validated plants you cannot stand up quickly, hospital contracts, and demand that doesn't flex with the economy. When Hurricane Helene flooded Baxter's **North Cove, NC** plant (the largest US IV-fluid factory) in Sept 2024, it triggered nationwide hospital saline shortages â€” the clearest possible proof of how essential and concentrated this supply is.

**Why it's beaten down (the de-rating).** A multi-year self-inflicted unwind plus external shocks:
1. **The Hillrom millstone.** Baxter bought Hillrom for ~$12.4B in 2021 at the top, funded with debt. The deal never earned its cost of capital; subsequent goodwill **impairments** are why trailing earnings and ROE are negative, and why the balance sheet is stretched at ~4x.
2. **The break-up + dividend reset.** Baxter sold its **Kidney Care unit (Vantive)** to Carlyle/Atmas for ~$3.8B net (closed Jan 2025) to pay down debt.
